# Arizer Air 2

Overview

The Arizer Air 2 (also referred to as the Air II) is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. Released in 2017 as the successor to the original Arizer Air, the Air 2 features a hybrid conduction/convection heating system with a ceramic heating element and glass vapor path. It is powered by a single user-replaceable 18650 lithium-ion battery and is classified as a session-style vaporizer.

The Air 2 is part of Arizer's second-generation portable lineup, which also includes the Solo 2 and ArGo. The device retains the core design philosophy of its predecessor — a glass aroma tube (stem) inserted into a stainless steel oven — while adding digital temperature control via an OLED display, replacing the original Air's preset temperature LED system.

Community Reception

The community generally regarded the Arizer Air 2 as a solid, reliable session portable that excelled in vapor smoothness, flavor, efficiency, and ease of cleaning. It was frequently recommended to newcomers and users seeking a smooth, low-irritation experience — particularly those with respiratory sensitivities such as asthma or allergies. One community member noted having "two friends with asthma and they get along well with Arizers," and the glass vapor path was widely cited as a key contributor to vapor purity and taste.

Pros identified by the community:

  • Smooth, clean vapor — The all-glass vapor path was considered a major advantage, producing vapor that was notably smooth and flavorful compared to vaporizers with plastic or metal air paths.
  • Efficient extraction — Users found the Air 2 to be very efficient with herb, making it well-suited for solo sessions.
  • Easy to load and clean — The glass stem system was praised for its simplicity: pack the stem, insert it, vape, and clean with isopropyl alcohol.
  • Replaceable 18650 battery — The ability to carry spare batteries was seen as a significant advantage over competitors with sealed batteries.
  • Digital temperature control — The ability to dial in a precise temperature (as opposed to the original Air's preset steps) was appreciated, especially for low-temperature flavor chasers.
  • Build quality and durability — Multiple community members reported years of reliable daily use from Arizer products. One member described using the original Air as a "daily driver for a good three years" with minimal wear.

Cons identified by the community:

  • Session-style extraction — The Air 2 was characterized as a "sipper," not a "ripper." Extraction was slower than hybrid/conduction competitors like the Storz & Bickel Crafty or Mighty, which some users found frustrating when sharing with groups or seeking quick sessions. One member who owned both an Air 2 and a Crafty noted the Crafty offered "quicker extraction" and chose to sell the Arizer.
  • Not ideal for group use — The small chamber and slow extraction pace made the Air 2 less suitable for passing around in groups.
  • Portability limitations — While more portable than the Solo 2, the protruding glass stem made the Air 2 less pocket-friendly than some competitors. One community member noted it was "not terribly portable without a case."
  • Longer actual heat-up time — Although the display may show the set temperature reached quickly, the community widely noted that the oven temperature indicator does not reflect the temperature of the herb in the glass stem. Actual time to first visible vapor was longer than the display suggested. One member performing a side-by-side comparison with the original Air found the Air 2 took "a lot longer to warm up" in terms of producing vapor, speculating that increased airflow in the redesign may account for this.
  • Display reliability concerns — At least one community member reported OLED screen failures on two separate Air 2 units, leading them to avoid the product in the future.
  • Customer service friction — Some community members described Arizer's customer support as difficult to deal with when issues arose.

Overall, community sentiment placed the Air 2 as a dependable, flavor-forward portable that served best as a personal daily driver for home or semi-portable use, rather than an on-the-go or group session device.

Tips & Techniques

Loading

  • Pack loosely. The Air 2's hybrid heating benefits from airflow through the herb. A loose to medium pack in the glass stem is generally recommended over a tight pack. Overpacking restricts airflow and can reduce vapor quality.
  • Use the stem as a scoop. A widely used technique across the Arizer portable line is to press the open end of the glass stem directly into a container or grinder of ground herb, then gently tamp it level. This produces a consistent, easy load.

Drawing Technique

  • Slow, steady draws. The Air 2 rewards patient, slow sipping rather than hard, fast pulls. Long, gentle draws allow the heater to maintain temperature and produce smoother, more flavorful vapor.
  • Allow extra warm-up time beyond the display. The temperature displayed on the OLED reflects the oven sensor, not the actual herb temperature in the glass stem. Community members recommended waiting an additional 30–60 seconds after the display reaches the set temperature before taking the first draw, to allow heat to fully transfer into the herb.

Session Management

  • Step up temperature. A common community technique is to start at a lower temperature for the first few draws (to savor flavor and terpenes), then gradually increase the temperature in 5–10°C increments through the session to extract remaining active compounds. This maximizes both flavor and efficiency from a single load.

For Users with Respiratory Sensitivities

  • The community specifically recommended the Air 2 for users with asthma or allergies, citing the glass vapor path and the ability to start at very low temperatures as key factors. Pairing the Air 2 with a water pipe adapter (WPA) for additional cooling and filtration was also suggested.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Stems

  • Soak in isopropyl alcohol (ISO). The glass stems are the primary component requiring regular cleaning. Soaking in 90%+ isopropyl alcohol for 15–30 minutes dissolves resin buildup. Rinse thoroughly with warm water afterward.
  • Stem honey reclamation. Over time, the interior of the glass stem accumulates a golden-brown oil residue (sometimes called "stem honey" or "stem milk"). This is a concentrated reclaim and can be collected by soaking the stem in warm whole milk or a fatty liquid, or by carefully scraping and saving the residue.
  • Cleaning frequency. Stems should be cleaned regularly — roughly every few sessions for optimal flavor, or whenever draw resistance increases.

Oven

  • The stainless steel oven can be gently brushed out with the included stirring tool or a small brush while still slightly warm (not hot). Occasionally, a cotton swab dampened with isopropyl alcohol can be used to clean the oven interior. Avoid excess liquid entering the device.

Screens

  • The stainless steel filter screens in the stems should be replaced periodically when clogged or discolored. They can be soaked in ISO for temporary restoration.

General

  • The exterior can be wiped down with a lightly damp cloth. Avoid submerging the unit.

Common Issues & Fixes

Display/Screen Failure

One community member reported the OLED screen ceasing to function on two separate Air 2 units. This appears to be an uncommon but documented hardware issue. If within the warranty period, contacting Arizer for a replacement is the recommended course of action, though some community members found Arizer's customer service challenging to work with.

Longer-Than-Expected Heat-Up Time

As noted above, the displayed temperature reflects the oven sensor, not the actual temperature of the herb in the glass stem. This is not a defect but a design characteristic shared across the Arizer portable line. The community solution is simply to allow additional warm-up time (30–60 seconds past the displayed set point) before drawing.

One member comparing the Air 2 directly to the original Air under identical conditions (same stem, same material) found the Air 2 took noticeably longer to produce vapor despite the display reaching temperature faster. The member speculated that "higher air flow accounts for the longer (actual) heat-up time," suggesting the Air 2's improved air path design may draw more ambient air across the herb, requiring more time for equilibrium.

Toggle Switch Wear (Original Air)

This was noted as a durability concern on the original Air but was addressed in the Air 2's redesigned button interface with the OLED display. Users upgrading from the original Air for this reason generally found the Air 2's controls more durable.

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems and Compatibility

  • Air / Solo stem cross-compatibility: The Air 2 uses the same diameter glass stems as the original Air, Solo, and Solo 2. All stems across these models are interchangeable.
  • ArGo stems are NOT compatible. The ArGo uses a slightly narrower and shorter glass stem with a different diameter. Community members confirmed that ArGo stems, including ArGo-specific water pipe adapters, will not fit the Air 2.
  • High-flow stems: Aftermarket and official stems with wider internal bore are available, offering reduced draw resistance. One for-sale listing mentioned including "a additional high flow stem" as a notable accessory.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

Glass WPAs are available that allow the Air 2 to be used with standard water pipes (typically 14mm or 18mm joints). This was recommended by the community for users seeking smoother, cooler vapor — particularly those with respiratory sensitivities. When purchasing, ensure the WPA is designed for the Air/Solo stem diameter, not the ArGo.

Carrying Cases

The Air 2 ships with a belt-clip carry case. Third-party cases and pouches were also available from accessory retailers. At least one community-recognized retailer (420EDC, based in Maine) specialized in bundling Arizer second-generation portables including the Air 2 with curated accessory packages.

Botanical Dish

The included glass botanical dish allows for aromatherapy use without packing herb directly into a stem.

Silicone Stem Caps

Two silicone stem caps are included, which cap the ends of loaded stems for on-the-go convenience — allowing users to pre-load multiple stems.

Spare Batteries

Carrying additional 18650 batteries was a commonly recommended practice, extending the Air 2's effective session capacity significantly for portable use.

Comparisons

Arizer Air 2 vs. Arizer Air (Original)

The most direct comparison, discussed at length by the community. Key differences:

  • Temperature control: The Air 2 offers precise digital control (1° increments) via OLED display, versus the original Air's fixed preset temperature steps indicated by colored LEDs.
  • Heating technology: The Air 2's heating was described as "a bit improved" with the air bath reportedly "a little more isolated." Community members suggested the Air 2 heats up quicker (by display reading) and may be slightly more powerful overall.
  • Actual vapor production time: Paradoxically, at least one member found the Air 2 took longer to produce actual vapor than the original Air under identical conditions, potentially due to increased airflow in the redesigned unit.
  • Build quality: The original Air's toggle switch was identified as a wear point after years of daily use; the Air 2's button/display interface addressed this.
  • Price premium: The Air 2 commanded roughly a $100 premium over the original Air at launch. Community opinion was somewhat divided on whether this premium was justified for users who didn't specifically need precise temperature control. One experienced member summarized that beyond digital temperature, improved heating and potentially better battery efficiency were the main functional upgrades.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Arizer Solo / Solo 2

All Arizer portables share the same core glass-stem-in-oven design and were described as having "comparable performance" across the lineup. The primary differences are form factor: the Solo/Solo 2 is larger with a non-removable (Solo) or larger internal battery, while the Air 2 features a removable 18650 battery for greater portability. The community advised choosing between Air and Solo variants based on "price and form factor preference" rather than performance.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Arizer ArGo

The ArGo is the most portable of the Arizer second-generation lineup, with a recessed stem design that fits fully inside the unit. However, the ArGo was described as a "sipper" not well-suited for group use. It uses smaller, incompatible glass stems. For maximum portability when "mainly vaping on the go," the community recommended the ArGo; for a balance of portability and session capability with swappable batteries, the Air 2 was preferred.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Storz & Bickel Mighty

A frequently discussed comparison. The community consensus was clear: these are "two entirely different vapes." The Mighty was characterized as a "ripper" — larger, more powerful, with faster extraction and significantly denser vapor, making it better suited for group sessions and users prioritizing strong effects. The Air 2 was the "sipper" — smaller, more efficient with herb, easier to clean, with a replaceable battery and arguably better flavor at low temperatures. One community member who acquired an Air 2 stated they would "take it over a Mighty," citing ease of loading, efficiency, easy cleaning, the replaceable battery, and the Mighty being "kind of a pain in the ass to clean" and a "herb hog." The choice between them depended largely on use case: solo sessions favored the Air 2, while group use and maximum vapor output favored the Mighty.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Storz & Bickel Crafty

One member who owned both noted the Crafty offered "quicker extraction for use at home" and chose to sell the Air 2. The Crafty's faster session time was its primary advantage, though the Air 2's replaceable battery and glass vapor path were counterpoints.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Fury Edge

At least one knowledgeable community member described the Fury Edge as "a great upgrade from Arizer portables," recommending it over the Air 2 for users seeking a more modern hybrid portable. However, this was offered as an alternative suggestion rather than a detailed head-to-head comparison.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Pax 2/3

The Air 2 and Pax were compared by users who owned originals of both. The Pax line offered greater stealth and pocketability with its sealed design and half-pack lid (Pax 3), plus concentrate capability. The Air 2 offered superior flavor via its glass vapor path, easier cleaning, and replaceable batteries. Community members seeking a daily driver for primarily home or semi-portable use generally favored the Air 2 over the Pax.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Vapir Mini Oxygen

One community member advised strongly against the Vapir Mini, describing it as a "very old vape" with a "weak" plastic body and "strong plastic taste." The Air 2, being similar to the Solo which is "much better" than the Vapir, was the clear community recommendation.
