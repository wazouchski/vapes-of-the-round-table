# Arizer Air 2

Overview

The Arizer Air 2 (also written as Arizer Air II) is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. Released in 2017 as the successor to the original Arizer Air, the Air 2 represents Arizer's second-generation portable line alongside the Solo 2 and ArGo. The device uses a hybrid conduction/convection heating system, with a ceramic heating element housed in a stainless steel oven that heats both the oven walls and the air drawn through the herb. It is powered by a single user-replaceable 18650 lithium-ion battery and features a glass aroma tube (stem) that serves as both the mouthpiece and the herb chamber—a signature design element shared across the Arizer portable lineup.

The Air 2's primary upgrades over the original Air include a digital OLED display with precise single-degree temperature control (replacing the original's fixed LED temperature presets), reportedly improved heating technology with better thermal isolation, and a modernized interface. The device occupies a middle ground in the Arizer portable family: more portable than the Solo 2 thanks to its slimmer cylindrical profile and replaceable battery, but less pocket-friendly than the ArGo, which uses shorter retractable stems.

Community Reception

The Arizer Air 2 was generally well-regarded within the vaporizer community as a reliable, efficient, and easy-to-use session portable. Community members frequently recommended it as an excellent entry-level or everyday vaporizer, particularly for users who prioritize clean flavor from the all-glass vapor path, ease of maintenance, and the security of a replaceable battery.

Pros highlighted by the community:

  • Smooth vapor quality: The glass stem vapor path was consistently praised for delivering clean, smooth vapor. Multiple community members specifically recommended Arizer portables for users with respiratory sensitivities, including asthma. One member noted having "two friends with asthma and they get along well with Arizers."
  • Ease of cleaning: The glass stem design was lauded for being exceptionally easy to maintain. One member described the Air 2 as "easy to load, very efficient, easy to clean."
  • Replaceable battery: The use of a standard 18650 battery was considered a significant advantage over sealed-battery competitors, enabling extended sessions with spare batteries.
  • Efficiency: The small glass chamber was noted for being efficient with herb, especially compared to vaporizers with larger ovens. One community member contrasted it favorably with the Mighty, which was characterized as a "herb hog."
  • Build quality and durability: Several users reported multi-year daily use of Arizer products. One member described using their original Air "as a daily driver for a good three years" and having "put many years on an EQ before that."
  • Precise temperature control: The digital display allowing single-degree adjustments was valued, especially for users interested in low-temperature flavor sessions.

Cons and criticisms noted by the community:

  • Session-style extraction speed: The Air 2 was characterized as a "sipper" rather than a "ripper," meaning it extracts slowly over a session rather than delivering heavy hits quickly. One community member explicitly stated "Argo is a sipper while the Mighty is a ripper," a characterization that applied equally to the Air 2. Some users found this slower extraction less satisfying, with one seller noting the Crafty offered "a quicker extraction" at home.
  • Portability limitations: While more portable than the Solo 2, the protruding glass stem made the Air 2 less pocket-friendly than purpose-built portable units. One member noted it was "not terribly portable without a case."
  • Not ideal for groups: The slow extraction rate and small chamber made it less suitable for group sessions. The community generally recommended more powerful units like the Mighty for group use.
  • Screen reliability concerns: At least one community member reported OLED screen failures on two separate Air 2 units, expressing frustration with the issue.
  • Customer service concerns: Some members expressed dissatisfaction with Arizer's warranty and customer service process, noting that "Arizer are a pain to deal with."

Overall, the community positioned the Air 2 as a dependable workhorse suited to solo daily use, flavor chasers, and users who value simplicity and clean vapor over raw extraction power.

Tips & Techniques

The community developed several best practices for getting the most out of the Air 2:

Loading:

  • Pack the glass stem bowl lightly to moderately. Avoid over-packing, as airflow through the herb is essential for the hybrid heating mechanism to function efficiently. A medium grind works well—not too fine, as this can restrict airflow and lead to uneven extraction.
  • The stem can be loaded by pressing the open end gently into a container of ground herb ("straw loading"), a technique shared across Arizer glass-stem devices.

Drawing Technique:

  • The Air 2 responds best to slow, steady draws rather than hard pulls. This allows the air to heat adequately as it passes over the ceramic heater and through the herb. The community noted that pure convection vapes require longer draws, and while the Air 2 is a hybrid, a patient sipping technique yields the best results.
  • One community member noted that users with asthma may find the Air 2's draw requirements more manageable than pure convection devices, which "always means longer draws compared to hybrids/conduction."

Heat-Up Patience:

  • Community members discovered that the displayed temperature reaching the set point does not necessarily mean the herb is ready to produce vapor. One experienced user observed: "Anyone familiar with the Air knows the indicator lights refer to the oven and not the glass." It was recommended to wait an additional 30–60 seconds beyond the indicated set temperature before drawing for best results, particularly on the first draw of a session.

Session Management:

  • Temperature stepping—starting low and gradually increasing the temperature over the course of a session—was a widely practiced technique for maximizing both flavor and extraction.

Maintenance & Cleaning

One of the Air 2's most praised attributes was its exceptionally simple cleaning process, largely owing to the glass vapor path:

Glass Stems:

  • Soak stems in isopropyl alcohol (ISO) for thorough cleaning. The accumulated resin ("stem honey" or "stem milk") inside the glass can also be reclaimed by simmering the stem in milk or a fatty liquid—a practice the community commonly referred to as making "stem milk."
  • For regular maintenance, a quick ISO wipe or soak after several sessions keeps the stems clear and vapor tasting fresh.
  • Stainless steel filter screens in the stem should be periodically cleaned or replaced to maintain airflow.

Oven:

  • The ceramic oven can be cleaned with a cotton swab dampened with ISO while still slightly warm (but not hot). This dissolves any residue that may have accumulated.
  • Avoid letting debris fall into the oven during stem removal; keeping the unit upright during unloading helps.

General:

  • The silicone stem caps included in the box help keep loaded or recently used stems clean during transport.
  • Cleaning frequency depends on usage; daily users may want to clean stems weekly and the oven bi-weekly.

Common Issues & Fixes

Longer-than-expected heat-up time:

A recurring observation in the community was that the Air 2 appeared to take longer than the original Air to begin producing vapor, despite the displayed temperature reaching the set point. One member conducted a side-by-side comparison and concluded that "the higher air flow accounts for the longer (actual) heat-up time." The community's recommended fix was to allow additional time after the display indicates the target temperature has been reached, or to start at a slightly higher temperature for the first few draws.

OLED screen failure:

At least one community member reported screen failures on two separate Air 2 units. This did not appear to be a widespread epidemic based on available community data, but it was noted as a potential point of failure. Users experiencing this issue were advised to contact Arizer for warranty service, though some found the warranty process frustrating.

Toggle switch/button wear (original Air):

While primarily an issue on the original Air, the physical controls were an area where long-term wear could manifest. The Air 2's updated button interface was an improvement, but users were generally advised to handle the controls with care.

Battery life management:

The replaceable 18650 battery was an advantage, but battery life could diminish with age. The community recommended keeping spare 18650 batteries on hand and using a dedicated external charger for optimal battery health rather than relying solely on the unit's USB charging.

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems and Water Pipe Adapters (WPA):

  • The Air 2 uses the same stem diameter as the original Air, Solo, and Solo 2. Stems are fully interchangeable across these models. However, ArGo stems are a smaller diameter and are not compatible with the Air 2—this was confirmed by community members after repeated inquiries.
  • Water pipe adapter (WPA) stems were available from Arizer and third parties, allowing the Air 2 to be used with water pieces for additional cooling and smoother hits. This was particularly recommended for users with respiratory sensitivities.
  • A "high flow stem" was mentioned by at least one community member as an aftermarket option that could improve airflow.

Batteries and Chargers:

  • Standard flat-top 18650 batteries work in the Air 2. The community recommended having at least one or two spares. External 18650 chargers were preferred over the built-in USB charging for battery longevity.

Cases and Carrying Solutions:

  • The included belt-clip carry case provided basic protection. Various third-party cases and tubes for protecting glass stems during transport were popular among daily portable users.
  • Silicone stem caps (included in the box) were useful for keeping loaded stems sealed and fresh.

Third-Party Retailers and Bundles:

  • Specialty retailers offered curated bundles for Arizer second-generation portables (including the Air 2), providing accessories like extra stems, WPAs, cases, and cleaning supplies at bundle pricing.

Glass Botanical Dish:

  • The included glass botanical dish allowed the Air 2 to be used for aromatherapy with non-cannabis botanicals, though this was a secondary use case for most community members.

Comparisons

Arizer Air 2 vs. Original Arizer Air

The most frequently discussed comparison. Key differences noted by the community:

  • Temperature control: The Air 2 offers precise single-degree digital control via OLED display; the original Air uses fixed LED temperature presets. This was considered the Air 2's most significant upgrade, especially for low-temperature flavor sessions.
  • Heating technology: The Air 2 reportedly features improved heating with better thermal isolation of the air bath, potentially leading to more efficient heating, though one member observed a longer actual vapor-production time despite the faster indicated heat-up.
  • Performance: Community opinion suggested the Air 2 heats up quicker to set temperature and may be "a bit more powerful" overall, though the real-world difference was considered modest. Some questioned whether the upgrade justified the $100 price premium at launch.
  • Build quality: The original Air's toggle switch was identified as a wear point over years of daily use; the Air 2's updated controls addressed this.
  • Stem compatibility: Full interchangeability; all Air/Solo stems fit both generations.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Arizer Solo / Solo 2

The Air 2 and Solo 2 share the same heating technology and generation. The primary differences are form factor: the Solo 2 has a larger body (not cylindrical/pocket-friendly), a built-in non-removable battery with longer life, and was considered slightly more powerful for home use. The Air 2's advantage was its replaceable 18650 battery and more portable form factor. The community generally recommended: "If you mainly vape at home, get a Solo or Solo II. If you want something in between with changeable [batteries], get an Air 2."

Arizer Air 2 vs. Arizer ArGo

The ArGo was the most pocket-friendly Arizer portable, using shorter stems that retract fully into the body. Key differences:

  • ArGo stems are a smaller diameter and not interchangeable with Air 2/Solo stems.
  • The ArGo was considered more discreet and portable but with a slightly smaller chamber.
  • Performance was described as comparable, with the community advising users to "choose by price and form factor preference."
  • The ArGo was noted as similarly not ideal for group use.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Storz & Bickel Mighty

A frequent comparison point. The community generally characterized the Mighty as significantly more powerful with faster extraction ("a ripper") versus the Air 2's slower, sipping session style. The Mighty was preferred for group use and users seeking intense effects. However, the Air 2 was favored for its efficiency (smaller chamber using less herb), ease of cleaning, replaceable battery, and glass vapor path. One community member stated: "I just got an Air II and can honestly say I'd take it over a Mighty," citing ease of loading, efficiency, cleanability, and the replaceable battery as advantages, while acknowledging the Mighty's superior power for solo users "who want to get really lifted."

Arizer Air 2 vs. Storz & Bickel Crafty

One user who owned both noted that the Crafty offered "a quicker extraction for use at home" compared to the Air 2, ultimately choosing to sell the Arizer. The Crafty's faster session time was its primary advantage in this comparison.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Fury Edge

Recommended by some community members as a more modern alternative. One experienced member described the Fury Edge as "a great upgrade from Arizer portables," though this was a minority opinion with limited elaboration in the available discussions.

Arizer Air 2 vs. Vapir Mini Oxygen

A dated comparison. The community unanimously preferred the Air 2, noting the Vapir Mini was "a very old vape" with a "weak plastic body" and "strong plastic taste." The Air 2, being similar to the Solo line, was considered "much better" in every respect.
