# Splinter by RBT

Overview

The Splinter is a 510-threaded dry herb vaporizer attachment designed and handmade by Ryan of RBT (Ryan's Bespoke Timber), a small artisan manufacturer based in the Adirondack region of New York. First appearing in beta form around mid-2018, the Splinter attaches to standard box mods via a 510 connection, using the mod's power and temperature control capabilities to heat dry herb through convection.

The device features a distinctive wooden body (commonly walnut) housing a mesh-based heater element, with a glass air path and replaceable screens. Rather than being a standalone vaporizer, the Splinter relies entirely on the user's choice of box mod for power delivery, temperature regulation, and battery life — making it a modular, customizable system. Several variants were produced, including the original Splinter, the Beta Splinter, and the Splinter V2 (also known as the Splinter Z).

The Splinter is classified as a convection vaporizer, as heated air passes through the herb material rather than the herb sitting on a hot surface. Its power source is whatever 18650-based (or similar) box mod the user pairs it with.

Community Reception

The vaporizer community regarded the Splinter favorably as a capable convection 510 attachment, particularly valued for its artisan build quality and the flavor produced by its wood-and-glass construction. Its handmade nature — early beta units had no decals or numbering on the bottom plate — gave it a boutique appeal that resonated with enthusiasts.

Pros frequently cited by the community:

  • Excellent convection flavor
  • Beautiful wood and glass aesthetics
  • Modularity — users could pair it with any compatible mod and customize the experience
  • DNA mod pairing allowed precise, shareable temperature control profiles
  • Relatively simple design with replaceable screens

Cons and challenges noted:

  • Steep learning curve, particularly for users unfamiliar with box mods, firmware, and temperature coefficient (TCR) settings
  • Required significant setup and tuning to achieve optimal performance
  • Draw technique differed substantially from conduction vaporizers, which tripped up newcomers
  • Risk of combustion if settings were configured improperly (one seller noted a visible combustion mark on a screen)
  • Dependence on an external mod meant total system cost could be significant, and battery life varied with mod choice

The community was notably welcoming and patient with new Splinter users. As one community member put it, the Splinter thread was a place where experienced users were happy to provide "a bit of handholding" since setup questions were common and expected.

Tips & Techniques

Draw Technique

The Splinter's convection heating requires a fundamentally different draw technique compared to conduction vaporizers. Community members emphasized:

  • Take slow, long draws that move a significant volume of air through the device. Unlike conduction vaporizers (such as the Arizer ArGo), where short sips can work, the Splinter needs sustained airflow to carry heat through the herb.
  • One experienced community member specifically cautioned newcomers against comparing draw technique to conduction devices, noting that moving a lot of air is essential for proper extraction.

Box Mod Configuration

Setting up the box mod correctly was the single most discussed topic regarding the Splinter. The community identified several approaches depending on the mod chipset:

For DNA-based mods (recommended by many):

Download and install Escribe software on a computer

Connect the DNA mod via USB

Install any available service packs for the mod when prompted by Escribe

Download the community-created Splinter/Stempod profiles from the Evolv forum (a file package commonly referred to as "FCShare")

Use the Load Profile function in Escribe to import the `.ecigprofile` file

Click Upload Settings To Device

Optionally install a custom theme (community member "Frank" created a well-regarded theme)

Critical tip for DNA mods: If the mod already has too many materials loaded in memory, importing new profiles may fail to load the associated material/TCR curve. The community advised removing unused materials from the device in Escribe before loading Splinter profiles. This was a common pitfall that caused confusion among new users.

For Joyetech/Wismec mods with myevic or Sur_myevic firmware:

Flash the mod with Sur_myevic firmware (this version includes "Cruise Mode," which was considered beneficial for Splinter use)

Enable Sweet PID under the Vaping > Algo menu for smoother temperature regulation

Start in plain TC (Temperature Control) mode to dial in settings before experimenting with Cruise Mode

For YiHi-based mods:

  • Less community consensus existed for YiHi setup. Users reported using TCR mode with settings around 170 and wattage around 40W for preheat, dropping to 29–32W during use, though these values were not universally confirmed.

General Mod Selection Advice

The community broadly divided mod recommendations into two tiers:

  • Budget option: Wismec RX Gen 3 Dual — widely used, with shareable settings and custom firmware (Arctic Fox/myevic) support. Does not support downloadable profiles but allows TCR and wattage customization.
  • Premium option: DNA-based mods (HCigar VT75D, Lost Vape Therion, etc.) — more expensive but supported downloadable profiles via Escribe, making setup more straightforward and results more consistent across users.

Temperature Guide

Temperature settings on the Splinter are entered through the box mod and do not directly correspond to the actual herb temperature, as the mod reads the heater's resistance to estimate temperature. Community members emphasized adjusting by vapor output rather than trusting the screen display.

Fine-Tuning Temperature

Because the displayed temperature is an estimate based on TCR calculations:

  • If vapor is too wispy, increase temperature by 10°C (18°F) and retry
  • Advanced users can fine-tune by adjusting the locked resistance up or down in 0.001–0.002 Ω increments, which effectively shifts the temperature curve
  • Adjusting TCR value also shifts effective temperature — a lower TCR (e.g., 125) versus a higher one (e.g., 140–170) changes how aggressively the mod heats

Maintenance & Cleaning

Specific maintenance procedures were not extensively detailed in the available discussions, but the following can be gleaned from community posts:

  • Screens are replaceable and should be swapped when clogged or when combustion residue is visible (brown/black spots indicate possible combustion events)
  • The glass stem should be cleaned regularly; standard glass cleaning methods (isopropyl alcohol soak) are applicable
  • The wooden body should not be submerged or soaked; dry brushing or a lightly dampened cloth is appropriate
  • Keeping the 510 connection clean ensures consistent resistance readings, which is critical for accurate temperature control

Common Issues & Fixes

Low or No Vapor Production

Symptoms: Wispy hits, significantly less vapor than expected, especially compared to conduction devices.

Solutions:

  • Ensure draw technique is correct: slow, long draws moving substantial air volume
  • Increase temperature in 10°C increments until satisfactory vapor is produced
  • Verify TCR is set appropriately (start with 125–140)
  • On myevic/Sur_myevic firmware, enable Sweet PID for better temperature regulation
  • Start tuning in plain TC mode before using Cruise Mode
  • Confirm the coil resistance is properly locked at room temperature

Profiles Not Loading Correctly on DNA Mods

Symptoms: Settings appear to load but the device does not perform as expected; material curves are missing.

Solution: Clear unused materials from the device in Escribe before importing Splinter profiles. The DNA chipset has a limited number of material slots, and new profiles will silently fail to load their associated material if no slots are available.

Firmware Version Confusion

Symptoms: Expected features (such as Cruise Mode) are missing after flashing firmware.

Solution: Ensure the correct firmware variant is installed. For Cruise Mode support, the Sur_myevic firmware build is required, not the standard myevic release.

Accidental Combustion

Symptoms: Harsh taste, visible brown/black marks on the screen.

Solution: Replace the screen, lower temperature settings, and ensure the mod is operating in TC mode (not wattage-only mode, which provides no temperature ceiling).

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems

The Splinter uses glass stems that serve as both the mouthpiece and loading chamber. Some models accept 19/19mm stems, which opened up compatibility with aftermarket glass pieces. One community listing mentioned a Vigreaux Shorty glass stem (2.75" long, the same length as an 18650 battery) as a compatible upgrade that enhanced vapor quality, though the seller noted uncertainty about which Splinter models it fit.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

The Splinter's glass stem design made it naturally compatible with water pipe use. Glass-on-glass adapters in 14mm and 18mm sizes were commonly used in the community.

Screens

The Splinter shipped with metal screens that could be replaced. Community members sometimes substituted screens of their preference; one seller noted including both the original screen and a preferred alternative.

Custom Themes (DNA Mods)

For DNA mod users, custom screen themes were available. A community member known as "Frank" (HerbieVonVapster) created and shared Splinter-specific themes (`.ecigtheme` files) and was actively updating profile packages for various DNA chipset variants, including the DNA75C.

Comparisons

Direct comparison data in the available threads was limited, but several contextual comparisons emerged:

  • Splinter vs. Arizer ArGo: One user found the Splinter initially produced less vapor than a fully heated ArGo. Community members attributed this to draw technique differences — the ArGo is a conduction/hybrid portable that works with short sips, while the Splinter's convection design demands longer, slower draws.
  • Splinter vs. Stempod: The two devices were frequently mentioned together as 510-threaded convection attachments that shared compatible Escribe profiles and firmware settings. The community-shared profile package on the Evolv forum covered both devices.
  • Splinter vs. iHeat: The iHeat, another 510 attachment featuring a wooden chamber, was mentioned as a point of interest for users attracted to wood-bodied vaporizers exploring the 510 ecosystem. No detailed performance comparisons were available in the reviewed threads.
  • Splinter vs. ModPod: Both appeared in the same marketplace listings, suggesting overlap in the user base. The ModPod used replaceable coils and a different design philosophy, while the Splinter's mesh heater was more integrated.
  • Splinter Original vs. Splinter Z (V2): The Z variant was referenced in the context of different Escribe profiles ("use the splinter or splinterZ profile"), indicating meaningful differences in heater characteristics or resistance that required distinct temperature control settings.
