# Sticky Brick Junior

Overview

The Sticky Brick Junior (commonly abbreviated as SBJ) is a compact, portable butane-powered dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Sticky Brick Labs, based in the United States. It is part of the broader Sticky Brick lineup, which includes the Sticky Brick OG (the original full-size model), the Sticky Brick Runt, the Flip Brick, and the HydroBrick. The Junior is the most compact handheld model in the range, designed for portability while retaining the powerful convection heating that the Sticky Brick line is known for.

The device uses convection heating — a butane torch flame is directed through an intake port and glass pathway, where the hot air passes over the herb loaded in a bowl, extracting vapor without direct contact between the flame and the material. This on-demand design means there is no heat-up wait time; the user controls vapor production in real time through their draw speed and torch technique. The power source is any standard butane torch lighter (typically a single- or dual-flame torch), making the Junior fully self-contained and independent of any electrical power source.

The body of the Sticky Brick Junior is crafted from hardwood, with internal glass air pathways and a glass mouthpiece. The device is known in the vaporizer community for delivering surprisingly dense, heavy-hitting vapor from a small, pocketable form factor.

Community Reception

The Sticky Brick Junior was widely well-regarded within the vaporizer community, particularly among users who valued hard-hitting, on-demand vapor extraction from a portable device. It earned a reputation as one of the most powerful portable vaporizers available, frequently recommended alongside heavy-hitting electrics like the Tubo Evic when users sought portable alternatives to desktop powerhouses such as the Flowerpot or Sublimator.

Pros frequently cited by the community:

  • Massive cloud production: One community member noted that the Junior produces "really smooth and huge clouds," adding, "Sometimes I don't realize how much I am getting until I exhale." This sentiment was echoed by many users who found the vapor density comparable to or exceeding many electric portables.
  • On-demand heating: No waiting for heat-up. The user is in full control of extraction speed and intensity.
  • Simplicity and durability: With no electronics, batteries, or circuit boards, the device has very few failure points. The hardwood body is robust.
  • Portability: Significantly easier to move around than a desktop setup — no extension cords or power outlets needed.
  • Pairs well with glass: The SBJ was frequently discussed in the context of use with water pipes, and the community considered it an excellent candidate for glass pairing.

Cons and commonly noted drawbacks:

  • Learning curve: Butane technique vaporizers require practice to find the right torch distance and draw speed to avoid combustion. This was a persistent topic of discussion.
  • Combustion risk: Users new to the device sometimes scorched or combusted their herb before developing proper technique.
  • Glass fragility: The glass mouthpiece and internal glass pathway, while functional and flavor-preserving, are breakable. Community members reported accidentally dropping and breaking the glass mouthpiece, and replacement parts could be difficult or expensive to source (upwards of €50 with international shipping in some cases).
  • Torch dependence: Requires a reliable butane torch lighter and periodic refilling.

Overall, the community regarded the Sticky Brick Junior as an outstanding value proposition for users seeking desktop-level vapor production in a portable, no-electronics package — provided the user was willing to invest time in mastering the technique.

Tips & Techniques

The Sticky Brick Junior's performance is almost entirely technique-dependent. The community developed and shared extensive guidance on getting the best results:

Torch Technique

  • Use a single-flame or dual-flame torch lighter. Triple- and quad-flame torches can deliver too much heat too quickly, increasing the risk of combustion.
  • Aim the flame into the intake hole at a moderate distance — not directly touching the glass. The goal is to heat the air entering the glass pathway, not to overheat the glass itself.
  • Feather the flame: Rather than holding the torch continuously, many experienced users recommended pulsing the flame — short bursts interspersed with brief pauses — to control temperature and prevent hotspots.

Draw Speed

  • Draw slowly and steadily. A slow draw pulls more hot air across the herb at a higher effective temperature, producing denser vapor. Drawing too fast cools the air and produces thin or no vapor; drawing too slowly with the torch engaged risks combustion.
  • The community widely agreed that finding the "sweet spot" between draw speed and torch intensity is the key skill to master.

Loading

  • Grind to a medium consistency. Too fine a grind can restrict airflow and increase combustion risk; too coarse reduces surface area and efficiency.
  • Do not overpack the bowl. A loosely packed, level bowl allows air to flow through evenly, promoting even extraction.
  • Stir between hits if doing a multi-hit session to expose fresh surfaces.

Restrictor Disc

  • Sticky Brick Labs offered an optional restrictor disc — a small disc with a hole that sits in the air path to slow and focus airflow. Many community members found this accessory essential for taming the device and making it more forgiving for beginners, as it reduces the likelihood of combustion by moderating how much hot air passes through the bowl at once.

Temperature Guide

Because the Sticky Brick Junior has no electronic temperature control, "temperature" is managed entirely through the user's technique — specifically torch distance, flame size, draw speed, and session duration. There is no digital readout.

That said, the community developed general guidelines based on the principles of convection vaporization:

EffectApproximate Air TemperatureTechnique Notes
Flavor-focused / light extraction~170–185°C (338–365°F)Hold the torch further from the intake; use a slightly faster draw. Produces wispy, flavorful vapor.
Balanced session~185–200°C (365–392°F)Moderate torch distance and draw speed. Good balance of flavor and cloud density.
Heavy clouds / full extraction~200–220°C (392–428°F)Torch closer to the intake; slow, steady draw. Produces dense, milky clouds. Risk of combustion increases at the upper end.

Community "sweet spot": Most experienced users reported that the SBJ excels when pushed into the mid-to-upper range, producing the thick, milky clouds that earned it its reputation. However, the device is also capable of excellent flavor at lower effective temperatures for users who prefer a lighter touch.

> Combustion warning: Temperatures above approximately 230°C (446°F) will combust the herb. If the vapor tastes harsh or acrid, or if you see dark smoke rather than lighter-colored vapor, you have likely combusted. Reduce torch proximity and/or increase draw speed.

Maintenance & Cleaning

The Sticky Brick Junior is relatively low-maintenance due to its simple design, but regular cleaning maintains airflow, flavor, and performance.

Glass Components

  • Glass mouthpiece and internal glass air path: Soak in isopropyl alcohol (ISO) — 90% or higher concentration is recommended — for 15–30 minutes, then rinse with warm water. A pipe cleaner can help dislodge residue from the glass tubes.
  • Clean glass components after every few sessions, or whenever vapor flavor degrades or draw resistance increases.

Screen

  • Replace or clean the bowl screen regularly. A clogged screen restricts airflow and affects performance. Screens can be soaked in ISO or simply replaced when they become too fouled.

Wood Body

  • Do not soak the wood in alcohol or water. Wipe the exterior with a barely damp cloth if needed.
  • The interior wood surfaces will develop a patina of resin over time. Some users regarded this as beneficial (adding thermal mass and seasoning), while others preferred to keep it clean using a dry brush or a cotton swab with minimal ISO.

General

  • Allow all components to dry fully before reassembling and using the device, especially after ISO cleaning.
  • Handle the glass mouthpiece with care — it is the most fragile and most commonly broken component.

Accessories & Modifications

Water Pipe Adapter (WPA) / U-Joint

The most discussed accessory for the Sticky Brick Junior was the U-joint water pipe adapter, which allows the SBJ to be connected to a water pipe (bong/bubbler) for cooled, filtered vapor. Key community insights on WPA use:

  • The official Sticky Brick Labs U-joint WPA was available in 14mm, matching the Junior's native joint size. Users with 18mm glass could use a simple 14mm-to-18mm glass adapter.
  • One community member advised that basing one's glass collection on the 18.8mm joint size is more adaptable, as it is easy to step down from 18mm to 14mm with an inexpensive adapter, but going the other way is less practical.
  • Some community members raised concerns about whether the U-bend adapter would bring the Brick far enough away from the central joint of certain glass pieces (such as the popular Mobius Matrix Sidecar) to avoid interference. Fit testing before committing was advised.
  • An alternative, budget-friendly approach mentioned in the community was to attach a short silicone whip over the glass mouthpiece, connected to a male glass adapter that fits into the water pipe joint. This avoided the cost of the official U-joint (which could run €50 or more with international shipping) and offered more flexibility in positioning.

Restrictor Disc

As noted above, the restrictor disc was considered nearly essential by many users, particularly for beginners. It moderates airflow and makes the device more forgiving.

Recommended Glass Pieces

The community frequently paired the SBJ with small to medium bubblers and sidecar-style water pipes. Popular choices included:

  • Mobius Matrix Sidecar (or DHgate clones thereof)
  • Small beaker-style or straight-tube pieces with minimal diffusion, to preserve flavor while adding water cooling

Torch Lighters

A reliable, adjustable single- or dual-flame butane torch lighter was considered a critical companion. Many users kept multiple lighters on hand, as inexpensive torches can be unreliable.

Comparisons

The Sticky Brick Junior was frequently compared to other vaporizers in community discussions, particularly when users sought hard-hitting portable options that paired well with glass:

Sticky Brick Junior vs. Tubo Evic

Both were recommended as top-tier portable options for users seeking desktop-level cloud production. The Tubo Evic (an electronic convection vaporizer) was described by one community member as "not quite as hard hitting as the Flowerpot, but the closest portable" they had used. The SBJ, being butane-powered, offered potentially faster and denser extraction with no battery limitations, but required more manual skill. The Tubo Evic offered electronic temperature control and more consistency, at the cost of battery dependence and higher price.

Sticky Brick Junior vs. Flowerpot (Desktop)

The Flowerpot was widely regarded as the gold standard for maximum extraction power through glass. Users who wanted Flowerpot-level performance in a portable form factor were sometimes directed to the SBJ as the closest butane-powered analog, though the community acknowledged that no portable could fully match the Flowerpot's sustained, hands-free power. One community member pushed back against the perception that the Flowerpot was overly complex, noting that "all you need is a single extension cable" to use it outdoors.

Sticky Brick Junior vs. Sticky Brick OG

The OG is the full-size original Sticky Brick. The Junior sacrifices some bowl size and ergonomics for significantly improved portability. Both share the same fundamental operating principle and convection heating design. Users who primarily used their Brick at home sometimes preferred the OG for its larger size and easier handling; the Junior was favored by those who valued pocket-friendliness.

Sticky Brick Junior vs. Arizer Solo

One user who had used the Arizer Solo for years and considered it a favorite found the SBJ appealing as a harder-hitting alternative. The Solo is a session-style electric portable with gentle extraction; the SBJ offers on-demand, significantly more powerful convection hits, but without the simplicity and consistency of the Solo's electronic temperature control.

Sticky Brick Junior vs. Lotus

Both are butane-powered convection vaporizers requiring manual technique. The Lotus uses a flame cap heated externally and placed on top of a bowl, while the SBJ directs heated air through an internal glass pathway. Both were well-regarded in the community for their analog simplicity and hard-hitting potential, with the SBJ being somewhat more portable and self-contained.
