# DaVinci (Original)

Overview

The DaVinci (sometimes written as "Da Vinci") is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by DaVinci (originally marketed under the Organicix brand). Released around 2012, it was among the earlier generation of portable conduction-style vaporizers. The unit features a rechargeable lithium-ion battery and uses conduction heating to vaporize dry herb. It was also marketed as capable of handling oils and concentrates via included oil cans. The DaVinci was characterized by its rugged, rectangular form factor with a cool black matte finish and a flip-open lid design. It was positioned as a feature-rich, affordable portable option during a period when the portable vaporizer market was still relatively young.

Community Reception

The original DaVinci received a mixed but generally modest reception within the vaporizer community. It was regarded as a solid entry-level portable vaporizer for its time, with community members acknowledging its rugged build quality and feature-rich platform. One community member selling their unit described it as a "great little vape," noting that it held its battery charge well and performed as expected when cared for properly.

Pros:

  • Solid, durable construction with a quality feel
  • Digital temperature control, which was a notable feature for portables of this era
  • Compact and pocketable design
  • Capable of handling both dry herb and oils/concentrates
  • Reasonable secondhand value, making it accessible as a budget option

Cons:

  • Short battery life was frequently cited as a significant drawback; community members explicitly noted this as a known issue with the device
  • Straw/mouthpiece degradation — the included straws were reported to fray and fall apart over time, a recurring annoyance
  • Oil can issues — users noted small annoyances with the oil concentrate accessories
  • Being a conduction vaporizer, it was generally considered to produce less flavorful vapor compared to convection alternatives that were emerging at the time
  • Odor management was a concern for stealth-conscious users, as with most portable vaporizers of its generation

At least one community member who had owned the original DaVinci (purchased in Fall 2012) moved on to research other options — specifically the Arizer Solo and Arizer Air — citing the battery life issues and accessory degradation as motivating factors for upgrading.

Tips & Techniques

While the available community discussion on this specific model is limited in terms of detailed technique sharing, the following general practices were understood:

  • Grind consistency: A medium to fine grind was generally recommended for conduction portables of this type to ensure even heat distribution throughout the chamber.
  • Packing: Firm but not overly tight packing helps ensure good conduction contact without restricting airflow.
  • Straw management: Given the known issue of straws fraying, users were advised to keep spare straws on hand and replace them at the first sign of degradation.
  • Smell containment: Community members emphasized that all portable vaporizers produce some smell, and recommended storing both the vaporizer and ground herb in odor-proof containers. One community member specifically recommended small wooden stash boxes or CVault containers for herb storage as part of an overall stealth strategy.
  • Session pacing: With battery life being a known limitation, users were advised to be mindful of session length and keep the unit charged between uses.

Temperature Guide

The DaVinci featured adjustable digital temperature control, which was a notable advantage over some competitors at the time. While community-specific temperature recommendations for this exact model were not extensively documented in the available discussions, general community guidance for conduction portables of this era applies:

Users were generally advised to start at lower temperatures and work upward through a session to maximize both flavor and extraction.

Maintenance & Cleaning

  • Cleaning brush: The DaVinci shipped with a dedicated cleaning brush (one community member noted theirs still had "all its bristles"), which was the primary tool for clearing the chamber of ABV (already-been-vaped) material after each session.
  • Straw replacement: The straws/mouthpieces required periodic replacement due to their tendency to fray and deteriorate with use. Keeping spare parts on hand was recommended.
  • Chamber cleaning: Regular brushing of the conduction chamber after sessions helps maintain vapor quality and prevents buildup of residue.
  • Isopropyl alcohol: Standard cleaning practices for portable vaporizers of this era involved periodic wipe-downs of the vapor path and chamber with isopropyl alcohol (90% or higher recommended) applied to a cotton swab or pipe cleaner.
  • Oil can maintenance: Users who utilized the oil/concentrate functionality were advised to clean the oil cans regularly to prevent clogging and residue buildup.

Common Issues & Fixes

IssueDetails & Fix
Short battery lifeThe most widely reported issue. The manufacturer claimed up to 1.4 hours, but real-world use often fell short. No user-serviceable fix was available, as the battery was internal. Keeping the unit plugged in when not in use and avoiding deep discharge cycles could help preserve battery longevity.
Straws fraying/falling apartA known design weakness. The mouthpiece straws degraded with regular use. The fix was simply to replace them with spare straws, which were available as replacement parts.
Oil can annoyancesSome users reported difficulties with the oil/concentrate accessories. Keeping them clean and properly seated was the recommended approach.
Lid design (non-magnetic vs. magnetic)Earlier production runs featured a non-magnetic lid, which some users found less secure. Later revisions introduced a magnetic lid closure. This was a manufacturing revision, not a user-fixable issue, though it is worth noting when purchasing secondhand units.

  • Cleaning brush

Community discussion of aftermarket modifications or water pipe adapter (WPA) compatibility for the original DaVinci was limited in the available threads. The device was largely used in its stock configuration.

Comparisons

The DaVinci was most frequently mentioned in the context of users who had outgrown it and were seeking upgrades. The most notable comparison points from community discussion include:

DaVinci vs. Arizer Solo / Arizer Air:

At least one experienced DaVinci owner specifically sought to upgrade to the Arizer lineup, citing the DaVinci's battery life and accessory durability issues as primary motivators. The Arizer Solo and Air were perceived as offering superior vapor quality, better battery life, and more durable construction, albeit at a higher price point (~$182 for the Air at the time). The Arizer devices use a hybrid heating approach with glass stems, which represented a significant step up in vapor path purity compared to the DaVinci's straw system.

DaVinci vs. Indica Vaporizer:

One community member noted that the Indica vaporizer produced less smell than other portables they owned, and featured the ability to use while charging (passthrough charging) — attributes that would have addressed some of the DaVinci's noted shortcomings. However, the Indica was priced around $200, placing it above the DaVinci's price bracket.

DaVinci vs. Lotus:

The Lotus, a butane-powered portable, was suggested as an alternative for users open to torch-lighter operation. It represented a fundamentally different approach (convection, manual heating) and was praised for being an "excellent value and loads of fun" by community members, though it sacrificed the electronic convenience the DaVinci offered.

Secondhand Market Value:

The original DaVinci settled at approximately $75 on the secondhand market within the community, which reflected its positioning as a functional but aging portable with known limitations. Multiple listings at this price point confirmed this as the community's consensus fair value for the device in good condition.
