# Arizer Extreme Q

Overview

The Arizer Extreme Q (commonly abbreviated as "EQ" or simply "the E" or "the Extreme") is a desktop dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company. First released around 2008 (with iterative version updates including the V3 and later the V4), the Extreme Q is a multi-function desktop unit offering both whip (direct draw) and balloon (bag) delivery methods. It uses a convection-dominant heating system with a ceramic heating element, drawing air up through a glass "cyclone bowl" where herb is loaded. The unit is powered by mains electricity and includes a built-in fan with adjustable speed settings for assisted draw and balloon inflation.

The Extreme Q quickly became one of the most popular budget-friendly desktop vaporizers in the community, frequently recommended as a versatile entry-level unit and often positioned as an affordable alternative to the Storz & Bickel Volcano. Its combination of whip and bag capability, digital temperature control, remote control, and 18.8mm glass-on-glass connections made it a fixture in community discussions for well over a decade.

Community Reception

The Arizer Extreme Q was one of the most discussed and widely recommended desktop vaporizers in the community, particularly for users on a budget or those seeking versatility. The community widely agreed on several key strengths and weaknesses:

Pros:

  • Versatility: The combination of whip, bag, and fan-assisted modes in a single unit was considered exceptional value. One community member with experience across multiple units described it as the vape to buy "if you like both bags and whip" since "it does both pretty good."
  • Price-to-performance ratio: Frequently cited as the best value in desktop vaporizers. The community consistently recommended it to first-time buyers and those who could not justify Volcano pricing.
  • Glass air path: The all-glass vapor path (cyclone bowl, elbow adapter) was regarded positively for flavor. A knowledgeable community member corrected a common misconception about air path purity, noting that "the air is not heated until it is inside the glass air path and past all circuitry."
  • 18.8mm glass connections: The standard glass-on-glass joint size made it highly compatible with water pipes and third-party accessories.
  • Remote control: Valued for convenience, particularly recommended for users with mobility challenges. One community member specifically recommended the EQ for an elderly user with hand tremors, noting it "can be used via a remote control (adjust temperature settings, fan, lighting etc)."
  • Group-friendly: The bag system made it excellent for social sessions. Multiple community members described it as a "great party vape."

Cons:

  • "Jack of all trades, master of none": This was perhaps the single most repeated characterization in the community. One experienced member who owned both the EQ and the Silver Surfer stated plainly: "Extreme.... Jack of all trades... master of none. SSV... Best whip vape." The community generally acknowledged that dedicated whip vapes (SSV, Da Buddha) produced thicker hits and dedicated bag vapes (Volcano) offered more polished bag functionality.
  • Bag system limitations: Unlike the Volcano's valve system, the EQ's bags do not seal with a clamping lock. One owner noted this as the primary complaint, calling workarounds "clumsy."
  • Herb consumption: Some users felt the EQ used more herb than necessary. One user switching from the EQ reported using "around 1g a day" and sought a more efficient unit.
  • Glass fragility: The reliance on glass components (cyclone bowl, elbow, whip wand) made the unit prone to breakage from accidental impacts. One experienced member noted they would "be more concerned about all the glass in this model, as far as accidents."
  • Bowl design flaw: The community identified that the hole in the bottom of the cyclone bowl was smaller than the screen diameter, causing uneven heating and herb being pushed to the edges during fan operation.
  • Fan speed for bags: The maximum fan speed was noted as slower than the Volcano's pump, with one user observing bags filled "at 50% of the speed" compared to the Volcano.

Despite these criticisms, the prevailing community sentiment was that the Extreme Q offered remarkable value. For users who wanted flexibility in delivery method without investing in multiple units, it was consistently the top recommendation in its price class.

Tips & Techniques

The community developed an extensive body of technique around the Extreme Q, with certain methods becoming near-universal recommendations.

The Elbow Pack Method

The single most important technique discovered and refined by the community for the Extreme Q. Instead of loading herb into the cyclone bowl, users pack a small amount (approximately 0.05–0.15g) directly into the screen of the elbow adapter piece. This method was widely praised for:

  • Increased vapor density: Multiple community members reported significantly denser hits. One member noted that "elbow packs produce very dense vapor" capable of "lung busting hits."
  • Better efficiency: The smaller load and closer proximity to the air stream meant more complete extraction with less material.
  • More even extraction: The hot air is forced directly through a thin layer of herb, eliminating the uneven heating issues of the cyclone bowl.

How to elbow pack: Grind herb finely, then press a small amount into the screen of the elbow adapter. The pack should be firm enough to stay in place but loose enough to allow airflow. Insert the elbow into the cyclone bowl (which remains empty) or directly onto the heating element via the glass-on-glass connection.

Preheating

The community strongly recommended allowing the unit to preheat for at least 10 minutes before use, well beyond the point where the digital display indicates the target temperature has been reached. This allows the ceramic element and glass components to fully saturate with heat, resulting in more consistent vapor production. Some users recommended preheating at a higher temperature (e.g., 200–230°C) and then adjusting downward before loading herb.

Bowl Stirring

When using the cyclone bowl method (as opposed to elbow packing), stirring between draws was considered essential. The included glass stirring tool was used to agitate the herb, with one member recommending creating "a small volcano pile right on top of" the center hole to ensure even heat exposure.

Fan-Assisted Whip Hits

Running the fan at the lowest setting while drawing from the whip was recommended for more consistent vapor production, as it maintained steady airflow through the herb rather than relying entirely on lung power.

Temperature Stepping

A widely practiced technique where users begin at a lower temperature and progressively increase over the course of a session. One experienced user described their EQ routine: starting at 175°C and "slowly making my way up to 260°C." This approach was said to provide a wider range of flavor profiles and effects while maximizing extraction.

Cyclone Bowl Modification

One community member shared a modification to address the undersized hole in the cyclone bowl bottom. By carefully enlarging the opening (using a round file or similar tool), hot air could contact the entire screen surface, dramatically improving even heating. This modification was recommended with the caveat of placing an additional screen underneath to prevent material from falling through.

Temperature Guide

Temperature recommendations varied depending on loading method, as the elbow pack positions herb farther from the heating element than the cyclone bowl.

Cyclone Bowl Temperatures

Temperature RangeEffect
170–185°C (338–365°F)Light, flavorful vapor; more cerebral effects; preserves terpenes
185–200°C (365–392°F)Moderate vapor density; balanced effects; community sweet spot for flavor and efficiency
200–220°C (392–428°F)Dense vapor; fuller body effects; more complete extraction
220°C+ (428°F+)Very dense vapor; diminishing flavor; approaching full extraction

Elbow Pack Temperatures

Because the herb sits farther from the heating element in an elbow pack, the community recommended adding approximately 5–10°C to compensate. One experienced member provided specific guidance: "190–220 for cyclone bowl, 200–230 for elbow pack generally."

Bag Filling

For balloon use, many community members reported good results around 190–210°C (374–410°F) for flavorful bags. A temperature of approximately 355°F (~180°C) was cited by one member as producing 2–3 good bags from a light load of less than 0.2g. Heavier loads of 0.4–0.5g with stirring reportedly produced 8–9 bags.

Temperature Stepping Protocol

A common community-recommended progression:

First draws: 175–185°C (347–365°F) — light, terpene-rich vapor

Mid-session: 195–210°C (383–410°F) — moderate density

Final extraction: 220–260°C (428–500°F) — maximum extraction, less flavor

Important Note on ABV

Users who wished to save their already-been-vaped (ABV) material for edibles often kept temperatures below 200°C (392°F) to preserve some active compounds. Those prioritizing potent ABV sometimes intentionally under-extracted at lower temperatures.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Components

All glass pieces (cyclone bowl, elbow adapter, whip wand, glass stirring tool) accumulate resin with use. The community recommended:

  • Isopropyl alcohol (ISO) soaking: Submerge glass pieces in 90%+ isopropyl alcohol for 30 minutes to several hours. Rinse thoroughly with hot water afterward.
  • "Stem milk" or reclaim harvesting: Before cleaning, many users would simmer glass pieces in milk (the fat content absorbs the cannabinoid-laden resin) to create a potent beverage, reclaiming what would otherwise be discarded.
  • Frequency: Glass components should be cleaned whenever visible buildup restricts airflow or affects flavor — typically every 1–2 weeks with regular use.

Screens

The stainless steel screens in the cyclone bowl and elbow adapter require regular attention:

  • Replace screens when they become clogged and no longer clean effectively
  • Soak in ISO to extend screen life
  • Keep spare screens on hand

Whip Tubing

The silicone or vinyl whip tubing collects resin over time and should be replaced periodically or cleaned with ISO. Some community members preferred to replace tubing regularly rather than clean it.

Heating Element

The ceramic heating element should be kept free of debris. Running the unit empty at a high temperature for several minutes can help burn off any residue. The community recommended doing this when the unit is new to eliminate any manufacturing residues ("burn off" period).

Common Issues & Fixes

Decreased Vapor Production

Symptoms: Gradually declining vapor output over time despite consistent technique.

Possible causes and solutions:

  • Dirty screens/glass: Clean all glass components and replace screens.
  • Herb quality variation: The community confirmed that different strains produce different amounts of visible vapor. One experienced member noted: "some strains simply vaporize better than others; more vapor is not necessarily an indication of potency, however, they often correlate." Less visible vapor does not necessarily mean fewer active compounds are being extracted.
  • Insufficient preheat: Allow at least 10 minutes of preheat time.

Uneven Herb Cooking in Cyclone Bowl

Symptoms: Herb at the center of the bowl is fully extracted while material at the edges remains green; herb pushed to sides during fan use.

Cause: The hole in the bottom of the cyclone bowl is smaller than the screen, concentrating hot air in the center.

Solutions:

  • Use the elbow pack method instead
  • Stir frequently between draws
  • Modify the cyclone bowl by carefully enlarging the bottom opening (advanced modification)
  • Pack less herb and create a mound over the center hole

Bag Not Sealing

Symptoms: Vapor escapes from bags; inability to store filled bags.

Solutions:

  • The EQ's bag system does not have the valve/lock mechanism found on the Volcano
  • Community members recommended CamelBak mouthpieces as aftermarket additions that fit the 10mm glass connection, preventing vapor escape
  • Some users fashioned DIY solutions using clips or clamps

Throat/Airway Irritation

Symptoms: Dry throat, irritation, or in extreme cases, blood in mucus after heavy sessions.

Community guidance: The community consistently recommended:

  • Use water filtration (connect EQ to a water pipe via the 18.8mm joint)
  • Take slower, cooler draws
  • Reduce session intensity
  • Consult a medical professional for persistent symptoms — the community was emphatic that users should "go see a doctor" rather than self-diagnose

Accessories & Modifications

DDave Mod Kit

One of the most discussed aftermarket modifications for the Extreme Q. The DDave mod kit included replacement glass pieces — most notably a shorter bowl that positioned herb closer to the heating element for more efficient extraction and better micro-dosing capability. The community frequently recommended the DDave mod as a near-essential upgrade, particularly for users seeking to reduce herb consumption. One community member specifically recommended the EQ with the DDave mod kit together for anyone choosing the EQ on a budget.

Water Pipe Adapter (WPA)

The EQ's 18.8mm male glass joint made it inherently compatible with most standard water pipes. Users connected the unit to bubblers and bongs for water-filtered vapor. This was one of the most praised aspects of the unit, with one user noting that connecting the EQ to glass was the reason their Volcano "has been pretty much gathering dust."

J-VOC (Judsyn Vapor Oil Collector)

A third-party accessory released in 2016, the J-VOC was designed specifically for the Extreme Q. It featured a glass collection chamber and stainless steel cooling coil (adapted from the Storz & Bickel Plenty) to improve vapor smoothness and flavor while collecting reclaim oil. The accessory included bowl adapters based on the elbow bowl design, with glass components hand-made in Canada. Community members raised concerns about top-heaviness and stability; the designer noted that the lightweight cooling coil could be bent to extend outward and that the EQ's weighted base helped maintain stability.

Custom Bowl Modifications

Early community members experimented with custom cyclone bowl designs featuring a screen-holding ring mechanism — a ring positioned between two female glass-on-glass joints to hold the screen securely in place. This addressed issues with screen stability and positioning in the stock bowl.

Volcano Liquid Pads

Community members discovered that Storz & Bickel's Volcano liquid pads could be used in the EQ's cyclone bowl for vaporizing concentrates. One member specifically advised: "Keep your volcano liquid pads, they'll come in handy" when switching from a Volcano to an EQ.

CamelBak Mouthpieces

A widely shared tip: CamelBak hydration pack bite valve mouthpieces fit over the EQ's 10mm glass connections on both the bags and whip, enabling hands-free whip use and preventing vapor escape from filled bags.

Comparisons

vs. Storz & Bickel Volcano (Classic/Digital)

This was the most frequently drawn comparison in the community, as both units offer balloon capability.

  • Bags: The Volcano was generally considered superior for bag delivery, with its valve system, faster pump, and more robust bag attachment. The EQ filled bags at roughly half the speed and lacked a proper sealing valve. However, the community noted that the vapor quality within the bags was comparable — "the vapor in cano bags and EQ bags is same."
  • Versatility: The EQ won decisively with its additional whip capability and water pipe compatibility.
  • Price: The EQ typically cost one-third to one-quarter of the Volcano, making it dramatically more accessible.
  • Concentrates: The EQ reached higher temperatures, giving it an advantage for concentrate use.
  • Build quality/durability: The Volcano was regarded as more durable, particularly due to its lack of fragile glass components. It was considered a more robust, "simplistic" design.
  • Air path: Both units had clean air paths, though the specifics of their designs differed. A knowledgeable community member confirmed the EQ's air "is not heated until it is inside the glass air path and past all circuitry," countering a misconception that it "nukes its internal circuitry with hot air."

Community opinion was divided: some users who owned both preferred the EQ for its versatility and glass compatibility, while purists valued the Volcano's superior bag implementation. Several members sold their Volcanos after acquiring an EQ, while others maintained both served different purposes.

vs. 7th Floor Silver Surfer Vaporizer (SSV)

A frequently discussed comparison, as both are popular mid-range desktop units.

  • Whip performance: The SSV was widely regarded as superior for whip-only use. "The hits are the thickest I have ever had," one experienced member stated. "The E can get thick hits, just not quite as thick as the SSV."
  • Versatility: The EQ offered bags and fan-assisted draw; the SSV was whip-only.
  • Build quality: The SSV was perceived as more robustly built and more resistant to shock.
  • Learning curve: The SSV's dial-based temperature control required more technique to master; the EQ's digital display was considered more beginner-friendly.
  • Recommendation: The community often suggested the SSV for dedicated whip users and the EQ for newcomers or those wanting delivery method options. As one member summarized: "For a vapor noob, I would recommend the E, especially if you don't know what you want in delivery methods."

vs. 7th Floor Da Buddha Vaporizer (DBV)

  • The DBV shared the SSV's heating element and was considered an excellent whip vape at a lower price point. One member noted it "provides more predictable hits, with the same element" as the SSV.
  • For whip-only use, both 7th Floor vapes were preferred over the EQ; the EQ's advantage remained its multi-method versatility.

vs. HerbalAire

  • The HerbalAire was praised for exceptional efficiency and durability ("built like a tank"), with the unique ability to work with unground herb.
  • It offered bag, whip, and water pipe compatibility similarly to the EQ.
  • Community members who valued bags and efficiency often recommended the HerbalAire as an underappreciated alternative, with one calling it "one of the most underrated vapes out there."
  • Reports of original HerbalAire units functioning flawlessly after 10+ years were noted.

vs. VapeXhale Cloud EVO

  • The EVO was positioned as a premium upgrade path for EQ users seeking better flavor and efficiency with smaller loads.
  • The EVO excelled at water pipe use and micro-dosing but lacked bag capability.
  • At roughly double to triple the EQ's price, the EVO served a different market segment.

vs. Log Vapes (Underdog, E-Nano, WoodScents)

  • Log vapes were recommended as an upgrade path for EQ users primarily interested in micro-dosing, efficiency, and water pipe compatibility.
  • They could be left on continuously, offered exceptional efficiency with tiny loads, and produced excellent flavor.
  • They lacked the bag capability and fan-assisted features of the EQ but were regarded as superior for solo on-demand desktop use.
