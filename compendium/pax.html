# Pax (Original / Pax 1)

Overview

The Pax is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Ploom (later Pax Labs), released in 2012. It was one of the most widely recognized portable vaporizers of its era, often drawing comparisons to Apple products for its sleek industrial design and emphasis on aesthetics. The Pax uses a conduction heating method powered by an internal rechargeable lithium-ion battery. It features an anodized aluminum body, a retractable mouthpiece that also serves as the power switch, and a compact form factor that made it one of the most popular choices for users prioritizing discretion and portability.

The Pax gained significant mainstream visibility and was frequently the first vaporizer encountered by newcomers to the dry herb vaporizer community. Within the enthusiast community, however, opinions were more nuanced — the device was respected for its design and stealth capabilities but was often critiqued for its performance relative to its price point, especially when compared to other portables available at the time.

Community Reception

The Pax occupied a distinctive and somewhat polarizing position within the vaporizer community. It was widely acknowledged as one of the most stealthy and discreet portable vaporizers available, and community members consistently cited it as a top recommendation when stealth was the primary concern. As one community member noted in a discussion of stealthy vapes, the Pax was small enough that "if I had a few puffs on that in the street I doubt anyone would give me a second look." Multiple members listed it alongside the MFLB, Pinnacle Pro, and Firefly as one of the top choices for discreet use in public settings.

However, the community widely agreed that the Pax had significant limitations that made it a less-than-ideal choice as a primary or sole vaporizer:

Pros:

  • Exceptional stealth and pocket-friendly form factor
  • Attractive, well-built industrial design
  • Simple operation — no buttons, temperature controlled via mouthpiece position
  • Strong brand recognition and generous 10-year warranty
  • Good battery life relative to other portables of its era

Cons:

  • Conduction heating with a metal air path raised concerns among health-conscious users
  • High maintenance requirements — the vapor path and mouthpiece mechanism required frequent cleaning to function properly
  • Battery life, while decent, meant frequent recharging for heavier users; one community member noted switching back to another vaporizer while "waiting for the Pax to charge"
  • Performance did not match its premium price point when compared to alternatives like the Arizer Solo
  • The oven required a relatively full pack to perform optimally, making it less efficient for light users

A recurring theme in community discussions was the observation that the Pax was not recommended as someone's only vaporizer. One experienced community member stated plainly: "A Pax is okay but I honestly wouldn't want it to be my only vape." Another member who owned both a Pax and an Arizer Solo reported selling the Pax after purchasing the Solo, noting they "realized within a week of quitting smoking that just one unit (that needed to be recharged every two hours) wasn't going to do it." The community generally positioned the Pax as an excellent secondary or on-the-go device rather than a daily driver.

When the Pax 2 was announced in early 2015, the original Pax saw a notable price drop on the secondhand market, with units appearing in community classifieds for around $120, which members considered a significantly better value proposition than the original retail price.

Tips & Techniques

While the source threads covered in this article focused primarily on purchase advice and comparative discussion rather than deep technique exploration, several community-shared practices were widely understood among Pax users:

  • Pack the oven firmly: The Pax's conduction oven performs best when packed snugly (but not so tight as to restrict airflow). A fine-to-medium grind packed firmly ensures good contact between the herb and the oven walls.
  • Sip, don't rip: The Pax responds best to slow, steady draws rather than hard pulls. Long, gentle sips of approximately 10–15 seconds allow the conduction heating to keep up with the draw rate.
  • Stir the oven: Midway through a session, popping open the oven lid and stirring the material helps ensure even extraction, as conduction heating can leave the outer layer more thoroughly vaped than the center.
  • Use the mouthpiece to manage sessions: Removing the mouthpiece (or pressing it down and back up) turns the unit off, allowing users to take a few draws and pocket the device for later without wasting material.
  • Keep it clean: The retractable mouthpiece mechanism is prone to sticking if residue builds up. Preventive cleaning after every few sessions is far easier than remedial cleaning once the mechanism has seized.

Temperature Guide

The original Pax features three preset temperature levels rather than precise temperature control:

SettingApproximate TemperatureRecommended Use
Low~370°F / 188°CBest for flavor-forward sessions; lighter vapor production; ideal for starting a session
Medium~390°F / 199°CBalanced setting; good vapor production with reasonable flavor; the most commonly used setting for general sessions
High~410°F / 210°CMaximum extraction; thicker vapor; best for finishing off a bowl or when prioritizing efficiency over flavor

A common community technique was to start on low and step up through the temperature settings over the course of a session, beginning with low for the flavorful initial draws and finishing on high to fully extract the material.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Maintenance was one of the most discussed aspects of Pax ownership within the community, and the device was widely regarded as high-maintenance compared to competitors. The retractable mouthpiece mechanism in particular was a frequent pain point.

Recommended cleaning routine:

Mouthpiece mechanism: Remove the mouthpiece and clean the channel with a pipe cleaner dipped in isopropyl alcohol (90% or higher). This should be done every 3–5 sessions to prevent the spring-loaded mechanism from sticking.

Vapor path: Run a pipe cleaner soaked in isopropyl alcohol through the internal vapor tube from the mouthpiece end down to the oven.

Oven: Use a brush or cotton swab with isopropyl alcohol to clean residue from the oven walls and screen. Replace the oven screen periodically if it becomes clogged.

Exterior: Wipe down the anodized aluminum body with a damp cloth or isopropyl alcohol wipe.

Mouthpiece lubrication: After cleaning, applying a tiny amount of food-grade lubricant (such as propylene glycol or the lubricant included in the Pax cleaning kit) to the mouthpiece mechanism helps maintain smooth retraction.

Frequency: The community generally recommended cleaning every 5–10 sessions for regular users, with more frequent attention to the mouthpiece channel. Neglecting cleaning was the single most common cause of user complaints and device malfunction.

Accessories & Modifications

The original Pax had a relatively limited accessory ecosystem compared to later models, but several items were commonly discussed:

  • Pax Cleaning Kit: Included pipe cleaners, isopropyl alcohol, and mouthpiece lubricant; considered essential by the community.
  • Replacement screens: Oven screens were a consumable item that required periodic replacement.
  • Aftermarket oven lids with vent modifications: Some users experimented with modified oven lids to improve airflow.
  • Carrying cases and holsters: Various third-party cases were available to protect the anodized finish.
  • Water pipe adapters (WPA): Aftermarket WPAs were available from third-party sellers, allowing the Pax to be used with a water piece for cooler, smoother vapor.
  • Pusher/screen inserts: Some users employed small screens or spacers to reduce the effective oven size for smaller loads, as the Pax performed poorly with a half-packed oven.

It is worth noting that the Pax's proprietary design limited the range of available modifications compared to more modular vaporizers.

Comparisons

The Pax was most frequently compared to the following devices in community discussions:

Pax vs. Arizer Solo

This was the most common comparison in purchase advice threads and represented a fundamental debate about stealth vs. performance. The community broadly favored the Arizer Solo for vapor quality, efficiency, ease of cleaning, and value. The Solo could be purchased for significantly less ($130–$160 at the time) and was considered a superior performer. However, the Solo's glass stem and larger cylindrical body made it far less discreet. As one member illustrated: pulling out a Solo with its glass mouthpiece in public would invite "a few weird looks," while the Pax could be used inconspicuously on a city street. Members who valued vapor quality and home use overwhelmingly recommended the Solo; those who needed true pocket portability and stealth leaned toward the Pax. Multiple community members who owned both ultimately sold the Pax in favor of the Solo.

Pax vs. MFLB (Magic-Flight Launch Box)

Both were frequently listed among the top stealth vaporizers. The MFLB was smaller and even more discreet, and had been used successfully by community members in airports and transit terminals. However, the MFLB required significant technique to use effectively and was generally "only recommended if you have prior experience with vaporizers." The Pax was considered more approachable for beginners.

Pax vs. Firefly

Both were listed as top-tier stealth options, though the Firefly offered convection heating (preferred by many enthusiasts for flavor) compared to the Pax's conduction method.

Pax vs. Fury 2

In later discussions (circa 2019), users seeking compact, stealthy vaporizers referenced the Pax's size as a benchmark, asking whether devices offering "Pax size, but better" were in development. The Fury 2 was considered by some to offer better performance in a similarly compact form, though the Pax still held its own in terms of recognizable form factor and pocketability.

Pax vs. Flowermate

Both were mentioned in budget-conscious discussions. The Flowermate was seen as a more affordable pocket-friendly alternative, particularly for users seeking a temporary or secondary portable. When the original Pax dropped in price on the secondhand market, the value gap narrowed.

Pax vs. Desktop Vaporizers (Da Buddha, etc.)

One notable thread highlighted a common misconception among newcomers who conflated the Pax (a true portable) with desktop units like Da Buddha. The community was quick to clarify that desktop vaporizers offered vastly superior performance for home use and recommended pairing a quality desktop unit with an inexpensive portable rather than relying solely on a Pax.
