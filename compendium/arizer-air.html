# Arizer Air

Overview

The Arizer Air is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. Released in late 2014, the Air was designed as a more portable, battery-swappable evolution of the company's popular Arizer Solo. It uses a hybrid conduction/convection heating method, with a ceramic heating element surrounded by a stainless steel oven that heats a borosilicate glass stem containing the herb material. The vapor path is all-glass, a design feature that became a hallmark of Arizer's portable lineup.

The Air is powered by a single user-replaceable 18650 lithium-ion battery, which was a significant differentiator at the time of release. It was widely regarded within the vaporizer community as one of the most reliable portable vaporizers on the market, earning a reputation as a dependable "daily driver" that could serve users well for years. One community member noted using their Air as a daily driver for over three years without issue, and the device was frequently recommended to newcomers transitioning from combustion to vaporization.

Community Reception

The Arizer Air was overwhelmingly well-received within the vaporizer community, consistently ranked among the most recommended portable session vaporizers in its price range. Its combination of reliability, simplicity, vapor quality, and affordability made it a perennial favorite.

Pros frequently cited by the community:

  • Exceptional reliability and durability. The Air was widely described as one of the most reliable portables on the market. Multiple community members reported years of daily use with minimal degradation. One member described it as among "one of the most reliable portables on the market already in your hand."
  • Pure, clean vapor quality. The all-glass vapor path was consistently praised for delivering excellent flavor. Community members noted the glass stems cooled vapor effectively and provided a superior taste experience compared to devices with plastic or metal air paths.
  • Removable battery. The ability to swap 18650 batteries was a major selling point, allowing users to carry spares for extended sessions away from a charger. This made it particularly attractive for outdoor use, hiking, and travel.
  • Efficiency with small loads. The Air was noted to work well with small amounts of herb. Community consensus held that it performed admirably even with partial stem packs, making it suitable for microdosing and conservation-minded users.
  • Glass stem system. The interchangeable glass stems could be pre-packed and capped for convenient on-the-go use, essentially functioning as portable, pre-loaded dose capsules.
  • Ease of sharing. One community member reported particular success using the Air to convert smoking friends to vaporization, noting that "whenever I went through my collection of vaporizers with friends who were willing to quit, then they all pointed their fingers on the Air if asked which one they liked best and would like to take with them." The slightly warm, dense vapor was noted to appeal to ex-smokers in a way that some smoother-hitting vaporizers did not.
  • Low maintenance. The glass stems are easy to clean, and the overall device requires minimal upkeep.
  • Value. At its price point (generally around $150 USD at retail), it was considered an outstanding value proposition.

Cons and limitations noted by the community:

  • Session-style only. The Air is a session vaporizer with a 10-minute auto-shutoff timer. Users who preferred a "hit-and-quit" or on-demand style found the Air less suitable. One user specifically decided to return their Air in favor of an on-demand unit because they preferred to "rip my pipe once or twice, and then put it down for ten or fifteen minutes."
  • Glass stem fragility and portability concerns. Carrying glass stems in a pocket was a recurring complaint. Multiple users cited this as a drawback for truly portable use, particularly during outdoor activities like hiking. Some users migrated to other devices specifically because they wanted an all-in-one unit without loose glass parts.
  • Relatively slow heat-up for actual vapor production. While the device reaches its set temperature within about 60–90 seconds, community members widely noted that the temperature indicator refers to the oven and not the glass stem. Actual vapor production may take an additional minute or two as the herb in the glass stem comes to temperature.
  • Proprietary charging port. The pin-style charging port was a known weak point. Multiple community members reported charger failures and issues with the charging pin on the unit itself. This was often cited as the most common hardware complaint.
  • Battery life for heavy users. While adequate for moderate use, heavy users (6–10 sessions per day) found a single battery insufficient and needed to carry and rotate multiple batteries.
  • Preset temperature steps. Unlike the Air II, the original Air offered only five preset temperature levels without the ability to dial in a specific degree. While most users found the presets adequate, some preferred more granular control.

Tips & Techniques

Loading the Stem

  • Grind consistency matters. A medium-fine grind was generally recommended. Too fine a grind can restrict airflow; too coarse reduces surface area and extraction efficiency.
  • Pack loosely. The community emphasized not overpacking the stem. A light tamp or simply pressing the stem into a pile of ground herb ("scoop and tap") was the preferred loading technique. Overpacking restricts airflow and reduces vapor quality.
  • Small loads work well. The Air is efficient with partial stem packs. Users found they could get satisfying results with as little as 0.05–0.1g, making it suitable for conservation and microdosing.
  • Pre-pack multiple stems. A widely adopted technique was to pre-pack several glass stems and cap them with the silicone caps included by Arizer (or aftermarket caps). This allowed for quick, mess-free reloading on the go, functioning similarly to dose capsule systems in other vaporizers. One community member described the workflow: "I'll prepack a few stems and cover them with the caps. Cleaning them out is as simple as blowing them out and recapping them."

Draw Technique

  • Slow, steady draws. The Air performs best with a slow, controlled sipping draw rather than a hard pull. Hard draws cool the oven and reduce vapor density. The session style of the device rewards patient, relaxed inhalation.
  • Wait for actual vapor. After the device indicates it has reached temperature, allow an additional 30–60 seconds before drawing for best vapor production, as the herb in the glass stem heats more slowly than the oven itself.

Reclaim Collection

  • Cotton in the stem. A community-discovered technique involves placing a small piece of organic cotton into the glass stem mouthpiece end. Over multiple sessions, the cotton collects cannabinoid-rich condensate (reclaim). The saturated cotton can then be vaped for a potent session. One experienced member advised: "If you stick a little piece of cotton in the stem and vape through it, it will filter out small particulate matter and eventually the cotton will be saturated with reclaim. Then vape the cotton piece for a nice treat!"
  • Swabbing reclaim. Alternatively, once the stem has accumulated visible reclaim on the inner walls, a piece of organic cotton can be used to swab it out. Slightly warming the stem first helps the reclaim release more easily.

Travel Tips

  • Dispose of glass before crossing borders. At least one community member reported disposing of glass stems and accessories before crossing international borders, keeping only the clean unit body for return travel.
  • Airtight storage. The Air and its used stems produce a noticeable earthy aroma. The community recommended storing the device in airtight containers, with popular suggestions including Tightvac containers, Ryot smell-proof cases, repurposed thermos flasks with O-ring seals, or a combination of a Tightvac inside a roll-top dry bag for maximum odor containment.

Temperature Guide

The Arizer Air uses five color-coded preset temperature levels:

LevelColorTemperatureRecommended Use
1Blue356°F / 180°CFlavor exploration; light, terpy hits with minimal visible vapor. Best for tasting a fresh bowl.
2White374°F / 190°CGood balance of flavor and mild effects. Suitable for low-temperature flavor chasers.
3Green392°F / 200°COften cited as the "sweet spot" for many users. Good vapor density with retained flavor. A popular starting point for most sessions.
4Orange410°F / 210°CHeavier effects, thicker clouds, more body sensation. Good for the middle-to-end of a session.
5Red428°F / 220°CMaximum extraction. Dense, warm vapor. Best for finishing a bowl or users seeking the strongest effects. Approaches but does not reach combustion temperatures.

Community-agreed sweet spots: Many users adopted a "temperature stepping" approach, starting at Green (392°F/200°C) for flavor and gradually increasing through Orange and Red over the course of a session to fully extract the material. Some users who transitioned from smoking and preferred a warmer, denser hit gravitated toward Orange and Red settings from the outset.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Stems

  • Frequency: Clean stems when visible reclaim buildup becomes apparent, or approximately every 5–10 sessions depending on use intensity.
  • Method: Soak glass stems in isopropyl alcohol (ISO), ideally 90% or higher concentration. A short soak (15–30 minutes) followed by a rinse with warm water is typically sufficient. For stubborn buildup, overnight soaking or gentle use of pipe cleaners may be needed.
  • Reclaim harvesting: Some users intentionally allowed reclaim to accumulate before cleaning, then either collected it with cotton or dissolved it in a warm beverage (the "stem milk" technique involving simmering the stem in milk or a fatty liquid to extract the cannabinoids before drinking).

Oven

  • The stainless steel oven occasionally accumulates residue. A cotton swab dipped in isopropyl alcohol can be used to clean the interior. Allow the oven to dry completely before use.

Exterior

  • The silicone sleeve (if used) can be removed and washed with soap and water.
  • The device body can be wiped down with a slightly damp cloth. Avoid getting moisture into the charging port or button mechanism.

General Advice

  • Burn-off cycles for new units. The community recommended running several heat cycles at the highest temperature setting with an empty stem before first use, to eliminate any manufacturing residues. While some online discussions referenced concerns about plastic or glue smells, the vaporizer community widely noted these concerns were largely unfounded for genuine Arizer units. As one member explained, any adhesive used in the oven area is a heat-safe cement on the exterior, and a few burn-off cycles resolve any faint "new device" smell.
  • Buy from authorized retailers. The community emphasized purchasing from authorized dealers to avoid counterfeit units, which were known to circulate and could present genuine safety concerns.

Common Issues & Fixes

Charger and Charging Port Failures

The most commonly reported hardware issue. The proprietary pin-style charger was a weak point, with multiple users experiencing charger breakdowns or intermittent charging issues.

  • Fix: Purchase a quality external 18650 battery charger. The community recommended the Xtar VP2 as an excellent option, with the Nitecore D2 as another popular choice. Charging at lower amperage (0.25A or 0.5A) was advised to extend battery lifespan and reduce heat during charging. One community member specifically recommended: "Charge at lowest setting, 0.25A — the less heat the better."
  • Avoid leaving batteries on the charger overnight, as this can degrade battery health over time.

Toggle Switch Wear

With extended daily use over several years, some users noticed wear on the physical toggle switch. This was generally a cosmetic or minor functional issue and was cited as the most visible sign of aging on the device.

Battery Degradation

As with any device using lithium-ion cells, battery capacity diminishes over time with regular charge/discharge cycles.

  • Fix: Replace the 18650 battery. The removable battery design was specifically praised for making this a simple, low-cost maintenance task rather than a device-ending issue.

Slow Actual Heat-Up vs. Indicated Temperature

The temperature display (LED color) indicates oven temperature, not stem/herb temperature. Users new to the Air sometimes expected immediate vapor production upon reaching the set temperature.

  • Fix: Allow 30–60 additional seconds after the LED indicates temperature is reached before drawing. This is normal behavior, not a defect.

Air II Screen Failure

For users considering the Air II as an upgrade, at least one community member reported display/screen failures on multiple Air II units, which may warrant consideration.

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems and Mouthpieces

The Air's glass stem system was a core part of its appeal, and a robust aftermarket ecosystem developed:

  • OEM Stems: Arizer offered long and short stems with delrin (plastic) mouthpieces, as well as all-glass stems in various lengths (60mm, 70mm).
  • Bent Stems: Arizer Solo bent glass stems were compatible with the Air and preferred by some users for ergonomic reasons.
  • PVHES (Planet Vape High Efficiency Stem): An aftermarket stem with a modified bowl design aimed at improving airflow and efficiency. The PVHES Vortex variant was also available and valued by the community. These were actively sought after on the secondary market.
  • Silicone Caps: Used to seal pre-packed stems for portable use.
  • Aroma Dish/Oil Diffuser: An Arizer accessory for aromatherapy use, compatible with both Solo and Air.

Glass-on-Glass (GonG) Adapters

GonG adapters allowed the Air to connect directly to water pipes, and were among the most popular accessories:

  • 14mm GonG: The most common size. Available from Arizer (as Namaste-branded stems), Planet Vape (PVHEGonG), and various third-party glass artisans.
  • 18mm GonG: Also available for use with larger water pipe joints.
  • Pinnacle Water Tool (PNWT): A small, portable bubbler that paired well with the Air via a 14mm GonG adapter. Chinese-made clones were also popular as an affordable alternative.
  • F-Bomb Hydratube: A compact water filtration attachment that was used with the Air via GonG adapters, providing portable water-filtered vapor.

Water Pipe Use

The Air was widely praised for its compatibility with water filtration. A community member specifically recommended the "Arizer Air + 14mm GonG adapter" combination as one of the best portable-to-water setups available, noting it paired more easily with water tools than some higher-end portables like the Mighty or Crafty.

External Battery Chargers

Given the proprietary charger's reputation for failure, an external 18650 charger was considered almost a must-have accessory:

  • Xtar VP2: Community favorite for its 0.25A low-current charging option.
  • Nitecore D2: Another well-regarded option, though some users noted uneven charging times between its two slots.
  • Extra 18650 batteries: Carrying one or two spare batteries was standard practice for users planning extended sessions away from a charger.

Silicone Sleeves

Available in various colors, these protective sleeves added grip, minor drop protection, and helped with heat management during extended sessions.

Storage Solutions

  • Ryot Cases: Smell-proof zippered cases available in multiple sizes, recommended for discrete portable carry.
  • Tightvac Containers: Vacuum-sealed containers for odor-free home storage.
  • Repurposed containers: Community members suggested thermos flasks, kitchen containers with O-ring seals, and contact lens holders (for carrying pre-ground herb on hikes).

Comparisons

The Arizer Air was frequently compared to other portable vaporizers in community discussions. Below is a summary of the most commonly discussed comparisons.

Arizer Air vs. Arizer Solo

The Solo was the Air's direct predecessor and shared the same glass stem system and heating design philosophy. The Air was generally considered a more portable version of the Solo, with the key differentiator being the Air's removable 18650 battery versus the Solo's larger internal battery. The Solo was noted to have longer battery life per charge (upwards of 60–90 minutes, with some users claiming up to 2 hours) and was sometimes seen as slightly more powerful in vapor output, but the Air's portability and battery-swap capability made it the preferred choice for on-the-go use. Some community members recommended selling a Solo and buying an Air for portable use, then pairing the Air with a desktop (such as a log vape) for home use.

Arizer Air vs. Arizer Air II

The Air II added digital temperature control (allowing degree-by-degree adjustment rather than five presets), a display screen, and reportedly improved heating technology with better heat isolation. Community members noted the Air II heated up quicker and was "a bit more powerful" overall. The digital temperature control was appreciated for enabling very low temperature flavor sessions. However, several users questioned whether the Air II justified its approximately $100 price premium over the original, particularly since the original Air's five presets were considered adequate by most. At least one user reported screen failures on multiple Air II units, and one side-by-side comparison noted the Air II actually took longer to produce vapor (as opposed to reaching the indicated temperature), possibly due to higher airflow in the updated design. Community sentiment was mixed: the Air II was generally considered an improvement, but the original Air was seen as the better value.

Arizer Air vs. ArGo (Arizer Go)

The ArGo was Arizer's most pocket-friendly model, featuring a retractable stem that stowed inside the unit. For users prioritizing pocket portability, the ArGo was the clear winner. However, the Air (and Air II) were generally preferred for overall performance and session quality when pocket-ability was less critical.

Arizer Air vs. Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

This was a common comparison for budget-conscious buyers. Community consensus strongly favored the Air over the MFLB for most use cases. The MFLB's manual technique requirement, inconsistent performance with degraded batteries, and lower vapor output made it less satisfying for users who had experience with other vaporizers. The MFLB was praised for its lifetime warranty and extreme portability/stealth, but one member summarized the consensus: "If you have used other vaporizers, the MFLB will not satisfy what you are looking for in a portable." The Air was considered far easier to share with friends and more forgiving of technique.

Arizer Air vs. Grasshopper

This comparison highlighted the fundamental difference between session and on-demand vaping styles. The Grasshopper offered near-instant heat-up and on-demand hits, making it better suited to users who preferred one or two quick draws rather than extended sessions. However, the Grasshopper's severe quality control issues and shipping delays (particularly during 2015–2016) led community members to recommend the Air as the safer, more reliable choice. As one member noted: "A future vape that may or may not work is less good than one of the most reliable portables on the market already in your hand."

Arizer Air vs. Boundless CFX

Both were similarly priced portables. Community feedback was nuanced: the CFX offered faster heat-up times and an all-in-one design without loose glass parts, but multiple users found the Air provided better vapor production, better flavor (attributed to the all-glass air path), and greater efficiency with smaller loads. One member stated they "happen to get better vapor production from my Air and prefer the glass air path it provides. Also feel less flower goes a lot longer in my Air during a session." The CFX was noted to perform better through water tools, while the Air was considered superior for native (non-water) use. Neither was considered a clear upgrade over the other — rather, they were viewed as different options at a similar price point.

Arizer Air vs. Crafty / Mighty

The Storz & Bickel Crafty and Mighty were frequently cited as the clear upgrade path from the Air for users seeking more powerful portable performance. The Mighty in particular was described as providing "rips almost comparable to desktops." However, both cost significantly more than the Air, and the Air's glass air path, removable battery, and overall simplicity kept it competitive for users who valued those attributes.

Arizer Air vs. DynaVap VapCap

The VapCap was frequently recommended alongside or as a complement to the Air, particularly for users transitioning from smoking. The VapCap's butane-powered, on-demand heating and cigarette-like form factor made it more similar to the smoking ritual, while the Air provided a more relaxed, session-style experience. Several community discussions suggested owning both: the VapCap for quick, on-demand hits and outdoor use without battery dependence, and the Air for longer, more leisurely sessions.

Arizer Air vs. Fury 2 / Fury Edge

Later-generation compact portables like the Fury 2 and Fury Edge were sometimes recommended as upgrades from the Air, offering faster heat-up, more precise temperature control, and a more pocket-friendly all-in-one design. One experienced community member recommended the Fury Edge as "a great upgrade from Arizer portables." However, the Air's removable battery, proven long-term reliability, and glass vapor path kept it relevant even as newer competitors emerged.
