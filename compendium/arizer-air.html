# Arizer Air

Overview

The Arizer Air is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. Released in late 2014 as a companion to the popular Arizer Solo, the Air was designed to bring similar vapor quality in a more compact, portable-friendly form factor. Its defining feature at launch was a user-replaceable 18650 battery—a significant advantage over most competing portables of the era, which relied on sealed internal batteries.

The Air uses a hybrid heating method, combining conduction from the stainless steel oven with convection as air is drawn through the glass stem and across the heated herb. The vapor path is primarily glass, which the community widely praised for delivering clean, flavorful vapor. Power is supplied by a single removable 18650 lithium-ion battery, and the device can also be used while charging via its included DC charger (pass-through charging).

The Arizer Air was regarded by the vaporizer community as one of the most reliable and approachable portable session vaporizers in its price class, frequently recommended to newcomers transitioning from combustion.

Community Reception

The Arizer Air earned a strong reputation within the community as a dependable, low-maintenance session vaporizer. It was frequently described as "one of the most reliable portables on the market," and its combination of glass vapor path, replaceable battery, and reasonable price point made it a perennial recommendation for both beginners and experienced users seeking a straightforward portable.

Praised Qualities:

  • Reliability and durability. Multiple community members reported using the Air as a daily driver for three or more years without significant issues. One member noted it was their third Arizer product and all had been "sturdy."
  • Vapor quality and flavor. The all-glass stem was widely credited with delivering clean, pure-tasting vapor. Community members noted the stems cooled the vapor effectively, producing smooth draws.
  • Efficiency with small amounts. The Air was frequently praised for performing well with small loads, making it suitable for microdosing or conservation-minded users.
  • Removable battery. The user-replaceable 18650 battery was considered a major advantage, allowing users to carry spare batteries for extended sessions and to replace degraded cells without servicing the unit.
  • Simplicity of use. The five-preset temperature system and single-button operation made the device extremely accessible. One community member noted having "most success in converting smoking friends with the Arizer Air," observing that friends consistently chose it as their preferred device when presented with a collection of vaporizers.
  • Glass stem system. The ability to pre-pack multiple stems with herb and cap them for portable use was a frequently cited convenience feature.

Criticisms and Limitations:

  • Session-only vaping style. The Air is a session vaporizer with a 10-minute auto-shutoff timer, making it poorly suited for users who prefer to take one or two quick hits and set the device down. Community members who described their usage pattern as "rip once or twice and put it down for ten or fifteen minutes" were generally steered toward on-demand alternatives.
  • Glass stems and portability. While the glass vapor path was praised for flavor, carrying glass stems in a pocket was seen as a practical concern for active outdoor use. One member specifically noted giving away their Air in favor of something more pocket-friendly for hiking, citing the inconvenience of "carrying glass in your pocket."
  • Heat-up time. While the device reaches its set temperature indicator relatively quickly, the community noted that the oven temperature indicator does not reflect the actual temperature at the herb. Effective vaporization often required additional waiting time beyond what the LED indicators suggested.
  • Included charger quality. The stock DC barrel charger received mixed reviews. At least one community member reported the charger eventually breaking, noting it "would not hold a charge and had issues charging while in use," and described the charging pin design as problematic.
  • Not a desktop replacement. While efficient for a portable, the community acknowledged that log vaporizers and other desktops outperformed the Air in terms of extraction power and efficiency at very small loads.

Tips & Techniques

Loading the Stem

The community developed several best practices for loading the Air's glass stems:

  • Grind medium-fine. A medium-fine grind was generally recommended for optimal airflow and extraction. Some users reported good results with the Magic Flight Finishing Grinder for an extra-fine grind, though this was more a matter of preference.
  • Pack lightly. The community consensus was that a loose to moderate pack produced the best results. Overpacking restricts airflow and reduces vapor quality. Small amounts (0.05–0.1g) were reported to work well, making the Air suitable for conservation.
  • Pre-pack stems for portability. A popular technique was to pre-load several stems with herb and cap them with the included silicone caps. This allowed quick stem swaps on the go without needing to handle loose herb. One member described the routine: "I'll prepack a few stems and cover them with the caps. Cleaning them out is as simple as blowing them out and recapping them."

Draw Technique

  • Slow, steady draws. The Air responds best to slow, consistent sipping rather than hard pulls. Drawing too aggressively cools the oven and reduces vapor production.
  • Wait beyond the indicator. Experienced users learned to wait an additional 30–60 seconds after the temperature indicator shows the set point has been reached. The LED indicates oven temperature, not the temperature of the herb in the glass stem. Starting draws too early results in thin, unsatisfying vapor.

Converting Smokers

The Air was noted as particularly effective for helping combustion users transition to vaporizing. One community member shared: "Whenever I went through my collection of vaporizers with friends who were willing to quit, they all pointed their fingers on the Air if asked which one they liked best." The device's slightly warm, moderately dense vapor was seen as a good middle ground that satisfied ex-smokers without being too harsh or too light.

Reclaim Collection

A community-developed technique involved placing a small piece of organic cotton inside the glass stem to filter particulate matter and collect condensed cannabinoid reclaim. Over multiple sessions, the cotton becomes saturated with active compounds and can then be vaporized for a potent experience. Alternatively, once a stem has built up visible reclaim on the inner glass walls, a small piece of cotton can be used to swab it out (slightly warming the stem first helps loosen the reclaim).

Temperature Guide

The Arizer Air uses five color-coded preset temperatures:

SettingColorTemperatureRecommended Use
1Blue180°C / 356°FFlavor-focused sessions; light, wispy vapor with excellent terpene expression. Best for low-temperature flavor chasers.
2White190°C / 374°FGood balance of flavor and moderate vapor production. A popular starting point.
3Green200°C / 392°FCommunity-favored "sweet spot" for balanced sessions combining flavor, visible vapor, and good extraction.
4Orange210°C / 428°FThicker clouds with more complete extraction. Heavier effects.
5Red220°C / 446°FMaximum extraction. Dense vapor with diminished flavor. Useful for finishing bowls or for users seeking heavy effects.

Community-agreed sweet spots: Many users reported starting sessions at White (190°C) or Green (200°C) for flavor, then stepping up to Orange (210°C) or Red (220°C) to finish extracting the bowl. This "temperature stepping" technique was one of the most commonly recommended approaches.

Users transitioning from smoking who wanted visible clouds and a throat sensation were typically directed to start at Green or Orange settings, as the lower temperatures may produce vapor too light to satisfy former combustion users.

Maintenance & Cleaning

The Air's glass vapor path made maintenance relatively straightforward compared to vaporizers with complex internal air paths.

Glass Stems

  • Regular cleaning with isopropyl alcohol (ISO). Soaking dirty stems in 90%+ isopropyl alcohol was the standard cleaning method. For routine maintenance, a quick soak of 10–30 minutes followed by a rinse with hot water was typically sufficient.
  • Reclaim harvesting before cleaning. Many community members chose to collect the honey-colored condensate (reclaim) from dirty stems before cleaning, either by pushing it out with a cotton swab or by simmering the stem in milk or a fatty liquid to extract the active compounds.
  • Frequency. Cleaning frequency varied by usage. Light users might clean stems every week or two; heavy daily users benefited from more frequent cleaning to maintain optimal airflow and flavor.

Oven

  • Brush out ABV (Already Been Vaped) material after each session using the included brush or a small, stiff-bristled brush. Keeping the oven free of debris ensures consistent heating and airflow.
  • Occasional ISO wipe. The oven can be wiped with an ISO-dampened cotton swab periodically to remove any residue buildup. Ensure the unit is off and cool before cleaning.

Burn-Off for New Units

The community recommended performing several burn-off cycles at the highest temperature setting before first use. This practice helps eliminate any manufacturing residues. One member noted that while the Air had little to no noticeable "new vape" smell compared to some competitors, burn-offs were still considered good practice for any new vaporizer.

Counterfeit Warning

The community raised awareness about counterfeit Arizer products in circulation. Purchasing from authorized dealers and verifying serial numbers through Arizer's support were recommended precautions. One member specifically cautioned: "Beware there are counterfeits out there."

Common Issues & Fixes

Included Charger Failure

The stock DC barrel charger was the most commonly reported point of failure. Symptoms included inability to hold charge, intermittent charging, and eventual complete failure.

Fix: Purchase an external 18650 battery charger. The community strongly recommended dedicated chargers such as the Xtar VP2, Nitecore D2, or Xtar SV2 Rocket. These provide more reliable charging with selectable charge rates. A charge rate of 0.25A–0.5A was recommended for battery longevity, as lower charging rates generate less heat and are gentler on the cells. The 1A fast-charge option available on some chargers, while functional, was considered less ideal for long-term battery health.

Battery Degradation

Over extended use, the stock 18650 battery loses capacity, resulting in shorter session times.

Fix: Replace with a fresh high-quality 18650 cell. The Air's removable battery design made this a simple swap rather than a warranty issue. Carrying spare batteries was recommended for heavy users.

Toggle Switch Wear

The physical power/temperature toggle switch was reported to show wear over extended daily use (multiple years).

Fix: This was primarily a cosmetic/tactile concern rather than a functional failure in most reported cases. Users who experienced this typically continued using the device or upgraded to the Air II.

Slow Effective Heat-Up

Some users, particularly those coming from the original Air to the Air II, reported that the device takes longer than expected to produce satisfying vapor, even after the temperature indicator shows the set point has been reached.

Fix: Allow additional time (30–60 seconds) after the indicator shows the target temperature. Begin with slow, gentle draws. The temperature reading reflects the oven, not the herb in the stem. One experienced user noted this is characteristic of how the Air's heating system works and not a defect.

Concerns About Plastic Smell / Adhesives

Some online reports (primarily from external forums) raised concerns about plastic or adhesive smells from the Solo/Air. The community largely dismissed these concerns as either related to counterfeit units, normal manufacturing residue eliminated by burn-off cycles, or confusion about the heat-safe cement used on the exterior of the oven, which does not contact the air path.

Community response: One member noted: "I do not recall there being any glue or even 'new vape' smell." Another confirmed: "The 'glue' that was referred to is on the outside of the oven. It is a safe heatproof cement."

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems and GonG Adapters

The aftermarket glass ecosystem was one of the Air's greatest strengths. Compatible accessories included:

  • OEM stems: Short and long glass "aroma tubes" with delrin mouthpieces, and all-glass variants in various lengths (60mm, 70mm).
  • Bent stems: Solo-compatible bent glass stems that allowed more comfortable ergonomic use.
  • Planet Vape High Efficiency Stems (PVHES): Aftermarket stems with modified bowl designs for improved airflow and extraction. The PVHES Vortex was a particularly sought-after variant.
  • Glass-on-Glass (GonG) adapters: Available in 14mm and 18mm joint sizes, these allowed the Air to connect directly to water pipes and bubblers. Sources included Planet Vape (PVHES GonG), Namaste (short and long 14mm GonG), and various third-party glassblowers.
  • F-Bomb hydratube: A dedicated small water filtration attachment that could be used with the Air via a GonG adapter.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

Pairing the Air with water filtration was a popular modification. The community recommended:

  • 14mm GonG adapter as the most common and versatile option.
  • Pinnacle Water Tool (PNWT) or similar compact bubblers for a portable water-filtered setup.
  • The Air + 14mm GonG combination was specifically cited as one of the easier portable setups for water filtration, alongside the Grasshopper.

Silicone Sleeves

Protective silicone skins/sleeves were available in various colors, providing grip, drop protection, and a degree of thermal insulation.

Aroma Dish / Oil Diffuser

Arizer offered an aromatherapy cup/oil diffuser accessory compatible with both the Solo and Air, for use with essential oils or concentrates (though the device was primarily designed for dry herb).

Storage Solutions

For smell-proof storage, the community recommended:

  • Tightvac containers for herb and accessories.
  • Ryot cases (smell-proof, available in multiple sizes).
  • Repurposed kitchen containers with screw lids and silicone gaskets.
  • Rolltop dry bags layered over a sealed container for maximum odor control.

External Battery Chargers

As noted in troubleshooting, the community strongly recommended upgrading to a quality external 18650 charger. The Xtar VP2 was the most frequently recommended model, praised for its 0.25A low-charge option and reliability at its price point.

Comparisons

Arizer Air vs. Arizer Solo

The Air was essentially designed as a more portable version of the Solo, sharing the same glass stem system and similar heating approach. The Solo offered slightly longer battery life due to its larger internal battery but lacked the Air's removable battery. The community generally recommended the Solo for home use and the Air for portability, though both were considered excellent session vaporizers. One member described their transition: "Sell the Solo for the Air (better portable) and then buy a log [vaporizer] down the road."

Arizer Air vs. Arizer Air II

The Air II introduced digital temperature control (precise degree-by-degree selection rather than five presets), an OLED display, and reportedly improved heating technology with better oven isolation. The community debated whether the ~$100 price premium was justified. Key differences noted:

  • Digital temperature control was considered "extremely nice" for accessing very low temperatures for flavor-focused sessions, but not essential for most users.
  • Faster heat-up was expected from the Air II, though at least one member reported the opposite in practice—the Air II taking longer to produce satisfying vapor despite reaching its displayed temperature quickly. Higher airflow in the Air II was suspected as a possible factor.
  • Durability concerns were raised by some members, with at least one reporting screen failures on two Air II units. The original Air's simpler construction (LED indicators, physical toggle) was seen as potentially more long-term durable.
  • The community consensus leaned toward recommending the Air II if buying new, but considered the original Air an excellent value if available at a lower price.

Arizer Air vs. ArGo

The Arizer ArGo was discussed as the most portable option in the Arizer lineup, with a retractable stem design that eliminated the need to carry exposed glass. For users prioritizing pocket-friendliness, the ArGo was the recommended choice, though at a higher price point.

Arizer Air vs. Grasshopper

This was a frequently debated comparison, centering on the fundamental difference between session and on-demand vaping styles. The Grasshopper offered near-instant heat-up and on-demand single hits, which better suited users who preferred to "rip once or twice and put it down." However, the Grasshopper was plagued by well-documented quality control and reliability issues during the period of most discussions. The community generally concluded that the Air was the safer, more reliable choice—"a future vape that may or may not work is less good than one of the most reliable portables on the market already in your hand"—while acknowledging the Grasshopper's theoretical advantages for on-demand use cases.

Arizer Air vs. Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

The MFLB offered a smaller form factor, manual (technique-dependent) operation, and a lifetime warranty. The Air offered more consistent, easier sessions that were better suited for sharing with friends. The community consensus strongly favored the Air for users who had experience with other vaporizers, with one member stating: "If you have used other vaporizers, the MFLB will not satisfy what you are looking for in a portable." The MFLB was acknowledged as durable and backed by an excellent warranty, but the Air was seen as the superior performer overall.

Arizer Air vs. Boundless CFX

The CFX offered faster heat-up time and an all-in-one design without glass parts, while the Air was praised for its glass air path and efficiency. One experienced owner of both noted: "I happen to get better vapor production from my Air and prefer the glass air path it provides. Also feel less flower goes a lot longer in my Air during a session." The CFX was considered a lateral move rather than an upgrade, described as "another option with a similar price point." For a true performance upgrade from the Air, the community frequently pointed toward the Storz & Bickel Mighty.

Arizer Air vs. Crafty / Mighty

The Storz & Bickel Crafty and Mighty were positioned at a significantly higher price point and were generally considered superior in raw performance, with one member noting the Mighty "provides rips almost comparable to my desktops." However, the Air's lower cost, glass vapor path, and replaceable battery kept it competitive as a value proposition.

Arizer Air vs. Fury 2 / Fury Edge

Later discussions saw the Fury Edge recommended as a potential upgrade path from the Air, offering a more compact form factor with dosing capsules and fast heat-up. One knowledgeable member described the Fury Edge as "a great upgrade from Arizer portables."

Arizer Air vs. DynaVap VapCap

The VapCap was frequently recommended alongside or instead of the Air for specific use cases, particularly for users transitioning from combustion who wanted a ritual similar to smoking. The VapCap's butane-powered, on-demand nature made it complementary to the Air rather than a direct competitor. Several community members owned both, using the Air for longer sessions and the VapCap for quick, powerful single hits. The VapCap was also noted as a superior option for outdoor activities like hiking due to its lack of battery dependency and compact, durable all-metal construction.
