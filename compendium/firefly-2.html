# Firefly 2

Overview

The Firefly 2 is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Firefly Vapor, released as the successor to the original Firefly (Firefly 1). The device uses a convection heating method, drawing hot air across the herb material rather than applying direct contact heat. This on-demand convection design was one of its defining characteristics, allowing users to take individual draws without committing to a full session. The Firefly 2 is powered by a rechargeable lithium-ion battery and features a distinctive clamshell-style lid design with touch-sensitive activation on the exterior panels.

The Firefly 2 was positioned as a premium portable vaporizer and was notably one of the earlier mainstream portables to offer true on-demand convection heating with app-based temperature control. It supports both dry herb and concentrate use.

The Firefly 2 was noted in community discussions for its rapid heat-up time—one of the faster portable convection units available at the time of release. The device featured a glass-lined air path, which appealed to users prioritizing inert materials and clean vapor quality.

Community Reception

Community opinion on the Firefly 2 was decidedly mixed, with strong advocates and vocal critics often in the same discussion threads.

Strengths frequently cited:

  • Pure convection flavor: The on-demand convection heating was praised for delivering excellent flavor, particularly on the first few draws of a fresh bowl.
  • Rapid heat-up: The near-instant heat-up time was appreciated by users seeking quick, on-demand hits rather than extended sessions.
  • Material safety: The glass-lined vapor path and use of inert materials in the air path were highlighted by health-conscious users. One community member noted the Firefly 2 among portables with "high marks for glass pathways and inert materials."
  • Efficiency for light users: Users who consumed small amounts of herb found the Firefly 2 well-suited to their needs. One community member who tracked six months of daily usage with the Firefly 2 reported consuming only approximately 0.5 g per month, getting roughly 20 pulls per load before swapping material.

Weaknesses frequently cited:

  • Steep learning curve: The device required specific draw technique and packing methods that frustrated many users, especially those transitioning from conduction-based vaporizers.
  • Battery life: Short battery life was a consistent complaint. The same member who tracked usage mentioned starting to log sessions specifically because "my battery died on me a couple times."
  • Cleaning difficulty: The Firefly 2 (and its predecessor) was widely regarded as demanding to keep clean. One community member described the Firefly 1 as "always a PITA to keep clean," a sentiment that carried over to the second generation.
  • Non-swappable batteries: The lack of easily replaceable batteries was a significant drawback for users who prioritized extended portable use.
  • Polarizing value proposition: At its retail price point, some community members felt the device underdelivered. One member bluntly described the original Firefly as "garbage" when advising against a used purchase, recommending alternatives in a lower price range instead.

By the time several years had passed since its release, one community member observed that "the FF2 is considered old now," reflecting the rapid pace of portable vaporizer development and the arrival of newer convection competitors.

Tips & Techniques

The Firefly 2's convection design demanded more technique than many other portables. The community developed several key practices:

  • Grind and loading: A medium grind was generally recommended—not as fine as required for conduction vaporizers like the Pax. The herb should be loaded loosely into the bowl without packing down, as convection heating requires adequate airflow through the material.
  • Draw technique: Users found that slow, steady, and relatively long draws (8–15 seconds) produced the best results. Quick, hard pulls tended to cool the heater and produce thin vapor.
  • Stirring between draws: Because convection heating can extract unevenly, the community recommended stirring or gently shaking the bowl between hits to expose fresh surfaces of herb to the hot air.
  • Bowl size management: The Firefly 2's bowl could be partially loaded for smaller sessions. Light consumers found this particularly efficient for single-use, on-demand hits throughout the day.
  • ABV (Already Been Vaped) collection: Some users noted that the Firefly 2 did not vape material as dark as some other devices, meaning the ABV retained more remaining compounds. One community member specifically mentioned saving ABV from their Firefly (as opposed to their Volcano, which extracted more thoroughly) for secondary use in edibles such as ABV-infused milk.

Temperature Guide

The Firefly 2 used app-controlled temperature settings rather than a direct digital readout. The community generally agreed on the following temperature ranges:

Setting / RangeTemperatureEffect
Low~170–182°C (338–360°F)Best for flavor; light, terpy vapor; minimal visible cloud
Medium~182–200°C (360–392°F)Balanced flavor and vapor production; most versatile range
Medium-High~200–215°C (392–420°F)Thicker clouds; more body effects; reduced flavor clarity
High~215–230°C (420–446°F)Maximum extraction; dense vapor; more sedative effects

Community members who tracked their sessions often experimented with temperature stepping—starting low for flavor and gradually increasing through subsequent draws on the same load to maximize extraction. No single universally agreed-upon "sweet spot" emerged from discussions, as preferences varied significantly based on individual tolerance and material quality.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Cleaning was one of the Firefly 2's most discussed pain points. The community recommended:

  • Frequency: Clean the bowl and lid area after every few sessions, or whenever residue buildup becomes visible. Allowing resin to accumulate significantly increased cleaning difficulty.
  • Glass bowl: Wipe the borosilicate glass bowl with a cotton swab dipped in isopropyl alcohol (ISO). The glass surface generally cleaned up well if addressed regularly.
  • Lid and contact surfaces: The magnetic lid and the area around the faceplate were prone to buildup. ISO-soaked cotton swabs were the standard tool. One member noted that the Firefly 1's faceplate area got "slightly dirty" with use but responded to "a swab of ISO."
  • Screen maintenance: The fine screen should be inspected regularly and replaced when clogged or damaged. Spare screens were included with the device and available as replacements.
  • Avoid submerging: The electronic components and battery meant the device should never be submerged; only the removable parts and surfaces accessible by swab should be cleaned with liquid.
  • Residual smell: Like many vaporizers, the Firefly 2 could develop a noticeable odor if not cleaned regularly, particularly around the lid seal.

Accessories & Modifications

  • Water Pipe Adapters (WPA): The Firefly 2 could be paired with water filtration using third-party adapters. Community discussions about pairing portables with water tools frequently mentioned the Pinnacle Watertool (PNWT) and similar devices as compatible options for convection portables, including the Firefly.
  • Concentrate pads: The Firefly 2 shipped with or supported concentrate pads for use with waxes and other concentrates, expanding its versatility beyond dry herb.
  • Replacement screens: Extra screens were available and recommended as regular maintenance items.
  • Carrying cases: Some units shipped with soft cases; the original Firefly 1 included a soft case in its kit.

The community did not develop an extensive aftermarket modification ecosystem for the Firefly 2 comparable to what existed for devices like the Arizer line (with their glass stem ecosystem) or the Pax (with third-party accessories from makers like Delta 3D Studios).

Comparisons

The Firefly 2 was frequently compared to other portable vaporizers in community discussions:

Firefly 2 vs. Arizer Solo

In a direct comparison thread, the community generally favored the Arizer Solo for value, particularly for users on a budget ($100–180 range). One member noted: "Firefly is probably better, but the Solo is unquestionably a better value." The Firefly's higher price point placed it outside many users' budgets, and the Solo's reputation for reliable performance and easy glass stem maintenance made it the default recommendation for home-use portables at a lower cost.

Firefly 2 vs. Pax 3

The two devices represent fundamentally different heating philosophies—the Firefly 2's on-demand convection versus the Pax 3's session-based conduction. One community member who had owned both the Firefly 2 and the Pax 3 expressed a preference for the Pax 3, noting: "Have owned fw5, f2, and an AS2 and I like the Pax 3 out of those." The Pax 3 was preferred for its stealth factor, solid construction, and ability to be operated without looking at it, though users disliked its large bowl, cleaning requirements, and residual smell. The Firefly 2 was noted as a convection alternative in these discussions, but its learning curve and battery limitations tempered enthusiasm.

Firefly 2 vs. Fury 2 / Fierce

The Healthy Rips Fury 2 was frequently recommended as an alternative to the Firefly 2, particularly for users seeking better value and simpler operation. One member recommended the Fury 2 over a used Firefly 1 at $90, suggesting users "add 50 to your 90 and get a Healthy Rips Fury 2." The Fierce (a larger Fury 2 variant with removable battery) was also mentioned as an alternative for users who wanted swappable batteries, though some community members raised concerns about off-gassing issues with early Fierce units.

Firefly 2 vs. Grasshopper

Both devices were noted for their rapid heat-up times and on-demand capability. In discussions about pairing portables with water tools, both the Firefly and Grasshopper were highlighted as convection portables that "heat up in several seconds and can provide very powerful clouds." The Grasshopper was sometimes preferred for its pen-like form factor and native water pipe compatibility, while the Firefly 2 offered a more established track record (the Grasshopper was plagued by long wait times and reliability issues during its early production).

General Positioning

The Firefly 2 occupied a distinctive niche in the portable vaporizer market as one of the more accessible pure convection on-demand portables. Its glass-lined air path and inert material construction appealed to health-conscious users, while its flavor quality was generally respected. However, its battery limitations, cleaning demands, learning curve, and premium pricing meant the community often steered newcomers toward simpler, more forgiving alternatives unless the user specifically sought the on-demand convection experience.
