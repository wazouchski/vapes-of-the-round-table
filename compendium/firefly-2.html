# Firefly 2

Overview

The Firefly 2 is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Firefly Vapor, released as the successor to the original Firefly (Firefly 1). The device uses a convection heating method, rapidly heating air over the material rather than relying on conduction from heated surfaces. This on-demand convection design was one of its primary distinguishing features, allowing users to take individual draws without committing to a full session. The Firefly 2 is powered by a rechargeable lithium-ion battery and features touch-sensitive activation on the exterior of the unit. It also includes Bluetooth connectivity and a companion smartphone app for temperature control.

The Firefly 2 was positioned as a premium portable vaporizer and was notable within the community for its emphasis on pure flavor, glass-lined vapor path, and modern industrial design. It also offered limited concentrate capability alongside its primary dry herb function.

Community Reception

Community opinion on the Firefly 2 was notably divided, making it one of the more polarizing portables discussed over the years.

Praised aspects:

  • Flavor quality: The convection heating and glass-lined vapor path were widely acknowledged as producing excellent, pure flavor, especially on the first few draws of a fresh load.
  • On-demand design: Users who wanted a portable for occasional, single draws—rather than full sessions—appreciated the ability to take a hit or two, set the device down, and return to the same load later without wasting material.
  • Health-conscious materials: The glass vapor path and the absence of plastics in the airway were frequently noted in discussions about the "healthiest" portable options. One community member highlighted the Firefly 2's glass pathway and inert materials when discussing health-oriented vaporizer choices.
  • Rapid heat-up: The near-instant heat-up was considered a strong advantage for portability and quick use on walks, hikes, and other activities.
  • Efficiency for light consumers: One community member who tracked every session over six months reported averaging just 0.5 grams per month using the Firefly 2, noting they could get approximately 20 pulls from a single load before replacing it—demonstrating the device's suitability for very light, efficient consumption.

Criticized aspects:

  • Steep learning curve: The technique required for consistent vapor production was a frequent point of discussion. Even sellers of the device would warn prospective buyers about the learning curve, suggesting it was not ideal for beginners.
  • Battery life: Battery life was widely regarded as a significant weakness. Multiple community members reported the battery dying unexpectedly during use, with one member noting they began meticulously tracking their sessions specifically to avoid running out of battery mid-load.
  • Cleaning burden: The Firefly line—both versions 1 and 2—was described as a "PITA (pain in the ass) to keep clean" by at least one longtime owner. Regular maintenance of the bowl and vapor path was considered more demanding than many competing portables.
  • Price-to-value ratio: At its original price point, many community members felt the Firefly 2 did not offer sufficient value compared to alternatives. One member bluntly characterized the original Firefly as "garbage" and recommended spending the money elsewhere. By the late 2010s, some community members described the Firefly 2 as feeling "old" relative to newer convection portables entering the market.
  • Counterfeit concerns: Particularly with the original Firefly, the community warned about counterfeit units circulating on the secondary market. Prospective buyers of used units were advised to verify authenticity through original packaging, receipts, and proof of purchase.

Overall, the Firefly 2 was regarded as a niche device best suited for flavor-chasing solo users with light consumption habits who were willing to master its technique and tolerate its battery limitations. It was not generally recommended as a first vaporizer or for group sessions.

Tips & Techniques

The Firefly 2's convection, on-demand heating required more user involvement than typical session portables. Community members shared several key techniques:

  • Draw technique: Slow, steady, and controlled inhales were essential. Pulling too hard or too fast could cool the heating element and result in thin, unsatisfying vapor. The community emphasized that this was a device that rewarded patience and practice.
  • Loading: A light, even pack in the bowl was preferred. Overpacking or packing too tightly would restrict airflow and impede the convection process. Some users reported best results with a moderately fine grind spread in a thin, loose layer across the bowl.
  • Single-hit use: The Firefly 2 excelled as a "one-hitter" style vaporizer. Users could load the bowl, take one or two draws, and return to the same load hours or even a day later. This made it popular among very light consumers.
  • Stir between draws: Stirring or lightly agitating the material between draws helped ensure even extraction, as convection heating could sometimes leave portions of the bowl less evenly heated than conduction devices.
  • ABV (Already Been Vaped) collection: One community member noted that the Firefly tends to produce lighter-colored ABV compared to conduction session vapes like the Pax, making the spent material potentially more potent for repurposing in edibles or infusions (such as ABV milk).

Temperature Guide

The Firefly 2's temperature was controlled via its companion smartphone app, which offered several preset temperature levels rather than precise degree-by-degree control. Community guidance on temperature selection:

SettingApproximate RangeEffect
Low~170–180°C / ~340–360°FBest flavor; wispy vapor; ideal for terpene-rich first draws
Medium~190–200°C / ~375–395°FBalanced flavor and vapor production; community sweet spot for most sessions
High~210–220°C / ~410–430°FThicker clouds; more complete extraction; less flavor nuance

  • The community widely agreed that starting at lower temperatures for the first few draws, then stepping up, was the best approach to maximize both flavor and efficiency.
  • For concentrate use, higher temperature settings were required, though the community generally felt the Firefly 2's concentrate performance was secondary to its dry herb capabilities.

Note: Exact temperature calibration was a subject of some community debate, and some users felt the app's reported temperatures did not always align perfectly with perceived vapor output.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Cleaning was one of the most frequently discussed aspects of Firefly 2 ownership, and the community stressed the importance of regular maintenance:

  • Frequency: Cleaning after every few sessions was recommended. Residue buildup on the bowl and the glass window/lid could impair flavor and function relatively quickly.
  • Bowl and lid: Wipe the bowl area and the interior of the lid with a cotton swab dampened with isopropyl alcohol (ISO). This was the primary maintenance task and the one most users found tedious.
  • Glass window: The borosilicate glass component of the vapor path could accumulate residue; gentle cleaning with ISO and a soft cloth was advised.
  • Screens: Replacement screens were available and should be swapped periodically. Clogged or dirty screens restricted airflow and diminished vapor quality.
  • Exterior: The touch-sensitive panels and exterior could be wiped with a slightly damp cloth but should be kept dry around the electronic components and charging contacts.

Community members consistently noted that the Firefly line required more cleaning attention than many competing portables, which was a meaningful factor in ownership satisfaction.

Common Issues & Fixes

IssueDetails & Community Solutions
Short battery lifeThe most common complaint. Users recommended keeping spare batteries on hand and tracking session count to avoid mid-session depletion. Charging fully before each use and minimizing high-temperature sessions helped extend battery life.
Inconsistent vapor productionOften attributable to draw technique rather than a device defect. The community recommended practicing slow, steady draws and adjusting grind consistency and pack density.
Difficulty cleaningAddressed by establishing a regular cleaning routine and not allowing residue to build up over multiple sessions. ISO and cotton swabs were the standard tools.

App connectivity issuesSome users reported Bluetooth pairing difficulties with the companion app. Restarting both the device and the phone, and ensuring the app was up to date, were the standard troubleshooting steps.

Accessories & Modifications

  • Replacement batteries: Given the known battery life limitations, spare batteries were considered practically essential for regular users.
  • Replacement screens: Community members recommended keeping a supply of replacement screens to maintain optimal airflow.
  • Water pipe adapters (WPA): The Firefly 2 was mentioned in community discussions about pairing portable vaporizers with water filtration. While not as commonly paired with bubblers or hydratubes as some other portables (e.g., the Arizer Air with a 14mm GonG adapter, or the Grasshopper), users did explore this option. Accessories like the Pinnacle Watertool (PNWT) and similar adapters were discussed as compatible options for those seeking water-cooled draws.
  • Concentrate pads: The Firefly 2 included or supported concentrate pads for use with waxy concentrates, though this was generally considered a secondary function.

Comparisons

The Firefly 2 was frequently compared to other portables in community purchase-advice discussions:

vs. Arizer Solo / Arizer Air

The community generally considered the Arizer Solo a far better value proposition, especially for users on a budget. One member summarized that the Firefly was "probably better" in some respects but the Solo was "unquestionably a better value." The Solo's session-style conduction/hybrid heating, longer battery life, and ease of use through glass stems made it a more straightforward recommendation, especially for home use. The Arizer Air was highlighted for its GonG adapter compatibility with water tools, a use case where the Firefly was less commonly paired.

vs. Pax 3

A direct comparison that arose frequently. The Pax 3 was praised for its stealth factor, solid construction, and strong battery life, but criticized for its large bowl, difficult cleaning, magnetic charger issues, and lingering smell. One community member who had owned both the Firefly 2 and the Pax 3 preferred the Pax 3 overall, noting that the Pax 3's conduction heating and the Firefly 2's convection heating offered fundamentally different experiences. The Firefly 2 was considered superior for pure flavor, while the Pax 3 was regarded as more practical for everyday portable use.

vs. Fury 2 / Healthy Rips Fierce

The Fury 2 and its larger sibling, the Fierce (which added a removable battery), were frequently recommended as alternatives to the Firefly 2. Multiple community members suggested the Fury 2 as a better overall value, with one bluntly recommending that prospective Firefly buyers redirect their budget toward a Fury 2 instead. The Fierce addressed one of the Firefly 2's weaknesses—battery life—by incorporating a swappable battery, though some members raised concerns about early Fierce units having off-gassing issues.

vs. Grasshopper

Both the Firefly 2 and the Grasshopper were recognized as fast-heating, on-demand convection portables. The Grasshopper was often favored in discussions about pairing portables with water pipes due to its pen-like form factor and native compatibility with standard WPAs. However, the Grasshopper had its own well-documented reliability and availability concerns that tempered recommendations.

vs. Firefly 1 (Original)

The Firefly 2 was a direct evolution of the original Firefly, sharing the same core convection-on-demand philosophy but in a lighter, slimmer, and more refined package. The original Firefly was remembered as a pioneering device but was plagued by weight, battery life issues, and a significant counterfeit problem on the used market. The Firefly 2 addressed some of these shortcomings while retaining the same general strengths and weaknesses in terms of technique sensitivity and cleaning demands.

---

This article is based on community discussions spanning approximately 2013–2019, aggregated from multiple threads involving purchase advice, usage tracking, device comparisons, and accessory recommendations. Community opinion on the Firefly 2 remained divided throughout this period, reflecting its nature as a device that strongly rewarded a specific style of use while frustrating those seeking a more conventional portable experience.
