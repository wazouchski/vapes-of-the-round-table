# Grasshopper

Overview

The Grasshopper is a portable, battery-powered dry herb vaporizer designed and manufactured by Hopper Labs, a small American company based in Colorado. The device gained attention through a successful crowdfunding campaign (Indiegogo) beginning in 2014, with initial units shipping to backers in 2015 and 2016. The Grasshopper is notable for its remarkably small form factor—roughly the size and shape of a pen—making it one of the most discreet portable vaporizers ever produced.

The Grasshopper utilizes a predominantly convection heating method and is powered by a proprietary rechargeable lithium-ion battery. It is classified as an on-demand vaporizer, meaning it heats up almost instantly and does not require a sustained session to extract material. The device features a rear-mounted temperature dial for analog temperature control and a simple click-on activation via the rear button (integrated into the pen clip). It was available in both stainless steel and titanium body variants, with the titanium models offered in several anodized color options (including blue).

Community Reception

The Grasshopper was one of the most discussed and debated vaporizers in the community over a period spanning several years. When working properly, the device was widely praised for its near-instant heat-up time, pen-like form factor, and impressive vapor production for its size. Community members frequently noted that the on-demand heating style was ideal for users who preferred to take one or two draws and then set the device down, rather than committing to a full session.

However, the Grasshopper was equally notorious for its reliability problems. The community extensively documented quality control (QC) issues, including high rates of dead-on-arrival (DOA) units and failures occurring after relatively short periods of use. One experienced community member characterized the situation by noting that despite their own unit running well for about nine months, they hesitated to purchase another or recommend the Grasshopper to newer vaporists due to a "high infant mortality/DOA rate" and "ambiguous and confusing failure symptoms." The same member observed that users often found themselves trying to parse the type and meaning of various LED blink codes and error states.

The community also widely discussed the extreme delays associated with both pre-orders and warranty service. Long wait times—sometimes measured in months—were a recurring theme. As one member put it, "a future vape that may or may not work is less good than one of the most reliable portables on the market already in your hand." The phenomenon of "playing vape tag through the mail"—repeatedly sending a unit in for warranty repair only to receive it back still faulty or have it fail again shortly after—was a commonly reported experience.

At least one community member attempted to conduct a structured survey to determine whether the reported failure rates were representative of all units produced or were disproportionately amplified by users seeking help online. The results and consensus remained inconclusive, though the volume of reports was difficult to dismiss.

Despite these concerns, the Grasshopper retained a devoted following among users who had received well-functioning units. The device's unique combination of form factor, convection heating, and on-demand operation was considered unmatched by any other electric portable at the time.

Pros (as discussed by the community):

  • Extremely compact and discreet pen-shaped design
  • Near-instant heat-up (~3–5 seconds)
  • On-demand convection heating—ideal for "sippers" who take a draw or two at a time
  • Impressive vapor production for the size
  • Lifetime warranty offered by manufacturer

Cons (as discussed by the community):

  • Widely reported quality control and reliability issues
  • High DOA and early failure rates
  • Lengthy pre-order wait times and slow warranty turnaround
  • Ambiguous and confusing error/failure symptoms (LED blink codes)
  • Proprietary battery with limited capacity
  • Availability of counterfeit units on secondary markets

Tips & Techniques

Community discussion of the Grasshopper focused primarily on reliability and purchasing considerations rather than extensive technique sharing in the reviewed threads. However, several usage principles were commonly referenced:

  • On-Demand Use: The Grasshopper was best suited for on-demand use—taking one or two draws, then setting the device down and returning later. This made it particularly appealing to users transitioning from pipes or one-hitters who preferred a "sip and set down" approach rather than committing to a multi-minute session.
  • Draw Technique: Due to its convection-dominant heating, a slow, steady draw was generally recommended to allow hot air to pass evenly through the herb chamber.
  • Loading: A moderately packed chamber—not too tight, not too loose—was considered optimal to allow airflow while ensuring even extraction.
  • Battery Management: Given the limited capacity of the GHB2 batteries, experienced users kept multiple charged batteries on hand. It was noted in marketplace listings that battery performance could degrade over time, with some batteries performing notably better than others even in the same set.

Temperature Guide

The Grasshopper's rear-mounted analog dial provides continuously variable temperature control across its range. While the community did not establish rigidly defined temperature benchmarks specific to the Grasshopper in the reviewed threads, general convection vaporizer guidelines applied:

Temperature RangeEffect
130–160°C (266–320°F)Flavor-forward, light vapor; best for terpene-rich, low-temperature draws
160–185°C (320–365°F)Balanced flavor and vapor production; moderate extraction
185–210°C (365–410°F)Maximum extraction and cloud production; hotter, more throat-feel ("tickle on the throat," as one user described their preference)

One user specifically noted that they appreciated a warmer vape with noticeable throat sensation, suggesting that the Grasshopper's upper temperature range was well-suited for users seeking a more smoking-like experience.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Detailed cleaning protocols were not extensively discussed in the reviewed threads. General community practices for the Grasshopper included:

  • Chamber Cleaning: Regular brushing of the herb chamber after sessions to prevent residue buildup.
  • Mouthpiece Maintenance: Both silicone and glass mouthpieces should be cleaned periodically; glass mouthpieces can be soaked in isopropyl alcohol.
  • Screen Maintenance: Keeping the chamber screen clear to maintain airflow, which is critical for convection performance.
  • Body Care: Stainless steel and titanium bodies are durable but can be wiped down with a lightly dampened cloth. One marketplace listing noted a unit in "clean like new condition" after only a few sessions, suggesting the device's materials resist staining.

Common Issues & Fixes

The Grasshopper was among the most warranty-dependent vaporizers discussed in the community. Common issues included:

  • Dead on Arrival (DOA) Units: A notable percentage of new units reportedly did not function correctly out of the box.
  • LED Blink Codes / Error States: The Grasshopper communicates errors via LED patterns, but the community found these "ambiguous and confusing." Users frequently sought help interpreting specific blink patterns to determine whether the issue was with the battery, the heater, or the body electronics.
  • Heater Failures: The internal heater was a frequent point of failure, sometimes manifesting as reduced vapor production or complete failure to heat.
  • Body Replacements: At least one long-term user reported having received a body replacement during the device's lifespan, suggesting this was a recognized warranty remedy.
  • Battery Degradation: The proprietary GHB2 batteries degraded over time, with users reporting that out of a set of four batteries, performance could vary significantly, with "two working better than the others."
  • Warranty Considerations: The lifetime warranty was tied to the registered owner. Warranty transfer was possible but required a fee (approximately $45). Users who modified the device (e.g., removing the rear pen clip) expressed uncertainty about whether their warranty would still be honored.

Community Advice: Given the high failure rate, the community generally recommended purchasing directly from Hopper Labs to ensure warranty coverage, and keeping multiple batteries and potentially a backup vaporizer on hand.

Accessories & Modifications

  • Mouthpieces: The Grasshopper supported multiple mouthpiece options, including a rubber tip, silicone mouthpiece, and glass mouthpiece. The glass mouthpiece was preferred by some users for flavor purity.
  • Front Ends: A bronze front end was available as an alternative to the standard option.
  • Carrying Cases: Third-party accessories such as the Delta3D leather Grasshopper case were sought after by community members for protecting the device during transport.
  • Water Pipe Adapter (WPA): The Grasshopper's form factor allowed it to be used with water pipe adapters, though specific WPA discussion was limited in the reviewed threads.
  • Batteries: The GHB2 proprietary battery was the standard; users typically maintained a supply of four or more batteries to ensure uninterrupted use.

Comparisons

Grasshopper vs. Arizer Air

This was a commonly discussed comparison in the community, as both devices occupied a similar price range (under $150–$225). The key differentiator was vaping style:

  • The Arizer Air was considered one of the most reliable session portables on the market—a known quantity with consistent performance and durable build quality. It excelled for users who enjoyed sitting down for a 10–15 minute session.
  • The Grasshopper was considered superior for on-demand use, appealing to users who preferred to take one or two draws and set the device aside. One user explicitly noted: "I am not a 'session' person. I tend to rip my pipe once or twice, and then put it down for ten or fifteen minutes."
  • Community members acknowledged that the Arizer Air delivered cooler vapor, while the Grasshopper offered hotter, more immediate draws.
  • Reliability heavily favored the Arizer Air. As one member summarized, the Air was "one of the most reliable portables on the market," while the Grasshopper's working condition was "still a bit of an assumption."

Grasshopper vs. Firewood

The Firewood (particularly the FW3 and FW4) was frequently suggested as an alternative on-demand convection portable for users interested in the Grasshopper's style of use but concerned about reliability and shipping delays. Community members noted the Firewood was periodically restocked and, while not as compact as the Grasshopper, offered similar on-demand convection performance with better reliability.

Grasshopper vs. Butane Vaporizers (Sticky Brick, VaporGenie, Vapman, Hammer)

For users seeking on-demand performance without the Grasshopper's reliability risks, butane-powered options were commonly recommended. The Sticky Brick (OG and Junior), VaporGenie, Vapman, and Hammer were all mentioned as alternatives that could deliver on-demand convection hits without dependency on electronics or proprietary batteries. These were considered particularly suitable for home use where the Grasshopper's stealth advantage was less relevant.

Grasshopper vs. DaVinci IQ

Both the Grasshopper and DaVinci IQ were subjects of community concern regarding quality assurance. At least one community member conducted parallel surveys of both devices' owners to assess whether reported failure rates were representative or skewed by self-selection bias among forum posters seeking help. The comparison centered less on performance and more on the risk calculus of purchasing devices with known QC challenges.

Counterfeit Grasshoppers

The community documented the existence of counterfeit Grasshopper units sold through platforms such as Alibaba and eBay. These knockoffs appeared while many legitimate pre-order customers were still waiting for their units. Anecdotal reports from online gatherings suggested that some buyers purchased multiple counterfeits as a hedge against failure (one account described a buyer who purchased ten knockoffs from Alibaba). Community consensus strongly cautioned against counterfeit purchases, noting the absence of warranty coverage and the inability to verify performance claims, as buyers typically lacked an authentic unit for comparison. Forum moderators actively removed links to counterfeit product listings.
