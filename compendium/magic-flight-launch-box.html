# Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

Overview

The Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB) is a ultra-compact, battery-powered portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Magic Flight, a small American company based in San Diego, California. First released in 2009, it quickly became one of the most recognizable and widely discussed entry-level vaporizers in the community. The device uses conduction heating via a fine stainless steel mesh screen that is resistively heated by a single AA NiMH rechargeable battery. The user manually controls temperature through draw speed and battery engagement duration, making it one of the most technique-dependent vaporizers ever produced.

The MFLB was notable for its handcrafted wooden construction, lifetime warranty, and minimalist philosophy. It was manufactured in several wood varieties including maple, walnut, and cherry, as well as limited "exotic" wood editions. The device contained no electronics — no circuit boards, no temperature regulation, and no buttons — making it entirely user-controlled and, by extension, highly divisive within the vaporizer community.

Version Notes: Over its production run, the MFLB received incremental design updates. One notable change was the addition of a small ball catch mechanism beneath the lid to keep it securely closed, replacing earlier versions that lacked this feature. Community members noted that purchasing directly from Magic Flight's own storefront ("Blissville") ensured receiving the latest version, while third-party retailers on Amazon or eBay sometimes shipped older models.

Community Reception

The Magic Flight Launch Box was one of the most polarizing vaporizers ever discussed within the community. It inspired passionate advocacy from dedicated users and equally strong criticism from those who found it frustrating or underpowered.

Praise

  • Stealth and Portability: The MFLB was widely regarded as one of the most discreet vaporizers available. Its tiny form factor, lack of visible electronics, and on-demand heating meant users could take a quick draw almost anywhere. One experienced community member stated that with proper hand technique, "you'll never see the battery," emphasizing its unmatched concealability.
  • Efficiency for Solo Microdosing: For light users who consumed small amounts infrequently, the MFLB was considered highly efficient. Its small trench was well-suited to single-user sessions with minimal herb.
  • Lifetime Warranty: Magic Flight's lifetime warranty was widely praised. The company replaced units with minimal hassle, and the process was described by community members as "pretty much no questions asked." The warranty replacement required the user to disassemble and disable the old unit (tearing out the screen), after which a replacement was shipped.
  • Craftsmanship and Philosophy: The handmade wooden construction and the company's artisan ethos resonated with many users who appreciated the device as more than just a tool.
  • On-Demand Heating: Because the screen heats and cools within seconds, the MFLB was a true on-demand vaporizer — ideal for single hits with no wasted herb between draws.
  • Low-Temperature Effects: Some users specifically sought out the MFLB for its ability to deliver what one user described as a "vibrant, uplifting, focused high" at low temperatures, attributing this to their refined technique and the device's inherently light extraction characteristics.

Criticism

  • Steep Learning Curve: The MFLB was arguably the most technique-dependent portable vaporizer of its era. Community consensus held that satisfying results required mastering draw speed, battery engagement timing, and shaking technique. Multiple users reported being unable to achieve satisfactory results despite extensive research and practice. One member recounted using the device "10 to 15 times trying to figure it out" across multiple years, trying "every technique there was," and ultimately selling it.
  • Limited Power and Vapor Production: Many users found the MFLB unable to produce the vapor density they desired, especially compared to electronically regulated portables. One community member bluntly described it as "the worst vape I've owned aside from some Chinese whip vape." Another noted that the device "just doesn't deliver fast enough for me."
  • Poor for Group Use: The community broadly agreed the MFLB was not suitable for sharing. Its small trench, technique dependency, and battery limitations made passing it around a group impractical.
  • Battery Dependency and Degradation: The NiMH AA batteries were a persistent frustration. Voltage dropped rapidly during use, and battery performance degraded over time, particularly if users did not follow proper charging practices. One member noted that after leaving batteries on the charger overnight, "they don't hold a charge very well anymore."
  • Value Proposition Over Time: As the portable vaporizer market matured, community members increasingly questioned the MFLB's value relative to electronically regulated alternatives. One member commented that they would "eliminate the MFLB" from consideration, stating "I don't think it's worth its current cost."
  • Coughing: Multiple users reported that the MFLB caused more coughing than other vaporizers. Community analysis attributed this to the very short air path (resulting in hotter vapor reaching the user's throat), the dryness of the vapor, and the potential for fine plant particulates to pass through the screen.

Overall Standing

The MFLB occupied a unique niche: beloved by a dedicated subset of technique-oriented solo users, but increasingly overshadowed and outperformed by more modern portables. By the mid-to-late 2010s, community members consistently recommended alternatives like the Arizer Solo/Air over the MFLB for new users, with the general sentiment being that while the MFLB was a good fit for a very specific use case, it was limiting for anyone who might want to expand their vaping habits. As one member summarized: "If you have used other vaporizers, the MFLB will not satisfy what you are looking for in a portable."

Tips & Techniques

The MFLB's performance was almost entirely determined by user technique. The community developed extensive guidance over years of discussion:

Grinding

  • Grind as fine as possible. The MFLB required a much finer grind than most other vaporizers. Many users recommended grinding herb, vaping it partially, then re-grinding the ABV (already been vaped) material to expose fresh surface area.
  • The finishing grinder sold by Magic Flight was considered a worthwhile accessory for achieving optimal consistency.

Draw Technique

  • Slow, controlled sipping draws were the foundational technique. Drawing too fast cooled the screen and produced thin vapor; drawing too slowly risked combustion.
  • A common technique involved engaging the battery for 3–5 seconds before beginning to draw, allowing the screen to reach operating temperature.
  • "Micro-hitting" — taking very small, short sips — was another approach, though some users reported this did not eliminate coughing.
  • More advanced users developed a rhythmic "sip and pause" pattern: engage the battery, take a slow sip, pause briefly (while the screen reheats), then sip again, all in one battery engagement cycle.

Shaking

  • Between draws, users needed to shake the box to redistribute the herb across the screen. Without shaking, herb in direct contact with the screen could combust while herb further away remained unvaped.
  • The community recommended a firm but controlled shake with the lid closed.

Battery Management

  • Fresh, fully charged batteries were essential for satisfactory performance. Partially depleted batteries resulted in dramatically reduced vapor production.
  • Keeping spare charged batteries on hand was considered a necessity for any session.
  • The Glyph batteries shipped by Magic Flight in later production runs were considered superior to earlier stock batteries.
  • Powerex 2400mAh batteries were a popular third-party alternative, reported by some users to be "slightly more powerful than the original ones."

Native Mode

  • Using the MFLB without any stem ("native mode") provided the shortest possible air path and was preferred by some users for maximum vapor density, though it also contributed to hotter, drier vapor.

Concentrate Use

  • Using concentrates (budder, BHO, kief) in the MFLB was a topic of significant debate. The community generally cautioned against placing gooey concentrates directly on the fine mesh screen, as cleaning was extremely difficult. One experienced member warned: "I wouldn't put any kind of gooey concentrate on a LB screen. It's a very fine mesh and since you can't remove it for cleaning...."
  • The recommended approach for concentrates was to place a small amount on top of a thin bed of dry herb or already-vaped herb, or to use Magic Flight's dedicated concentrate trays.
  • Some users reported success with BHO "against manufacturer's instructions," but this was not broadly recommended.

Temperature Guide

The MFLB had no temperature readout or electronic regulation. Temperature was controlled entirely through battery engagement duration and draw speed. The community developed an understanding of the device's thermal behavior through experimentation:

VariableEffect on Temperature
Longer battery engagement before drawHigher starting temperature
Slower draw speedHigher temperature (less cooling airflow)
Faster draw speedLower temperature (more cooling airflow)
Fresh batteryHigher temperature potential
Depleted batteryLower, inconsistent temperature

General Temperature Ranges (Estimated)

  • Low temperature / flavor range: ~150–180°C (302–356°F) — Achieved with shorter battery engagement and moderate draw speed. Associated with lighter, more flavorful vapor and the "uplifting, focused" effects some users prized.
  • Medium temperature: ~180–200°C (356–392°F) — The general-use sweet spot for most users; balance of flavor and vapor production.
  • High temperature / efficiency range: ~200–220°C (392–428°F) — Achieved with longer engagement and slower draws. Greater extraction but increased coughing risk and harsher vapor.
  • Combustion threshold: Approximately 230°C+ (446°F+) — Users who engaged the battery too long or drew too slowly risked combustion, identifiable by a burning taste and blackened herb.

Community members noted that with modified power sources (such as LiPo battery setups at 1.60–1.70V output), "the tray can easily hit 400+ degrees" Fahrenheit, requiring extra caution to avoid combustion.

Community Insight

A veteran community member noted that temperature-related coughing could be triggered by differences as small as 5°F, suggesting that the MFLB's lack of precise temperature control was a meaningful limitation for sensitive users.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Screen Cleaning

  • The stainless steel screen could not be removed and was the most challenging component to maintain.
  • Isopropyl alcohol (ISO): While ISO was used sparingly by some users, the community noted that the MFLB "can't be cleaned to [a legally clean] magnitude" due to the wood body's tendency to absorb residue and the screen's delicate construction.
  • Brush cleaning: The included brush was used after each session to sweep out spent herb and prevent buildup.
  • Burn-off method: Engaging the battery with an empty trench to burn off residual material was a common maintenance technique, though it needed to be done carefully to avoid damaging the screen.

Stem and Whip Cleaning

  • Glass stems accumulated condensed vapor oil ("reclaim") over time. Cleaning frequency of twice per week was suggested by at least one community member.
  • A small nug or piece of herb could be pushed through an oily stem to collect the reclaim, then vaped — a practical if informal harvesting method.
  • A twisted piece of napkin or paper towel was another simple cleaning approach for stems.
  • Some users considered harvesting stem oil worthwhile, though others felt "not enough develops to make it worth your while" and preferred to focus on saving ABV for edibles instead.

General Maintenance

  • Keeping the box dry was important given the wood construction.
  • The contact points where the battery met the box's rails could develop oxidation or residue, which community members cleaned carefully to maintain electrical conductivity.

Common Issues & Fixes

Weak or No Vapor Production

  • Cause: Most commonly attributed to depleted or degraded batteries, improper technique, or insufficient grind fineness.
  • Fix: Use freshly charged batteries; ensure herb is ground extremely fine; practice proper draw speed and battery engagement timing. Purchasing new, high-quality NiMH batteries (Glyph or Powerex 2400mAh) often restored performance.

Battery Fit Issues with Charger

  • Issue: Some users reported that supplied Glyph batteries were slightly too long to properly seat in the PU-02DV-MGFL charger, making contact only at an angle.
  • Fix: The community suggested checking battery and charger compatibility, trying alternate battery brands, and contacting Magic Flight for support. This appeared to be an intermittent quality control issue rather than a universal problem.

Excessive Coughing

  • Cause: Attributed to the short air path (delivering hotter, drier vapor), potential fine plant particulates passing through the screen, and/or dirty screen buildup.
  • Fix: Use the glass draw stem or whip to extend the air path; use through a water pipe with the WPA; clean the screen regularly; moderate draw speed to control temperature. Water filtration was reported to help significantly.

Combustion

  • Cause: Battery engaged too long, draw too slow, or herb not redistributed (shaken) between hits.
  • Fix: Shorter battery engagement periods; faster draw speed; shake between every draw; ensure herb is evenly distributed across the screen.

Lid Not Staying Closed

  • Cause: Older versions lacked the ball catch mechanism.
  • Fix: Upgrading to a newer version with the ball catch, or purchasing directly from Magic Flight's storefront to ensure receiving the latest revision.

Counterfeits

  • Magic Flight published an official counterfeit identification page to help users distinguish genuine units from imitations. The community discussed several counterfeit products, including the "Dragon Lite," which was identified as an MFLB knockoff. Counterfeits were reported to be potentially dangerous, with some failing to reach proper vaporization temperatures and others exceeding them to the point of combustion. Purchasing from authorized retailers was strongly recommended.

Accessories & Modifications

Official Accessories

  • Power Adapter (PA 2.0): A wall-powered adapter that replaced batteries, providing consistent voltage and eliminating battery degradation issues. Available in wall and car adapter versions. Widely considered a transformative upgrade that addressed the MFLB's primary weakness. The PA included adjustable power output.
  • Concentrate Trays: Screened and unscreened versions available for use with concentrates, allowing users to avoid placing material directly on the herb screen.
  • Glass Draw Stems: Multiple lengths available; extended the air path for cooler vapor.
  • Draw Whip: A flexible tube (various lengths, commonly under one foot) for use similar to a desktop whip vaporizer.
  • Water Pipe Adapter (WPA): Allowed the MFLB to connect to standard water pipes, addressing coughing and vapor harshness issues.
  • Finishing Grinder: Designed to produce the ultra-fine grind the MFLB required.
  • Glyph Batteries: Magic Flight's own branded NiMH batteries, introduced as an upgrade to earlier generic stock batteries. Available in standard and "Gold" variants.

Community Modifications

#### LiPo Battery Modification

One of the most extensively documented community modifications involved replacing the stock NiMH AA batteries with a 1S LiPo (lithium polymer) battery combined with a resistor-based voltage dropping circuit. A community member documented the following configurations:

  • 0.25 ohm / 80W resistor: Dropped 4.20V LiPo output to 1.45–1.55V at 10 amps — described as "slightly higher than a freshly charged AA cell."
  • 0.20 ohm / 100W resistor: Dropped 4.20V LiPo output to 1.60–1.70V at 12 amps — described as "actually quite a bit hotter than stock," capable of pushing the tray above 400°F. Required careful use to avoid combustion.
  • Recommended LiPo capacity of 3000mAh minimum, with the modder personally using 6000+ mAh cells for extended sessions.
  • This modification required fabricating a dummy battery to interface with the MFLB's stock contact system, or devising an alternative connection method.
  • Another community member suggested using a switching step-down voltage regulator instead of resistors for better efficiency and more consistent output voltage, though the original modder defended the resistor approach as simpler and more cost-effective.

#### Custom Stems

Some community members crafted custom wooden stems for the MFLB as DIY projects, though documentation of completed builds was limited.

Water Pipe Use

Multiple users invested significantly in pairing the MFLB with high-quality glass water pieces. One community member described a "$700 setup" combining the MFLB with the Power Adapter, water pipe insert, and a premium glass water pipe, noting the substantial upgrade from basic configurations. The community recommended smaller water pieces for vapor use, as large chambers could dissipate vapor density.

Comparisons

The MFLB was most frequently compared to the following devices in community purchase-advice discussions:

MFLB vs. Arizer Solo / Arizer Air

This was by far the most common comparison in the community. The consensus strongly favored the Solo/Air for most users:

  • Ease of use: The Solo and Air were electronically temperature-regulated, eliminating the MFLB's steep learning curve. Community members consistently recommended these as better options for new users.
  • Vapor production: The Solo/Air produced significantly denser vapor with less effort.
  • Group use: The Solo/Air were considered far better for sharing, with one member noting "the sessions with friends will be easier" with the Air.
  • Portability: The MFLB was smaller and more discreet than the Solo (which one member described as "kinda bulky" but still portable). The Air was more competitive in size.
  • On-demand vs. session: The MFLB was on-demand (single hits possible), while the Solo/Air were session vaporizers requiring 8–12 minutes per stem load.
  • Overall recommendation: Multiple community members reported stopping MFLB use entirely after acquiring a Solo, with one stating: "I stopped using the MFLB permanently after getting the Solo."

MFLB vs. DaVinci (Original)

One experienced member used both regularly, describing the DaVinci and MFLB as their portable rotation. The DaVinci offered temperature control and a larger bowl, while the MFLB offered smaller, more discreet on-demand hits. The DaVinci was recommended for use up to about 385°F.

MFLB vs. Pax / Pax 2

The Pax was considered more pocketable and easier to use but significantly more expensive. One member who owned both strongly preferred the Pax for its simplicity, cloud production, and ability to preload a bowl. The MFLB's advantage was its lower price point and on-demand capability versus the Pax's session-oriented design.

MFLB vs. Desktop Log Vaporizers (E-Nano)

For home use, log vaporizers like the E-Nano were consistently recommended over the MFLB. The E-Nano could be left on continuously for instant availability, handled whole nugs, and delivered dramatically superior vapor production in just a few draws. The MFLB's sole advantage in this comparison was portability.

MFLB vs. Cera

The Cera was considered superior in vapor purity and taste due to its all-ceramic air path, but had a smaller herb capacity and a heavier, more cumbersome body. One community member noted the MFLB was their "first vape" and described it as ideal for light users willing to invest in learning the technique, while the Cera was better for those prioritizing vapor purity.
