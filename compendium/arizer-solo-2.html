# Arizer Solo 2

Overview

The Arizer Solo 2 is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. Released in 2017 as the successor to the original Arizer Solo, the Solo 2 is a session-style conduction/convection hybrid vaporizer powered by an internal rechargeable lithium-ion battery. It uses Arizer's signature glass stem system, where ground herb is packed into a borosilicate glass aroma tube that inserts into a ceramic heating chamber atop the unit. The Solo 2 was widely regarded within the vaporizer community as a reliable, easy-to-use, and affordable entry point into quality dry herb vaporization, and it maintained a strong following for years after its release.

The device is classified among Arizer's "2nd Generation Portables," alongside the Arizer Air 2 and Arizer ArGo. It charges via a 12V DC barrel connector and features an OLED display with precise digital temperature control.

Community Reception

The Arizer Solo 2 was broadly well-received within the vaporizer community, particularly as a recommendation for users transitioning away from combustion. It was frequently cited as a dependable "daily driver" — one community member noted that the Arizer Air had been their daily driver for years before upgrading to the Solo 2. The device was praised for its simplicity, build quality, glass vapor path, and consistent performance.

Pros:

  • Flavor quality: The all-glass stem vapor path was appreciated for delivering clean, pleasant flavor, especially at lower to mid temperatures.
  • Ease of use: Simple interface, precise temperature control, and minimal learning curve made it an ideal first quality vaporizer.
  • Reliability: The Solo 2 gained a reputation as a workhorse device with few mechanical failures.
  • Affordability: Frequently available at attractive price points, especially during sales events. Community marketplace listings showed used units selling for $99–$125, making it accessible on the secondhand market.
  • Battery life: Generally considered strong for its class, supporting multiple sessions per charge.

Cons:

  • Airflow restriction: This was the most consistently noted drawback. Multiple community members described the Solo 2's draw resistance as restrictive, requiring long, slow sips rather than quick, open inhales. One user who owned a Solo 2, Mighty+, and Airvape Legacy Pro stated that none of them were "really open" in terms of airflow, and specifically sought alternatives offering shorter, faster breaths.
  • Airpath cleanliness concerns: A knowledgeable community member pointed out that the Solo 2's airpath is "not 100% clean," noting that air enters through the 12V charger port in addition to the designated inlet air holes, meaning intake air may pass over internal electronics. This was considered a significant shortcoming by users who prioritized pure airpath design.
  • Vapor harshness at higher temperatures: One long-time Arizer user reported that the vapor from the Solo became too harsh over time, noting increased sensitivity as they aged. This is consistent with the device's session nature — extended draws at elevated temperatures can produce warm, dry vapor.
  • Session-only operation: As a session vaporizer, the Solo 2 does not support on-demand use, which was a limitation for users who preferred quick single hits.

Overall, the community positioned the Solo 2 as an excellent value proposition and a strong session vaporizer, while acknowledging that users seeking open airflow, on-demand capability, or absolute airpath purity would eventually look elsewhere.

Tips & Techniques

  • Loading the stem: Arizer's signature technique involves packing ground herb directly into the glass aroma tube. A medium grind works well — not too fine, as this can increase draw resistance and lead to herb pulling through the screen. Some users preferred to gently tamp the herb without over-packing.
  • Stem insertion: Insert the loaded glass stem into the heated chamber with a slight twist to ensure a snug fit. The herb sits inside the stem above the heater, receiving a combination of conducted heat from the glass walls and convected hot air from below.
  • Draw technique: The Solo 2 rewards long, slow, steady draws rather than hard pulls. Community members consistently noted that attempting quick, forceful inhales does not work well with this device due to its restricted airflow. A slow sip technique maximizes vapor production and flavor.
  • Temperature stepping: Many users practiced "temp stepping" — starting at a lower temperature for flavor and gradually increasing through the session. This technique extracts lighter terpenes first, then progresses to more complete extraction at higher temps.
  • Dry burn cycles: Running the device through a few heating cycles without herb before first use was standard practice to off-gas any manufacturing residues, as noted by multiple sellers and users.
  • Stem as a measuring tool: The glass stem bowl naturally holds an appropriate dose. Users could pack it loosely for lighter sessions or more firmly for denser vapor.

Temperature Guide

The Solo 2 offers precise temperature control from 50°C to 220°C (122°F–428°F) in 1°C increments. Community-discussed temperature ranges include:

Temperature RangeEffect Profile
170–185°C (338–365°F)Flavor-focused, light effects. Best for terpene appreciation and "flavor chasing." Thin, wispy vapor.
185–200°C (365–392°F)Balanced flavor and vapor production. Good middle ground for most users.
200–210°C (392–410°F)Increased vapor density, stronger effects. Flavor begins to diminish.
210–220°C (410–428°F)Maximum extraction. Dense clouds, strongest effects. One community member specifically noted the device "hits strong" at 220°C. Flavor is diminished and vapor can be harsh, especially without water filtration.

Community members who described themselves as "flavor chasers" generally started sessions in the lower ranges and stepped up through the session to finish the bowl efficiently.

Maintenance & Cleaning

  • Glass stems: The glass aroma tubes are the primary components requiring regular cleaning. Soaking in isopropyl alcohol (90%+ recommended) dissolves resin buildup effectively. Stems can also be simmered in milk or a fat-containing liquid to reclaim active compounds — a technique common among Arizer users.
  • Stem screens: The small screens in the glass stems should be checked periodically for clogs and cleaned or replaced as needed.
  • Heating chamber: The ceramic oven should be kept free of debris. A dry brush or cotton swab can remove loose particles. Avoid getting liquid cleaners into the oven or the charging port.
  • ABV (Already Been Vaped) removal: Empty the stem while still warm for easiest cleaning. Used herb tends to fall out easily from a warm stem.
  • Cleaning powder: At least one community member recommended powdered brewery wash as an effective cleaning agent for glass components and water pieces used alongside the Solo 2.
  • Charger port awareness: Given the community observation that air enters through the 12V charger port, keeping this area clean is advisable, though this is more of a design consideration than a maintenance step.

Common Issues & Fixes

  • Restricted airflow: The most frequently discussed issue. Users seeking more open airflow explored aftermarket stems, WPA adapters through water pieces (which can slightly reduce perceived restriction), or ultimately moved to different devices with more open airflow designs.
  • Harsh vapor at high temperatures: Using the Solo 2 through a water pipe adapter and bubbler significantly smooths the vapor. Temperature stepping rather than starting high also mitigates this.
  • Air intake through charger port: One community member raised the concern that air entering through the 12V charging port means the airpath is not fully isolated from internal electronics. There is no user-side fix for this design characteristic; users who prioritized airpath purity sometimes moved to devices with fully isolated air paths.
  • Stem fit issues: Occasionally stems may fit too loosely or too tightly. The o-ring at the top of the heating chamber can be replaced to restore a proper seal.
  • Battery degradation: As with all lithium-ion battery devices, capacity diminishes over time. The internal battery is not user-replaceable without disassembly, which contrasts with the removable-battery design of the Arizer Air series.

Accessories & Modifications

The Arizer Solo 2 benefited from a robust ecosystem of both OEM and aftermarket accessories:

  • Glass stems: Multiple lengths available — standard 70mm, shorter 60mm, and plastic-tipped variants for reduced breakage risk. Aftermarket artisan glass stems with various designs were popular.
  • Water Pipe Adapters (WPA/GonG): Available in both 14mm and 18mm glass-on-glass configurations. The WPA stem replaces the standard aroma tube and allows the Solo 2 to be used directly with water pieces. Community marketplace listings consistently included WPA adapters alongside the unit, indicating their popularity. One community member sold a complete setup with a 14mm WPA and Mobius Ion Matrix bubbler.
  • PlanetVape Stealth Adapter: A notable aftermarket accessory mentioned in the community that allowed the Solo stem to fit inside a beverage cup for discreet public use — a popular stealth technique.
  • Stem caps: Silicone caps for the ends of loaded glass stems, enabling pre-loading and portable carry. Multiple stems could be pre-packed for sessions away from home.
  • Carrying cases and pouches: Both OEM and aftermarket options were available for protecting the unit and glass stems during transport.
  • Bundles: At least one dedicated retailer (420EDC, based in Maine) specialized in outfitting Arizer 2nd Generation Portables including the Solo 2, offering customizable bundles with accessories.

Comparisons

The Solo 2 was frequently compared to other vaporizers within the community, both within and outside the Arizer product line:

vs. Arizer Air / Air MAX:

A user choosing between the Solo 2 and the newer Arizer Air MAX received community advice suggesting that the Air MAX might address some of the Solo 2's shortcomings, particularly the airpath cleanliness issue. The Solo 2 was generally considered the more powerful session performer within the Arizer portable lineup, while the Air series offered the advantage of removable batteries.

vs. Storz & Bickel Mighty / Mighty+:

Both the Solo 2 and Mighty were described by one owner as having restricted airflow compared to what they desired. The Mighty was generally considered a more powerful session vaporizer with better vapor cooling, while the Solo 2 offered a cleaner glass vapor path and more affordable price point. Both were characterized as session-style devices requiring slow, deliberate draws.

vs. TinyMight:

The TinyMight was frequently recommended as an upgrade path for Solo 2 owners seeking open airflow. A community member described the TinyMight as having "much better airflow than all those portables" (referring to the Solo 2, Mighty, and Airvape Legacy Pro), noting that "pure convection on demand typically has open airflow." The TinyMight was positioned as a different class of device — on-demand convection versus the Solo 2's session hybrid approach.

vs. Convection On-Demand Devices (Tubo, Tetra P80, Firewood, Tafee Bowle):

The community noted that the Solo 2's flavor, while good for its class, was "not near Tubo/Mi3/Splinter/HI" according to one experienced member. Devices with fully clean airpaths and pure convection heating were considered superior for flavor purity. The Solo 2 was viewed as an accessible, reliable entry point, while these more specialized devices represented a higher tier of performance at correspondingly higher prices and with steeper learning curves.

In summary, the community positioned the Arizer Solo 2 as one of the best value propositions in portable vaporization — easy to use, reliable, and producing good flavor and effects — while acknowledging its limitations in airflow openness, airpath purity, and extraction speed compared to more advanced or specialized devices.
