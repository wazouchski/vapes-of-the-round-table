# 7th Floor Da Buddha (DBV)

Overview

The 7th Floor Da Buddha (commonly abbreviated as DBV) is a desktop whip-style dry herb vaporizer manufactured by 7th Floor LLC, based in Colorado Springs, Colorado. The Da Buddha is a convection-dominant vaporizer that uses a ceramic heating element powered by standard AC mains electricity. It has been in production since the mid-to-late 2000s and became one of the most widely recommended entry-level desktop vaporizers within the vaporizer enthusiast community, valued for its simplicity, durability, and vapor quality.

The unit features a cylindrical, anodized aluminum housing with a ground glass heater cover and a glass wand ("whip") that the user holds against the heating element to draw vapor. Temperature is controlled via an analog dial (rheostat) rather than digital controls, making the DBV a straightforward and mechanically reliable device. It is often considered the more affordable sibling of 7th Floor's Silver Surfer Vaporizer (SSV), sharing the same core heating technology in a simplified form factor.

Community Reception

The Da Buddha was widely regarded within the community as one of the best value propositions in the desktop vaporizer category. Users consistently praised several attributes:

Pros:

  • Exceptional vapor flavor, particularly at lower temperature settings. One community member described the taste experience at low settings with notable enthusiasm, remarking on the quality of flavor even before visible vapor was produced.
  • Build quality and longevity. The DBV's simple design — essentially a ceramic heater, a rheostat, and glass — meant there was very little to break. The absence of digital electronics was seen as a reliability advantage.
  • Affordability. Priced well below many competing desktop units, the DBV was frequently recommended to first-time buyers. At the time of community discussions, new units could be found for approximately $130–160 USD (including shipping to the UK).
  • Efficiency. Users noted the DBV was economical with material.
  • Upgrade path. The shared glass joint standard with the Silver Surfer meant users could swap in SSV heater covers and wands for enhanced performance — a modification community members referred to as turning the Da Buddha into "Da Beast."

Cons:

  • Lack of precise temperature control. The analog dial provided no numerical readout, requiring users to learn their unit's behavior through experience. Temperature sweet spots could vary between individual units.
  • Narrow usable dial range on some units. Some users, particularly those with units sold outside the US, reported that the effective vaporization range was compressed into a very small portion of the dial — sometimes just the lowest settings, near the "click of extinction" (the point where the heater would shut off). This issue was corroborated by multiple community members.
  • Whip-only design. Unlike competitors such as the Arizer Extreme Q, the DBV offered no balloon/bag filling capability out of the box, limiting its versatility for group sessions.
  • Learning curve. Draw technique significantly affects vapor quality, requiring new users to develop an appropriate draw speed and rhythm.

Despite these limitations, the community widely agreed that the DBV punched well above its price point and remained a beloved device even among users who later upgraded to more expensive units. One seller on the community marketplace noted they were only parting with their DBV because they had acquired two VapeXhale Cloud units — testament to the fact that only significantly more expensive devices tended to displace it.

Tips & Techniques

Community members developed and shared a number of techniques for optimizing the Da Buddha experience:

Loading

  • Use a moderate grind — not too fine, not too coarse. Material should be loosely packed into the glass wand; overpacking restricts airflow and reduces convection efficiency.
  • Screens should be properly seated in the wand to prevent material from falling into the heater.

The Low-Temperature Session Ritual

One experienced community member described a refined low-temperature technique that became a widely cited approach:

Turn the dial to minimum (just above the shutoff point) while preparing your material.

Begin drawing at this low setting. The first 2–4 draws will produce pure flavor with no visible vapor — this is the desensitization/dehydration phase as the herb warms.

After this initial phase, visible vapor begins to appear and can be sustained for approximately ten draws.

When both vapor and flavor suddenly diminish, the bowl is spent.

Throughout the session, the dial position remains unchanged — the entire extraction occurs at one low setting.

This technique was praised for maximizing flavor and producing a thorough extraction without approaching combustion temperatures.

Draw Technique

  • Slow, steady draws are essential. The DBV is a convection device, and the airflow over the herb is what creates vapor. Drawing too quickly pulls cool air through the material too fast; drawing too slowly can lead to excessive heat buildup.
  • Some users reported gently rotating or tapping the wand between draws to redistribute material for more even extraction.

Positioning the Wand

  • The angle and depth at which the wand is inserted into the heater cover affects the distance between the herb and the heating element, providing an additional dimension of temperature control beyond the dial setting.

Temperature Guide

Because the Da Buddha uses an unmarked analog rheostat dial, temperature guidance is expressed in terms of dial position rather than precise degrees. Community members described dial positions using a clock-face analogy:

Dial Position (Clock)Approximate EffectDescription
Just above shutoff (~7 o'clock)Flavor-focused, low vaporPure terpene extraction; minimal visible vapor initially, building to light visible clouds. Ideal for flavor chasers.
~12 o'clock to 1 o'clockOptimal range (US models)Balanced flavor and vapor production. This was the most commonly cited "sweet spot" for US-market units.
Above 1 o'clockHeavy clouds, reduced flavorHigher extraction of remaining compounds, darker ABV. Risk of combustion increases at higher settings.

Important note: Community members observed that the usable dial range could vary significantly between individual units and especially between US and international (European) models. Some European-market units exhibited a much narrower effective range, with optimal vaporization occurring at the very bottom of the dial — just before the unit would click off. This was attributed to differences in mains voltage (120V vs 230V) and the rheostat's behavior under different electrical conditions. One community member with technical background discussed the possibility that voltage differences affected the rheostat's operating characteristics, noting that the heating element's response could vary depending on whether power control was managed by a simple variable resistor or an electronic equivalent.

Users were advised to start low and work up gradually with any new DBV unit, as each unit's dial behaves slightly differently.

Maintenance & Cleaning

The Da Buddha's glass-path design makes maintenance relatively straightforward:

Regular Maintenance

  • Screens: Replace or clean wand screens regularly. Clogged screens restrict airflow and degrade vapor quality. Screens can be soaked in isopropyl alcohol (ISO) and rinsed.
  • Glass wand: Soak in isopropyl alcohol (91% or higher recommended) to dissolve resin buildup. A pipe cleaner can be used for stubborn deposits.
  • Whip tubing: Silicone tubing should be periodically replaced or cleaned by running hot water and ISO through it. Over time, tubing accumulates residue and can affect flavor.
  • Glass mouthpiece: Clean with ISO and rinse thoroughly.

Deep Cleaning

  • Heater cover (ground glass joint): Carefully clean the glass heater cover with ISO, being cautious not to drip liquid into the heating element. Some users recommended using a cotton swab dampened with ISO rather than submerging.
  • Ceramic heating element: Should not be cleaned with liquids. If material falls onto the element, it can be carefully brushed away when the unit is cool, or burned off by running the unit at a high temperature with no wand inserted (a "burn-off" cycle).

Reclaiming

  • Community members noted that the honey-colored resin ("reclaim") that accumulates inside glass components could be collected and used, a common practice among enthusiasts.

Common Issues & Fixes

Narrow Usable Dial Range (Especially International Units)

Problem: The effective vaporization temperature range is compressed into a very small portion of the dial, making fine temperature control difficult. Some users found they could only vaporize at the lowest possible dial setting before the unit clicked off.

Cause: Community discussion pointed to the interaction between mains voltage (230V in Europe vs. 120V in the US) and the rheostat's design. The higher input voltage may cause the heating element to reach vaporization temperatures much lower on the dial.

Fixes/Workarounds:

  • Learn to work within the narrow range — the low-temperature session technique described above was partly developed in response to this issue.
  • Use wand positioning (depth of insertion) as a secondary temperature control.
  • Some users considered using a voltage regulator or variac, though this was not widely adopted.

Herb Falling into Heater

Problem: If the wand is held at certain angles or screens are poorly seated, ground herb can fall through and contact the heating element.

Fix: Ensure screens are properly seated and consider using slightly coarser grinds. Some users placed a second screen or used a different screen configuration.

Glass Breakage

Problem: Glass wands and heater covers are fragile.

Fix: 7th Floor sells replacement parts. Community members noted that third-party glass-on-glass (GonG) adapters and wands were also compatible and sometimes more durable or functional.

Accessories & Modifications

The Da Buddha's glass-on-glass joint system opened up a wide range of accessories and modifications:

GonG (Glass-on-Glass) Accessories

  • GonG heater covers and wands replaced the standard whip setup with ground glass joints, allowing direct connection to water pipes and bubblers. This was one of the most popular upgrades.
  • Water Pipe Adapters (WPAs): With a GonG wand, the DBV could be connected directly to any water pipe with a compatible joint size, enabling water-filtered vapor. This was a highly recommended modification within the community.

SSV Parts Compatibility — "Da Beast"

  • The Da Buddha shares heater architecture with the Silver Surfer Vaporizer (SSV), and community members discovered that SSV heater covers and wands could be used on the DBV. This modification was affectionately termed turning the Da Buddha into "Da Beast." One marketplace listing specifically advertised a DBV equipped with both standard GonG accessories and an SSV heater cover and wand combination.
  • The SSV wand's angled design was considered by some to offer improved ergonomics and vapor path geometry compared to the standard DBV wand.

Custom and DIY Modifications

Community members reported a variety of custom modifications documented in the extensive English-language discussion thread (which spanned over 100 pages):

  • Custom ceramic covers for the heating element
  • Stainless steel wands as alternatives to glass
  • Mini screen configurations for micro-dosing
  • Aroma cup usage for aromatherapy with essential oils or herbs not intended for inhalation

7th Floor Factory Upgrades

7th Floor offered a service where users could send their Da Buddha units in for upgrades and refurbishment, as noted by at least one community member who had taken advantage of this program.

Comparisons

Da Buddha vs. Arizer Extreme Q

This was one of the most frequently asked comparison questions from prospective first-time buyers:

Community consensus generally held that the DBV was the better pure whip vaporizer, delivering superior flavor and vapor quality through its simpler, more direct air path. The Extreme Q was recommended for users who wanted the flexibility of balloon bags or preferred digital temperature control. For users primarily interested in whip vaping, the DBV was the more common recommendation.

Da Buddha vs. Silver Surfer Vaporizer (SSV)

The SSV, also manufactured by 7th Floor, was considered the DBV's upscale sibling. Both shared the same core ceramic heating element technology. Key differences included:

  • The SSV featured an angled heater cover and wand design, considered more ergonomic.
  • The SSV offered more customization options (custom glass, various aesthetic choices).
  • The SSV was priced higher.
  • Community members noted that the optimal dial range on the SSV was typically around "12 o'clock to 1 o'clock," similar to US-market DBV units. The parts interchangeability between the two units meant DBV owners could incrementally upgrade toward SSV-like performance.

Da Buddha vs. VapeXhale Cloud

While not a direct competitor in terms of price, several community members who owned both devices acknowledged that the Cloud was a significant step up — but at a much higher price point. The fact that users upgraded from the DBV to the Cloud (rather than to other mid-range desktops) was seen as a testament to the DBV's quality: it took a premium device to meaningfully surpass it.
