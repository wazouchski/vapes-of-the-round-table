# Arizer Solo

Overview

The Arizer Solo is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. First announced and released in mid-2011, the Solo quickly became one of the most recommended portable vaporizers in the community, earning a reputation for reliability, vapor quality, and exceptional value that endured for many years after its initial release. The device was designed by Arizer's founder, Steve, and was notable at launch for its combination of a stainless steel body and borosilicate glass aroma stems — a design approach that set it apart from competitors at the time.

The Solo utilizes a hybrid heating method, primarily conduction with some convection contribution as air is drawn through the heated chamber and glass stem. It is powered by an internal rechargeable lithium-ion battery and can also be operated while charging via its AC adapter, effectively allowing it to function as a semi-desktop unit for home use. The device is roughly the size of a Red Bull can, making it portable but not pocket-sized.

The Solo was succeeded by the Arizer Solo II, which added a digital display and precise temperature control, though the original Solo maintained a devoted following even after the release of its successor and other Arizer portables (the Arizer Air and ArGo).

Note on Temperature Settings: The Solo does not feature an LCD screen with a precise temperature readout. Instead, it uses numbered heat settings (1–7) indicated by LED lights. The battery charge level is indicated by a separate set of LED indicators, which caused some initial confusion among new users at launch. One community member initially speculated the numbers indicated battery charge rather than temperature, highlighting the lack of documentation in early discussions.

Community Reception

The Arizer Solo is one of the most universally and consistently praised portable vaporizers in the history of the vaporizer community. It was frequently described as a device that "stands the test of time," and its reputation only grew stronger in the years following its release. Community sentiment can be summarized by one experienced member's statement: "Sometimes a product is so good that it stands the test of time. The Solo is one of those products. How do I know... I'd buy another without concern for what else is out there."

Praise

  • Flavor Quality: The all-glass air path was repeatedly cited as one of the Solo's greatest strengths. The borosilicate glass stems deliver clean, pure-tasting vapor that community members regarded as exceptional for a portable device. One member described the experience of holding and drawing from the Solo as feeling "classy."
  • Reliability and Durability: The Solo was consistently described as "built like a tank." In an era when many competing portables suffered from reliability issues, the Solo's longevity was a major selling point. Community members routinely reported years of daily use without failure. This was especially notable when contrasted with devices like the Flowermate V5.0S, which some users reported failing within months.
  • Value for Money: At a typical street price of approximately $130–$160 USD (often available for $160 or less through retailers like PuffItUp with coupon codes), the Solo was the community's default recommendation for a budget-conscious buyer seeking quality. One well-respected member put it this way: the Solo "sips your product like it was paying for it."
  • Efficiency: A peer-reviewed study published in the *Journal of Cannabis Therapeutics* (PMID: 26784441) was frequently cited in community discussions. That study found the Arizer Solo delivered 82.7% of THC and 70.0% of CBD in its vapor — significantly outperforming the Volcano Medic (58.4% THC, 51.4% CBD), the Plenty (66.8% THC, 56.1% CBD), and the DaVinci (54.6% THC, 56.7% CBD). This data point became a cornerstone of the Solo's reputation for extraction efficiency.
  • Versatility: The Solo was praised as a true "jack of all trades" portable. It functioned well as a handheld session vape, connected easily to water pipes, could run off AC power for desktop-like sessions, and could even be charged in a car. As one member noted: "It covers a lot of bases."
  • Beginner-Friendliness: The Solo was the single most commonly recommended first vaporizer in the community. However, experienced members were careful to note that it was recommended to beginners not because it was merely a "beginner's vape," but because it was genuinely an excellent device. As one veteran member stated: "When someone asks about which vape to use as a beginner I almost always suggest the Solo. Not because it's a beginner's vape but because it's a great vape to start off with."
  • Medical Suitability: The Solo was specifically recommended for users with respiratory sensitivities, including asthma. One community member reported having two friends with asthma who "get along well with Arizers," noting that the Solo's hybrid heating method did not require the long draws that pure convection vapes demand — an important consideration for those with limited lung capacity.

Criticisms

  • Size and Portability: The Solo's most consistently cited drawback was its size. While technically portable, it was not pocket-friendly. Community members frequently noted it would not fit in pants pockets, and for users requiring true on-the-go stealth, other devices were suggested. This limitation led Arizer to develop the more compact Air and later the ArGo.
  • Session-Style Usage: The Solo is a session vaporizer, meaning it is designed to be used over a 12-minute session rather than for quick one-hit use. Community members noted this was not ideal for users who preferred an on-demand style. One member observed: "The Solo is brainless but you don't want to fire it up for just one hit."
  • No Swappable Battery: Unlike the Arizer Air, the Solo's internal battery cannot be user-replaced in the field. While battery life was generally praised (8–12 sessions per charge), the inability to carry spare batteries was a disadvantage for extended trips away from power.
  • Lack of Precise Temperature Control: The 7-step heat settings, while functional, were less precise than the digital temperature control found on later models. Some users desired more granular adjustment.
  • Airflow Restriction: Some community members found the stock stems somewhat restrictive on airflow. This was widely addressed through aftermarket stem options (see Accessories & Modifications).

Long-Term Standing

Even as newer portables entered the market, the Solo retained a dedicated user base. A notable community observation was that many users who upgraded to the Arizer Air found themselves returning to the Solo, with multiple members reporting the sentiment that "the Air is a great vape... but I still prefer the Solo." One long-time community member with an extensive vaporizer collection stated that the Solo was the portable they reached for most often, even among much more expensive alternatives.

Tips & Techniques

Loading the Stem

  • Grind Quality: A medium to medium-fine grind was generally recommended. Too fine a grind could lead to particles falling through the stem into the stainless steel heating chamber and affecting taste.
  • Packing Technique: The community-preferred method was to push herb gently into the stem bowl and pack it snugly but not too tightly — firm enough to stay in place but loose enough to allow airflow. After packing, many users recommended lightly blowing over the open end of the stem to dislodge any small loose particles that might otherwise fall onto the heater and create unpleasant taste.
  • Domed Screen Method: One widely praised technique was placing a domed screen inside the glass stem. This was reported to improve airflow and prevent herb from falling onto the heater while also allowing for a more even extraction. Multiple experienced members recommended this modification, with one stating it significantly improved the Solo's delivery system.
  • Inverted Loading: Because gravity can cause herb to fall from the stem into the oven when inserting it, some users would load the stem and insert it into the Solo with the unit inverted (upside down), then flip the unit right-side up once the stem was seated.

Drawing Technique

  • Slow, Steady Draws: The community widely agreed that slow, long sips produced the best results with the Solo. Aggressive or fast inhalation would draw too much cool air through the stem, reducing vapor production and temperature consistency.
  • Session Pacing: A typical session involved starting on a lower heat setting for the first few draws to extract flavor terpenes, then gradually stepping up the temperature over the 12-minute session to extract remaining active compounds and produce thicker vapor.

Concentrates

The Solo can handle concentrates using the sandwich method: a layer of dry herb, then a small amount of concentrate (wax, oil, or hash), topped with another layer of herb. Some community members also reported success using small pieces of organic cotton as a medium for concentrates in the glass stem, though dry herb sandwiching was more commonly recommended.

Stealth Use

Despite its size, creative community members found ways to use the Solo discreetly. A popular trick involved inserting the glass stem through the lid of a fast-food drink cup, allowing the user to appear to be sipping a beverage. The third-party PlanetVape Stealth Adapter was also designed specifically for this purpose, fitting the Solo stem into standard drink cup lids. This technique became so well-known that images of it were widely shared across vaporizer communities.

Session Continuation

The Solo can be used for partial sessions. Users could vape for a few minutes, remove the stem, and return to the same bowl later. The herb in the stem would retain its partially-vaped state. One community member noted: "You could go for 5 min and take the stem out when finished. Later you can put the stem back in and finish the bowl."

Temperature Guide

The Solo's 7 heat settings correspond to approximate temperatures as follows (exact temperatures were debated, but community consensus settled on these approximate values):

SettingApproximate TemperatureRecommended Use
1~50°C / ~122°FToo low for most herb vaping; primarily for aromatherapy
2~185°C / ~365°FLow-end vaporization; maximum flavor, light effects
3~190°C / ~374°FExcellent flavor, mild to moderate effects
4~195°C / ~383°FGood balance of flavor and vapor production; popular starting point
5~200°C / ~392°FCommunity "sweet spot"; strong vapor production with good flavor
6~205°C / ~401°FHeavy vapor; diminished flavor; stronger effects
7~210°C / ~410°FMaximum extraction; thick clouds; heaviest effects

Community-Agreed Sweet Spots

  • Flavor chasers: Settings 3–4 (approximately 190–195°C / 374–383°F). Best for tasting the terpene profile of the herb.
  • Balanced sessions: Setting 5 (approximately 200°C / 392°F). The most commonly recommended single-setting sweet spot.
  • Cloud chasers and maximum extraction: Settings 6–7 (approximately 205–210°C / 401–410°F). Produces the thickest visible vapor and strongest effects, at the cost of flavor.

Step-Up Technique

The most recommended approach was to start a session at Setting 3 or 4 and incrementally increase the temperature by one step every few minutes. This "step-up" method extracts the full spectrum of cannabinoids and terpenes, beginning with the lower-boiling-point flavor compounds and finishing with the higher-boiling-point compounds responsible for heavier effects.

Charring vs. Combustion

Community discussion addressed confusion about black herb residue versus brown ABV (Already Been Vaped material). On the Solo, settings up to 7 could produce very dark brown ABV. Some users reported occasional blackening, particularly of herb directly contacting the heater at the base of the stem. Community opinion was somewhat divided on whether blackening constituted combustion. The general consensus was that true combustion produces white or grey ash, while black charring without ash formation indicated extremely thorough extraction but not actual ignition. However, some members maintained that any black coloration indicated partial combustion. If consistently dark ABV was a concern, users were advised to avoid overpacking, ensure adequate airflow, and stay at or below Setting 5 for most sessions.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Stems

  • Frequency: Clean stems regularly; every few sessions for optimal flavor, or when visible residue builds up.
  • Method: Soak glass stems in isopropyl alcohol (90% or higher) for 15–30 minutes. Use pipe cleaners or cotton swabs to remove any remaining residue. Rinse thoroughly with hot water before use.
  • Reclaiming Stem Honey: The honey-colored resin that accumulates inside the glass stem is potent reclaim containing concentrated cannabinoids. Many community members saved their dirty stems and periodically extracted this residue by soaking the stems in warm whole milk (the fat content dissolves the cannabinoids), creating an infused beverage. Alternatively, stems could be soaked in ethanol for reclamation.

Heating Chamber (Oven)

  • The stainless steel oven should be kept clean of herb debris. Small particles that fall from the stem into the oven during loading will combust and produce an unpleasant taste. Use a cotton swab dipped in isopropyl alcohol to carefully clean the oven when cool.
  • Prevention: The blowing technique described in Tips & Techniques (lightly blowing loose particles off the loaded stem before insertion) was the community's primary preventive measure.

Exterior

  • The stainless steel body can be wiped down with a damp cloth. Avoid submerging the unit.

Common Issues & Fixes

Herb Falling into the Oven

Problem: Small herb particles fall from the stem into the stainless steel heating chamber during loading, producing unpleasant burnt taste.

Solution: Pack the stem firmly (but not too tightly), blow loose particles off the loaded stem before insertion, consider using a domed screen in the stem, or load the stem with the unit inverted.

Charring/Blackening of Herb Near the Heater

Problem: Herb at the bottom of the stem (closest to the heater) turns black while the rest remains brown.

Solution: This was more commonly reported with the Arizer Air than the original Solo. Ensure the stem is properly seated, avoid excessively high temperature settings, and do not overpack the stem. A screen at the base of the bowl can create a small buffer between the herb and the heater.

Battery Degradation Over Time

Problem: After extended use (typically years), battery capacity may diminish.

Solution: The Solo's internal battery is not user-swappable without disassembly. Some community members performed DIY battery replacements, though this required soldering skills. Using the AC adapter at home can extend the practical lifespan of the device.

Controls Becoming Unresponsive

Problem: Occasional reports of the button controls becoming "flaky" over time.

Solution: Community consensus was that this was rare. One member reassured a prospective buyer: "As to the controls getting flaky, I wouldn't worry about it. They are pretty dependable."

Accessories & Modifications

Aftermarket Glass Stems

  • PlanetVape PVHE (High Efficiency) Stems: One of the most popular aftermarket accessories for the Solo. The PVHE stem featured a modified bowl design that improved airflow compared to stock stems. Community members reported that it made achieving thick hits easier, particularly for newer users who had a learning curve with the stock stems. The PVHE Turbo variant had a larger bowl size.
  • Ed's TnT Wooden Stems: High-quality wooden stems that were praised for improving the draw restriction and aesthetics of the Solo. Community members noted that these stems "well improved the restriction of air" on the Solo.
  • PlanetVape Stealth Adapter: A specially designed adapter that allowed the Solo stem to fit through a standard drink cup lid, enabling discreet public use.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

The Solo was widely praised for its compatibility with glass water pipes (bongs and bubblers). Several options existed:

  • PlanetVape WPA: A glass adapter that replaced the standard stem and allowed the Solo to connect to 14mm or 18mm water pipe joints. The Solo was described as "capable of milking glass of all sizes" through water.
  • DIY solutions: Some community members used the stock glass stems directly with appropriately sized water pipe joints, as the stem's outer diameter could create a friction fit with certain glass joints.
  • Whip Adapter: PlanetVape also produced a whip adapter for the Solo, allowing hands-free use with a silicone or vinyl whip. One member noted "the Solo is great with a whip."

Screens

  • Domed screens placed inside the glass stem bowl were a highly recommended modification. They improved airflow, prevented herb from contacting the heater, and allowed for more even extraction.
  • Sandwich screens (flat screens placed above and below the herb in the stem) were used by some members to contain herb and prevent debris from falling into the oven.

Charging Accessories

  • The Solo could be used while plugged into its AC adapter, effectively converting it to a desktop unit for extended home sessions.
  • Car charger adapters were available, making the Solo suitable for road trips and camping.

Comparisons

Arizer Solo vs. Arizer Air

The Air was Arizer's follow-up portable, released as a slimmer, more pocket-friendly alternative. Key differences discussed by the community:

  • Portability: The Air is significantly smaller and lighter, with a user-swappable 18650 battery — a major advantage for on-the-go use, as spare batteries can be carried.
  • Battery Life: The Solo's dual internal batteries provided roughly double the battery life of the Air's single 18650 cell per charge. Without spare batteries, the Air had a notable disadvantage in session count.
  • Performance: Community opinion was notably tilted in favor of the Solo for vapor quality and extraction, despite the shared heating technology. Multiple members reported preferring the Solo even after purchasing the Air. A common sentiment was that "the Air is a great vape... but I still prefer the Solo."
  • Charring: Some users reported more herb charring/blackening with the Air than the Solo, though this was not universal.

Arizer Solo vs. Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

The MFLB was another frequently compared entry-level portable:

  • Usage Style: The MFLB is on-demand (single hits as desired) while the Solo is session-based (12-minute cycles). The MFLB offered a more pipe-like, one-hit experience.
  • Learning Curve: The MFLB required a specific technique to master, while the Solo was described as "brainless" and "idiot proof."
  • Vapor Quality: The Solo was regarded as producing significantly better flavor and thicker vapor.
  • Social Use: The Solo was considered more shareable, as the MFLB's technique-dependent nature made it difficult for inexperienced users. One member noted: "The MFLB is a good fit for you as well, but you may find it limiting should you want to experiment or share with a friend."
  • Portability: The MFLB was substantially smaller and more discreet.

Arizer Solo vs. Pax

The Pax (and Pax 2) represented the premium end of the portable market:

  • Portability and Stealth: The Pax was far more pocket-friendly and discreet.
  • Performance: The Solo was generally regarded as producing better-tasting vapor due to its all-glass air path.
  • Maintenance: The Pax required more frequent and intensive cleaning of its conduction oven and vapor path. The Solo's glass stems were trivial to clean by comparison.
  • Price: The Solo was significantly less expensive.
  • Smell: The Pax was noted for retaining odor more than the Solo, due to its enclosed oven design.

Arizer Solo vs. Flowermate V5.0S

The Flowermate was a popular budget option:

  • Reliability: The Solo was regarded as substantially more reliable. Community members reported Flowermate units failing within months, while Solos lasted years.
  • Price: The Flowermate was cheaper, but the Solo's longevity and superior performance made it the better long-term value.

Arizer Solo vs. Desktop Vaporizers (Volcano, E-Nano, Log Vapes)

  • vs. Volcano: The peer-reviewed extraction efficiency study showed the Solo actually outperforming the Volcano in THC delivery percentage (82.7% vs. 58.4%), a data point that surprised many in the community. However, the Volcano remained preferred for group sessions and bag delivery.
  • vs. Log Vapes (E-Nano, Underdog): Log vapes were acknowledged as superior for raw extraction power and on-demand use through water. The E-Nano was described as "very much a heavy hitter" that was "definitely superior to the Solo in terms of extraction and vapor production." However, the Solo's portability and versatility gave it a different niche. One community member summarized: "The Solo is a great general purpose vape... very versatile and I think everyone should own one."
  • Desktop Competition: Multiple community members stated that the Solo was "strong enough to stand against some of the better desktops," particularly when used through a water pipe.

Arizer Solo vs. Storz & Bickel Mighty

The Mighty was generally considered a superior performer but at more than double the price. Community members who owned both praised the Mighty as their top pick but acknowledged the Solo and Air as "very, very good" alternatives. For budget-conscious buyers, the Solo was the overwhelming recommendation.
