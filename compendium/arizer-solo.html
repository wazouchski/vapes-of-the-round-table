# Arizer Solo

Overview

The Arizer Solo is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. First announced and released in 2011, the Solo quickly became one of the most popular and widely recommended portable vaporizers in the enthusiast community. It was often described as a "cult classic" and remained a top recommendation for years after its debut, even as newer competitors entered the market.

The Solo employs a hybrid heating method, predominantly conduction with some convection as air is drawn through the heated chamber and glass stem. It is powered by an internal rechargeable lithium-ion battery and can also be used while plugged in via a power adapter. The unit is roughly the size of a can of Red Bull and features an all-glass vapor path through removable borosilicate glass stems that insert into a stainless steel heating chamber (commonly referred to as the "oven").

One of the Solo's most celebrated design features at launch was its glass aroma stem, which community members noted gave it a discreet, inconspicuous appearance compared to other portable vaporizers of its era. One early community member remarked that the glass top was "something every portable vape is missing to make it look inconspicuous," contrasting it with more obviously paraphernalia-like devices available at the time.

The Solo was eventually succeeded by the Arizer Solo II (Solo 2), which added a digital display and precise temperature control, though the original Solo continued to enjoy a strong following.

Community Reception

The Arizer Solo was one of the most beloved portable vaporizers in the enthusiast community, enjoying a remarkably long tenure as a top recommendation. The community widely regarded it as exceptional value for money and one of the most reliable portables ever produced.

Praise

  • Value proposition: The Solo was consistently cited as offering the best performance-to-price ratio in portable vaporizers. At typical retail prices between $100–$160 USD, community members frequently described it as "more bang for your buck" than nearly any competitor. One seasoned community member stated plainly: "I'd buy another without concern for what else is out there."
  • Build quality and durability: The unit was described as "built like a tank" and "rock solid." Members reported years of daily use with no degradation in performance. One member reported using an older model "every day for the last few years" without issues.
  • Flavor quality: The all-glass vapor path was universally praised for delivering excellent flavor. The combination of borosilicate glass stems and a stainless steel oven produced what many considered among the cleanest, most flavorful vapor available from a portable device. As one member put it, "The flavor that is in your cannabis will be heightened and highlighted by the Solo's delivery system."
  • Versatility: The Solo was praised for working well across a wide range of use cases — from solo microdosing sessions to sharing with friends, from direct draw to water filtration setups. One community member called it "the king of 'in between vaping'" and "a hybrid, as far as half home vape, half portable."
  • Scientific validation: Community members frequently cited a published study (Pomahacova et al., PMID 26784441) comparing cannabinoid delivery across vaporizers. The Solo achieved 82.7% THC delivery and 70.0% CBD delivery in the vapor — figures that significantly outperformed the Volcano Medic (58.4% THC / 51.4% CBD), the Plenty (66.8% THC / 56.1% CBD), and the DaVinci (54.6% THC / 56.7% CBD).
  • Longevity of relevance: Even years after its release, community members noted that people who upgraded to the Arizer Air often commented that they "still prefer the Solo." The device stood the test of time remarkably well.
  • Smooth vapor for medical users: The Solo was frequently recommended for users with respiratory sensitivities, including asthma. Community members reported that friends with asthma tolerated Arizer products well, and the hybrid heating approach did not require the long, deep draws that purely convection vaporizers demanded.

Criticisms

  • Draw resistance: The most commonly cited drawback was the Solo's restricted airflow. The stock glass stems required a slow, measured draw, and users accustomed to free-flowing airflow sometimes found it uncomfortable. At least one community member reported that the restrictive draw exacerbated TMJ (temporomandibular joint) pain. However, some members argued that the restricted draw was actually beneficial, as it naturally slowed the user's breathing to produce optimal vapor without requiring careful technique.
  • Session style only: The Solo was a session vaporizer with a 12-minute auto-shutoff timer. Each stem took approximately 8–12 minutes to fully extract, which was not ideal for users seeking quick, on-demand hits.
  • Size and discretion: While portable, the Solo was not a pocket vaporizer. Its Red Bull–can form factor combined with an externally protruding glass stem made it less than stealthy for use in public.
  • Non-replaceable battery: The Solo's internal battery could not be easily swapped by the user, which was a concern for heavy daily users worried about long-term battery degradation. One community member specifically cited this as a reason to consider the Extreme Q desktop instead.
  • Not a "beginner's vape" — a misnomer: Some in the community characterized the Solo as an "entry-level" or "beginner's" vaporizer. More experienced members pushed back against this label, arguing that calling it a beginner's vape undersold its capabilities. As one member wrote: "I almost always suggest the Solo... not because it's a beginner's vape but because it's a great vape to start off with."

Tips & Techniques

Loading

  • Grind quality: A medium to medium-fine grind was generally recommended. The herb should be ground but not powdered — overly fine material could fall through the screen or restrict airflow further.
  • Standard loading method: Pack the glass stem bowl by pressing it gently into a pile of ground herb. The herb should be loosely packed; do not tamp it down tightly as this increases draw resistance and can impair vapor production.
  • Inverted loading for micro-loads: One community member discovered that for extremely small loads (as little as 0.005g), the herb could be poured into the oven of an inverted Solo rather than packed in the stem. The stem was then inserted over the herb, effectively turning the Solo into a conduction-dominant vaporizer. This technique was reported to work surprisingly well for microdosing.
  • Domed screen modification: Adding a domed screen to the stock stems was a widely recommended modification that improved airflow and kept herb from contacting the bottom of the stem directly.

Draw Technique

  • Slow and steady: The most important technique for the Solo was learning to draw slowly. Community members repeatedly emphasized that the biggest mistake new users made was trying to inhale too quickly. One member explained: "The biggest problem people have is they try to suck faster than the air can move through it, which causes them to run out of breath. If you just slow your draw to the point that you aren't sucking, then it becomes effortless."
  • Long, gentle sips: Draws of 10–15 seconds at a relaxed pace produced the best vapor density without fighting the restricted airflow.

Session Management

  • Start low, step up: A commonly recommended approach was to begin a session at a lower heat setting (levels 3–4) for flavorful hits, then increase to higher settings (5–7) as the session progressed to fully extract the remaining cannabinoids.
  • Typical session length: Most users reported sessions of 8–12 minutes per stem load, though quicker 5-minute sessions were possible at higher temperatures.
  • Battery optimization: With battery life ranging from 60–120 minutes, users could expect approximately 8–12 sessions per full charge depending on temperature settings and session length.

Stealth Use

  • One inventive technique that circulated widely in the broader vaporizer community involved placing the Solo inside a large fast-food cup with a straw hole in the lid, drawing vapor through a straw. The PlanetVape "Stealth Adapter" was also designed specifically for this type of discreet use.

Temperature Guide

The Solo used a numbered heat setting system (1–7) rather than precise temperature readouts. Exact temperatures were not officially published by Arizer for the original Solo, but community testing and documentation established approximate ranges:

SettingApproximate TemperatureRecommended Use
1~50°C / ~122°FBelow vaporization threshold; sometimes used for aromatherapy
2~185°C / ~365°FLight flavor exploration
3~190°C / ~374°FGood starting point for flavor-focused sessions
4~195°C / ~383°FCommunity sweet spot for balanced flavor and vapor production
5~200°C / ~392°FThicker vapor with good flavor; popular for most users
6~205°C / ~401°FDense clouds; less flavor, more extraction
7~210°C / ~410°FMaximum extraction; thickest clouds, least flavor

Community Sweet Spots

  • Settings 4–5 were the most commonly recommended for everyday use, balancing flavor and vapor density.
  • Step-up sessions (starting at 3 and finishing at 6–7) were popular for maximizing both flavor and efficiency from a single load.
  • Setting 7 was commonly used when pairing the Solo with water filtration, as the water cooled the hotter vapor and made it comfortable to inhale.

Note: Actual temperatures may have varied between units and across different production runs. Community members occasionally reported slight variations.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Stems

  • Isopropyl alcohol (ISO) soak: The primary cleaning method. Stems were soaked in 90%+ isopropyl alcohol for 15–60 minutes, then rinsed with hot water. Stubborn resin could be removed with pipe cleaners or cotton swabs while soaking.
  • Reclaim collection: The honey-colored oil that accumulated inside the stems over time was considered valuable by many community members. Some would save dirty stems and harvest this reclaim by soaking in high-proof ethanol (such as Everclear) to create a tincture, or by gently warming the stems to collect the oil.
  • Frequency: Cleaning every 5–10 sessions was a reasonable cadence for most users, though some preferred to clean after every session for maximum flavor.

Oven / Heating Chamber

  • Dry swab after each use: A cotton swab wiped around the oven after each session kept the chamber clean and prevented residue buildup.
  • ISO wipe: For deeper cleaning, a cotton swab lightly dampened with isopropyl alcohol could be used to clean the stainless steel oven. Users were advised to ensure the oven was completely dry before the next use.
  • Periodic ISO cleaning: A more thorough ISO cleaning of the oven was recommended approximately once per week for daily users.

General

  • Burn-off before first use: Running the Solo through 2–3 heat cycles at the highest setting without herb loaded was recommended before first use to eliminate any residual manufacturing residue.
  • Screen maintenance: Screens in the glass stems should be checked periodically and replaced if clogged or damaged.

Common Issues & Fixes

Restricted Airflow / Draw Resistance

Problem: The Solo's most commonly reported issue. Users found it difficult to draw vapor, especially with stock stems.

Solutions:

  • Purchase PlanetVape High Efficiency Stems (PVHES), which featured wider air channels and significantly improved airflow. Community members considered these an almost essential upgrade.
  • Ensure the stem is clean; resin buildup dramatically worsened draw resistance.
  • Avoid overpacking the stem bowl.
  • Use a domed screen modification to improve air circulation.

Plastic/Chemical Smell on New Units

Problem: Some users, particularly in discussions originating outside the community, reported plastic or glue-like smells from new units.

Solutions:

  • The community generally considered this a non-issue for authentic Arizer products. The adhesive referenced in some reports was identified as a heatproof cement applied to the exterior of the oven, not in the air path.
  • Performing multiple burn-off cycles before first use was strongly recommended to eliminate any residual manufacturing odors.
  • Users were warned to beware of counterfeit units, which were known to exist and might use inferior materials.
  • Purchasing only from authorized retailers was emphasized.

Battery Degradation Over Time

Problem: After extended use (typically 1–2+ years of daily use), battery capacity diminished.

Solutions:

  • The battery was technically replaceable with some technical skill, though Arizer did not design it for easy user replacement. Community guides existed for the replacement procedure.
  • Using the Solo with the power adapter plugged in reduced battery wear for home use.
  • Some users noted that even after a year or more, 60 minutes of battery life was still common.

Auto-Shutoff Timer

Problem: The 12-minute auto-shutoff could interrupt longer sessions.

Solution: Users simply restarted the device. While mildly inconvenient, most community members found that a single 12-minute session was sufficient to extract a full stem load.

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems

  • Stock stems: The Solo shipped with one straight and one curved (bent) borosilicate glass stem. Both featured a small dish at the end for packing herb.
  • PlanetVape High Efficiency Stems (PVHES): Widely considered the single most important aftermarket accessory. These stems featured enlarged air channels that dramatically improved airflow and draw resistance. Available in straight, curved, and GonG (glass-on-glass) configurations. The community broadly agreed that PVHES stems should be mentioned alongside any recommendation of the Solo.
  • Ed's TnT wooden stems: Beautiful handcrafted wooden stems that were prized by community members for both aesthetics and function.
  • PlanetVape Stealth Adapter: A stem designed for discreet use, allowing the Solo to be used with drink cups and similar concealment methods.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA) / GonG Stems

  • PVHES GonG (Glass-on-Glass) stems: Available in both 14mm and 18mm sizes, these allowed the Solo to be connected directly to standard water pipes and bubblers. The 14mm size was compatible with the stock straight stem as well.
  • Log vaporizer stems: One community member discovered that glass stems designed for log vaporizers (such as the Underdog or E-Nano) fit the Solo's oven and could serve as GonG adapters. The narrower bowl produced reportedly better flavor, though with increased draw restriction.
  • The Solo paired with a water piece was widely praised as a "vaporbong" setup. Community members noted it produced impressive clouds for a portable, and the water filtration smoothed out any harshness, especially at higher temperature settings.

Water Pipe Recommendations

  • Small bubblers with minimal diffusion were generally preferred over large, heavily percolated pieces. Community members noted that excessive diffusion could strip flavor and reduce vapor density.
  • The D-020 D (a popular Chinese-made bubbler) was mentioned as a good pairing.
  • Ash catchers were considered unnecessary for vapor use, as there was no ash to catch and they added complexity and breakage risk.

Power Adapter

  • An optional power adapter allowed the Solo to be used while plugged in, effectively converting it into a corded unit for home use. Community members noted that using the power adapter with the Solo made it function similarly to a log vaporizer. The adapter did not increase performance but allowed unlimited session length without battery drain.

Comparisons

Arizer Solo vs. Arizer Air

The Air was essentially a more portable evolution of the Solo design with user-replaceable 18650 batteries. The community generally agreed that both produced comparable vapor quality, but opinions were divided on which was preferable:

  • The Solo was often cited as having slightly better performance and battery life per session.
  • The Air won on portability and the ability to carry spare batteries.
  • Multiple community members noted that some Air owners said they "still preferred the Solo."
  • For home and around-the-house use, the Solo was the typical recommendation; for true on-the-go portability, the Air was preferred.

Arizer Solo vs. Arizer Extreme Q (Desktop)

  • The Extreme Q used more herb per session and offered bag and whip delivery methods.
  • The Solo was more efficient with small loads and offered portability.
  • Some users noted the Solo with a power adapter essentially replicated the home-use advantages of the EQ for solo consumption, while using less material.
  • The EQ's advantage was in group sessions and bag delivery, which the Solo could not replicate.

Arizer Solo vs. Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

  • The MFLB was smaller, stealthier, and less expensive, but required significantly more technique to use effectively.
  • The Solo was simpler to use, produced thicker vapor, and was more forgiving of poor technique.
  • The MFLB was better for true pocket portability; the Solo was better for at-home and around-the-house use.
  • Community members frequently recommended the Solo over the MFLB for first-time vaporizer buyers, noting the MFLB could be "limiting" for users who later wanted to experiment.

Arizer Solo vs. Pinnacle Pro

The community strongly favored the Solo over the Pinnacle Pro. The Solo was described as "tested, proven, rock solid" with an extensive accessory ecosystem, while the Pinnacle Pro had reliability and design concerns that led some community members to actively discourage its purchase.

Arizer Solo vs. PuffIt

The Solo was considered superior in every performance metric. As one member succinctly stated: "The Solo exceeds the performance of the PuffIt in every way except stealth."

Arizer Solo vs. DaVinci Ascent

  • The Ascent offered precise temperature control and a sleeker design but was significantly more expensive.
  • The Solo was considered the better value and more proven product.
  • The Ascent was noted as being relatively new at the time of most comparisons and still working through production issues.

Arizer Solo vs. Log Vaporizers (E-Nano, Underdog)

  • Log vaporizers were considered more efficient and capable of producing more potent effects, particularly with small loads.
  • The Solo's advantage was portability — it could be unplugged and used anywhere.
  • For users who primarily vaped at home, a log vaporizer was sometimes recommended as a superior performer, with the Solo serving as a complement for portable use.

Arizer Solo vs. DynaVap VapCap

In later comparisons, the VapCap was sometimes suggested as a complement or alternative to the Solo for users wanting a more smoking-like, on-demand experience. The Solo's session-style approach was better suited to leisurely use, while the VapCap offered quick extraction in just a few heating cycles. Many users ultimately owned both.
