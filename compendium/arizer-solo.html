# Arizer Solo

Overview

The Arizer Solo is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. First announced and released in 2011, the Solo quickly established itself as one of the most recommended portable vaporizers in the community and maintained that reputation for many years. The device was often described as being roughly the size of a Red Bull can.

The Solo uses a hybrid conduction/convection heating method, with a stainless steel oven that heats a glass stem (referred to as the "aroma tube" or "glass aroma top") inserted into the top of the unit. The all-glass air path was a distinguishing feature at launch and remained a core selling point throughout the device's lifespan. The unit is powered by an internal rechargeable lithium-ion battery and can also be used while charging via its AC adapter, effectively enabling pass-through desktop-style use. The Solo was also capable of being charged via a car adapter, expanding its utility for camping and travel.

The Solo was widely regarded by the community as a "best bang for your buck" vaporizer and one of the most commonly recommended first vaporizers for new users transitioning away from combustion. Despite being positioned by some as a "beginner's vaporizer," many experienced users with extensive collections continued to use it as their daily driver years after purchase.

Note on temperature settings: The Solo's interface consists of numbered heat settings (1 through 7) indicated by LEDs rather than a digital display with precise temperature readout. Early community discussion included some confusion about whether the numbered indicators represented temperature levels or battery charge. The community confirmed these numbers represent heat settings. Settings 6 and 7 were understood to be engineering/diagnostic temperatures above normal vaping range.

Community Reception

The Arizer Solo received overwhelmingly positive reception within the vaporizer community and maintained its reputation for years after release. It was one of the most frequently recommended portable vaporizers in purchase advice threads from 2011 through at least 2018.

Strengths

  • Value proposition: Consistently praised as offering the best performance-to-price ratio in the portable vaporizer market. At typical street prices of $130–$160 USD, the community widely agreed it was difficult to beat.
  • Flavor quality: The all-glass air path was credited with producing excellent, clean-tasting vapor. One community member described the Solo's delivery system as something that "heightens and highlights" the flavor of the cannabis.
  • Build quality and reliability: The device was repeatedly described as "built like a tank." Multiple community members reported using their Solos for over two years without issue. Reliability was a key differentiator, especially compared to competitors that experienced higher failure rates.
  • Ease of use: Frequently described as "brainless" and "idiot proof" to operate, making it ideal for beginners while still satisfying experienced users.
  • Efficiency: The Solo was regarded as remarkably efficient with material. One community member noted it "sips your product like it was paying for it." A frequently cited peer-reviewed study (PMID: 26784441) showed the Solo delivering 82.7% THC and 70.0% CBD in vapor — figures that exceeded the Volcano Medic (58.4% THC / 51.4% CBD), the Plenty (66.8% THC / 56.1% CBD), and the DaVinci (54.6% THC / 56.7% CBD).
  • Versatility: Functioned well as both a portable and a home unit, especially with pass-through charging. Compatible with water tools, whips, and a wide range of aftermarket accessories.
  • Battery life: 8–12 sessions per charge was considered excellent for the era, and the ability to charge via car adapter extended its usefulness for outdoor and travel use.
  • Desktop-competitive performance: Multiple community members asserted the Solo could "compete with a desktop" vaporizer, a notable claim for a portable device at its price point.

Weaknesses

  • Size and portability: While technically portable, the Solo was not pocket-friendly. Multiple users noted it was "a bit bulky" and would not fit comfortably in pants pockets, making it better suited for purse or bag carry, or home use. Users seeking true pocket portability were often directed to the Arizer Air instead.
  • Session-style usage: The Solo is a session vaporizer with a 12-minute auto-shutoff. It was not suited for quick one-hit use. Community members noted "you don't want to fire it up for just one hit."
  • Non-replaceable battery: Unlike the Arizer Air, the Solo's battery is not user-swappable, meaning eventual battery degradation requires unit service or replacement.
  • Grind requirement: The Solo performed best with ground herb, unlike some competing devices (such as log vapes) that could handle whole nugs.
  • No precise temperature display: The numbered heat settings, while functional, lacked the precision of devices with digital temperature readouts.

Long-term Reputation

As one community member eloquently summarized: "Sometimes a product is so good that it stands the test of time. The Solo is one of those products." Another noted that many users who upgraded to the Arizer Air still preferred the Solo, observing that community members frequently said "The Air is a great vape... but I still prefer the Solo." Even as newer devices came to market, the Solo retained a dedicated following among both new and experienced vaporists, with many seasoned collectors continuing to reach for it as their most-used device.

Tips & Techniques

Loading

  • Grind: A medium to fine grind was generally recommended for best results.
  • Packing technique: Push herb into the glass stem firmly but not too tightly. After packing, blow lightly over the open end of the stem to dislodge any small particles that might fall through onto the heater element, which could affect taste.
  • Domed screens: The community recommended using domed (dome-shaped) screens pushed into the stock stems to improve airflow and prevent herb from falling through into the stainless steel oven. This was frequently cited as a significant improvement to the stock experience.
  • Stem-down loading: Many users loaded the herb directly into the stem while it was upright, using the stem itself as a scoop for ground material, then tamping gently.

Drawing Technique

  • Slow, steady draws: The Solo responds best to slow, consistent sipping rather than hard pulls. The session-style heating rewards patient inhalation.
  • Step-up technique: Several community members recommended starting at a lower temperature setting and gradually increasing over the course of a session to maximize both flavor and extraction. One user described vaping "once on low, then bumping it up and getting all I can at a higher temp," noting that going straight to high temperatures produced irritating vapor.

Session Management

  • Mid-session pause: The community noted that you could remove the stem mid-session, store it, and return to it later to finish the bowl. This extended the utility of each load despite the 12-minute auto-shutoff.
  • Multiple sessions per stem: Depending on the starting temperature, a single stem pack could yield material for more than one 12-minute session by progressively increasing the heat setting.

Concentrate Use

  • Sandwich method: For using concentrates (wax, oil, hash) in the Solo, the community recommended sandwiching the concentrate between two layers of dry herb in the stem.
  • Organic cotton method: Some community members reported success vaping concentrates by placing them on a small piece of organic cotton in the stem, though this was considered less common than the sandwich technique.

Stealth Use

  • An inventive community technique involved inserting the Solo's stem through the lid of a large soda cup, allowing the user to draw from a straw-like setup in public. This technique gained widespread community recognition and was later formalized by aftermarket products like the PlanetVape stealth adapter, which fit the Solo's stem into a beverage cup lid.

Temperature Guide

The Solo uses 7 numbered heat settings rather than precise degree readouts. The community developed approximate temperature correlations:

SettingApprox. TemperatureRecommended Use
1~50°C / ~122°FBelow vaping threshold; not useful for herb
2~185°C / ~365°FLow-temperature flavor sessions; light, terpy vapor
3~190°C / ~374°FGood balance of flavor and mild effects
4~195°C / ~383°FCommunity sweet spot — good vapor production with excellent flavor
5~200°C / ~392°FThicker clouds, more body effects, still good flavor
6~205°C / ~401°FHeavy extraction; diminishing flavor, increased harshness
7~210°C / ~410°FMaximum extraction; community generally avoided this for daily use

Community notes on temperature:

  • Settings 3–5 were the most commonly used range for daily sessions.
  • The step-up method (starting at 3 or 4 and finishing at 5 or 6) was widely recommended for getting the most from each stem pack.
  • On setting 7, herb could turn very dark brown to black. The community debated whether blackened herb at the highest settings constituted combustion or merely charring. The general consensus was that black residue indicated charring (an early stage approaching combustion) but not full combustion, which would produce white ash. Users were nonetheless advised to avoid the highest settings for routine use.
  • Users with asthma or respiratory sensitivity were advised to stay at lower settings and draw slowly, as higher temperatures produced more irritating vapor.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Stems

  • Frequency: Clean stems when visible residue builds up or airflow becomes restricted, typically every few sessions for heavy users.
  • Method: Soak glass stems in isopropyl alcohol (90%+ recommended) for 15–30 minutes, then rinse thoroughly with warm water. A pipe cleaner can be used to remove stubborn residue from inside the stem.
  • Stem milk: A popular community technique involved simmering used stems in milk (the fat content extracts accumulated resin) to both clean the stems and create an infused beverage. This was considered a pleasant bonus of the glass stem design.

Stainless Steel Oven

  • Keep the oven free of debris by regularly brushing out any herb particles that fall through the stem.
  • A cotton swab dipped in isopropyl alcohol can be used to wipe the oven surface when the unit is cool and off.
  • Blowing lightly into a freshly packed stem before inserting it was recommended to prevent loose particles from falling into the oven.

General

  • The exterior of the unit could be wiped down with a damp cloth.
  • Ensure all alcohol has evaporated completely before using the device after cleaning.

Common Issues & Fixes

Herb Falling into the Oven

Problem: Small herb particles falling from the stem into the stainless steel oven, causing burnt taste and requiring frequent cleaning.

Solution: Use a domed screen pushed into the stem to act as a barrier. Alternatively, pack the herb more firmly and blow lightly across the stem opening to remove loose particles before inserting into the unit.

Charring/Blackening of Herb

Problem: Herb turning black at the point where it contacts the heater, particularly at higher temperature settings or with the Arizer Air (which some users noted ran slightly hotter than the Solo in the same stem position).

Solution: This was generally considered charring rather than true combustion (which produces white ash). To avoid it: pack herb slightly recessed from the very bottom of the stem, use lower temperature settings, and avoid overpacking. The community noted that some discoloration at the contact point was normal and did not necessarily indicate combustion.

Battery Degradation

Problem: Over time, the internal battery's capacity diminishes, resulting in shorter session counts per charge.

Solution: The Solo's battery is not user-swappable by design. Some technically inclined users opened the unit to replace the battery, but this voided the warranty. For most users, battery degradation was addressed by using the device with the AC adapter (pass-through mode) or eventually replacing the unit.

Draw Resistance

Problem: Some users found the stock stems to have more draw resistance than desired.

Solution: Aftermarket stems, particularly those from PlanetVape (PVHE stems) and Ed's TnT, offered modified airflow characteristics. The PVHE stems were noted for making it easier to get large clouds with less effort.

Accessories & Modifications

Aftermarket Stems

  • PlanetVape PVHE (High Efficiency) Stem: One of the most popular aftermarket accessories. Community members reported that the PVHE stem made it noticeably easier to produce thick vapor compared to stock stems. The PVHE Turbo variant featured a larger bowl size.
  • Ed's TnT Stems: Wooden-bodied stems that were praised for improving the draw resistance characteristics of the Solo. Community members noted these stems "improved the restriction of air" on the Solo.
  • Domed screens in stock stems: Pushing a small dome-shaped screen into the stock glass stem was a simple, widely recommended modification that improved airflow and prevented herb from falling through.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

The Solo was widely praised for its compatibility with water tools. The glass stem could be inserted into 14mm and 18mm glass joints with appropriate adapters. Dedicated WPA stems (both from Arizer and aftermarket manufacturers like PlanetVape) were available and allowed the Solo to be used directly with bubblers, bongs, and other water filtration devices. The community frequently cited the Solo's ability to "milk glass of all sizes" as a major advantage.

Whip Adapter

PlanetVape produced a whip adapter for the Solo that was well-received. One community member specifically noted that "the Solo is great with a whip," expanding its versatility for home use.

Stealth Adapter

PlanetVape offered a stealth adapter designed to fit the Solo's stem into a beverage cup lid, formalizing the community's DIY soda cup technique for discreet public use.

Car Charger

Both the Solo and the Mighty were noted as being chargeable via car adapter, making the Solo a practical choice for road trips and camping.

Comparisons

Arizer Solo vs. Arizer Air

The Air was Arizer's more portable successor, featuring a slimmer profile and user-swappable 18650 batteries. The community acknowledged the Air's improved portability but frequently noted that many users still preferred the Solo. The Solo was perceived as having better battery life per session (owing to its dual internal batteries versus the Air's single 18650), and many community members described the overall experience as slightly superior. One common community observation was: "The Air is a great vape... but I still prefer the Solo." For users who prioritized true pocket portability or the ability to carry spare batteries, the Air was recommended; for home or bag-portable use, the Solo was generally favored.

Arizer Solo vs. Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

Both were common entry-level recommendations, but for different user profiles. The MFLB was smaller, more stealthy, and functioned as an on-demand device, but had a steeper learning curve and was considered more limiting for users who might want to share with friends or take larger hits. The Solo was recommended for users who wanted a more straightforward, session-style experience with better vapor production and less technique-dependence. As one community member summarized: the MFLB was "a good fit" for minimal solo use, but "you may find it limiting" compared to the Solo's versatility.

Arizer Solo vs. Log Vapes (E-Nano, Underdog)

Log vapes were acknowledged as superior in extraction efficiency and vapor density, particularly through water tools. However, they required a power outlet. The Solo was recommended as the better choice when any degree of portability or cordless operation was needed. One community member described the Solo as "a great general purpose vape" that was "portable but can run off AC," while the E-Nano was "definitely superior to the Solo in terms of extraction and vapor production." For home use near an outlet, log vapes were often preferred; for versatility, the Solo won out.

Arizer Solo vs. Storz & Bickel Mighty

The Mighty was generally regarded as the superior performer but at significantly higher cost ($300–$400 vs. $130–$160 for the Solo). One community member who owned both ranked the Mighty first overall but described the Solo as "very very good." The Solo's dramatically lower price point made it the recommended choice for budget-conscious users, while the Mighty was suggested for those willing to invest more.

Arizer Solo vs. Volcano

The peer-reviewed study frequently cited by the community showed the Solo outperforming the Volcano Medic in THC delivery percentage (82.7% vs. 58.4%) and CBD delivery (70.0% vs. 51.4%), a data point that surprised many users given the Volcano's higher price and desktop form factor. The community speculated that condensation on the Volcano's bag surfaces accounted for some of this difference. While the Volcano remained respected for group use and its bag delivery system, the Solo's efficiency numbers gave it an impressive scientific credential that bolstered its reputation.

Arizer Solo vs. Flowermate V5.0S

Users who experienced reliability problems with the Flowermate were frequently directed to the Solo as a more dependable alternative. While the Flowermate occupied a lower price bracket, the Solo's proven reliability and superior vapor quality made it the standard community upgrade recommendation for Flowermate owners seeking a replacement.

Arizer Solo vs. Pax

Both were popular portable options, but they served different niches. The Pax was preferred for stealth and pocketability, while the Solo was preferred for vapor quality, efficiency, and value. The Solo's glass air path and lower price gave it an edge in community recommendations for users who did not require maximum discretion.
