# Arizer Solo

Overview

The Arizer Solo is a portable dry herb vaporizer manufactured by Arizer, a Canadian company based in Waterloo, Ontario. First announced and released in mid-2011, the Solo quickly established itself as one of the most recommended portable vaporizers in the community and maintained that reputation for many years, long after competitors entered the market. The device was designed by Arizer's Steve Gale and was notable for being one of the earliest battery-powered portables to feature an all-glass vapor path via its removable borosilicate glass stems.

The Solo employs a hybrid conduction/convection heating system, with a stainless steel oven (heater) that heats herb loaded into the bottom of a glass stem inserted into the unit. Air is drawn past the heated herb, producing vapor that travels through the glass stem to the user. The device is powered by an internal lithium-ion battery and can also be used while charging via its AC power adapter. Its form factor is roughly the size of a Red Bull can, making it portable though not truly pocket-sized.

The Solo's combination of reliability, vapor quality, affordability, efficiency, and simplicity of use earned it a reputation within the vaporizer community as one of the most enduring and universally recommended devices, particularly for new users — not because it was considered merely a "beginner's vape," but because it was genuinely regarded as an excellent all-around performer.

Community Reception

The Arizer Solo was one of the most universally praised portable vaporizers in the community's history. It was routinely described as the default recommendation for anyone seeking their first vaporizer, but experienced users also continued to rely on it as a daily driver even after acquiring multiple other devices.

Strengths

  • Vapor quality and flavor: The all-glass vapor path was widely regarded as producing exceptionally clean, flavorful vapor. Community members consistently praised the taste, with one describing the Solo's delivery system as something that "heightens and highlights" the flavor of the cannabis. The glass stem design was cited as a major differentiator from competitors that used plastic or metal vapor paths.
  • Reliability and durability: The Solo was described as "built like a tank" by multiple community members. Stories of devices lasting for years of daily use were common. This stood in marked contrast to many cheaper portables that were reported to fail within months.
  • Efficiency: The Solo was regarded as highly efficient with herb material, with community members noting that it "sips your product." A peer-reviewed scientific study (published in the Journal of Cannabis Therapeutics and indexed at PubMed ID 26784441) was frequently cited within the community, showing the Solo achieved 82.7% THC delivery and 70.0% CBD delivery in vapor — significantly outperforming the Volcano Medic (58.4% THC / 51.4% CBD), the Plenty (66.8% THC / 56.1% CBD), and the DaVinci (54.6% THC / 56.7% CBD). This data point became a touchstone of community discussions about the Solo's value proposition.
  • Value for money: At a typical price point of $130–$160 USD, the Solo was consistently described as offering exceptional bang for the buck. Community members noted it could be found on sale from vendors like PuffItUp for even less, sometimes with discount codes.
  • Simplicity: The Solo was described as "brainless" and "idiot proof" to use — no technique mastery required, no app needed, no complex temperature control. Just load the stem, select a heat level, and draw.
  • Versatility: The Solo worked well as both a portable and a home vape. It connected easily to water tools, worked with a whip adapter, and could be used while plugged into AC power. One community member noted the Solo could be charged via a car adapter, making it suitable for camping.
  • Longevity as a product: Even years after release, community members continued to endorse it. As one member put it: "Sometimes a product is so good that it stands the test of time. The Solo is one of those products." Multiple members noted a recurring phenomenon where users who upgraded to the newer Arizer Air would comment that they still preferred the Solo.

Weaknesses

  • Size: While portable, the Solo was not truly pocket-sized. Its dimensions made it more of a "semi-portable" or purse/bag device. Users who needed true pocket stealth were often directed toward smaller alternatives like the Arizer Air or Pax.
  • Session-style usage: The Solo is a session vaporizer with a 12-minute auto-shutoff. It was not well-suited to quick, on-demand single hits. Community members noted: "You don't want to fire it up for just one hit."
  • Glass stems: While the glass path was praised for flavor, it introduced fragility. Broken stems were a common occurrence, and carrying the device required care. The protruding stem also reduced stealth.
  • Non-user-replaceable battery: Unlike the Arizer Air, the Solo's battery could not be swapped by the user without disassembly, meaning once the battery degraded, the unit either required repair or replacement.
  • Charging while using: While the device could be used during charging, this was more of a workaround than a true pass-through feature.

Community Reputation Summary

The community widely regarded the Solo as a "desert island" portable vaporizer — the one device that would satisfactorily serve nearly any user's needs. It was the most commonly recommended portable vaporizer for beginners and was frequently recommended even to experienced users. One community member captured the sentiment: "I'd buy another without concern for what else is out there." Another noted that even with a collection of multiple vaporizers, the Solo remained the most-reached-for device.

On the broader internet, the Solo also generated strong loyalty. Reddit's r/vaporents community featured highly upvoted posts of users celebrating their switch from combustion to vaping thanks to the Solo, with one popular post receiving over 2,100 upvotes from a user declaring they would "never need to smoke ANYTHING again."

Tips & Techniques

Loading the Stem

  • Grind quality: A medium grind was generally preferred. Overly fine grinds could allow small particles to fall through the stem and onto the heater element, producing an unpleasant taste.
  • Packing technique: Community members recommended pushing herb into the glass stem firmly enough that it would not fall out when the stem was inverted, but not so tightly as to restrict airflow. One widely shared technique was to pack the herb in, then gently blow across the top of the stem to dislodge any loose fine particles before inserting it into the unit.
  • Domed screen method: Several community members recommended using a domed (convex) stainless steel screen inside the stock stems. This helped contain herb in the bowl area and prevented material from falling onto the heater. This was described as a significant improvement to the stock experience.
  • Inverted loading ("stem-up" method): Some users would hold the Solo upside-down when inserting the loaded stem, so gravity would help keep the herb in place against the heater end of the stem.

Drawing Technique

  • Slow, steady draws: The Solo rewarded long, slow sipping draws rather than hard, fast pulls. This allowed the heater to maintain temperature and produce consistent vapor density.
  • Temperature stepping: A common technique was to start a session at a lower heat level (2–4) for flavor, then increase the temperature across the session to fully extract the remaining material. One community member described vaping "once on low, then I bump it up and get all I can at a higher temp."

Concentrate Use

The Solo could handle concentrates using the "sandwich method" — placing a small amount of concentrate between two layers of dry herb. Some community members also reported success vaporizing concentrates in a bed of organic cotton placed inside the glass stem.

Stealth Use

A creative "stealth adapter" was available from PlanetVape that allowed the Solo stem to be concealed inside a beverage cup with a straw. Community members on Reddit also independently devised similar solutions using fast-food cups, as documented in a widely shared tip post that garnered nearly 1,900 upvotes.

Temperature Guide

The Solo uses a 7-level numbered system rather than precise temperature readouts. The community mapped approximate temperatures as follows:

LevelApprox. Temp (°C)Approx. Temp (°F)Community Notes
1~50°C~122°FAromatherapy only; no meaningful vapor production from herb
2~185°C~365°FLight flavor hits; minimal visible vapor
3~190°C~374°FGood flavor; light to moderate vapor
4~195°C~383°FSweet spot for many users; balanced flavor and vapor production
5~200°C~392°FSolid vapor production; slightly diminished flavor
6~205°C~401°FDense vapor; good for extraction
7~210°C~410°FMaximum extraction; may produce black/very dark ABV

Community-agreed sweet spots:

  • Flavor-focused sessions: Start at Level 3, finish at Level 5
  • Balanced sessions: Start at Level 4, step up to 5 or 6
  • Maximum extraction: Level 6 or 7 (note: Level 7 could produce very dark, nearly black ABV, which some community members debated as approaching combustion territory — see Common Issues section)

Temperature stepping was the most commonly recommended approach: begin low for terpene-rich flavor hits, then gradually increase to fully extract cannabinoids.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Glass Stems

  • Frequency: Stems should be cleaned regularly, typically after every few sessions or when visible resin buildup reduces airflow or flavor quality.
  • Method: Soak stems in isopropyl alcohol (ISO, 90%+ recommended). Resin dissolves readily. Rinse thoroughly with warm water afterward.
  • Stem milk: A popular community technique involved simmering used stems in whole milk or cream. The fat content would dissolve the accumulated resin, producing an edible infused beverage. This was a widely loved "bonus" of Solo ownership.

Heater/Oven

  • The stainless steel oven should be kept free of debris. Small particles of herb falling from the stem onto the heater element was a common occurrence and could produce an unpleasant burnt taste.
  • Cleaning method: Use a cotton swab dampened with isopropyl alcohol to gently wipe the inside of the oven. Avoid getting excess liquid into the device.
  • Prevention: Using the blow-off technique when loading (described in Tips & Techniques) and using domed screens significantly reduced debris accumulation.

Unit Body

  • Wipe the exterior with a damp cloth as needed. The Solo's construction was robust and the housing generally resisted wear well.

Common Issues & Fixes

Herb Falling Into the Oven

Problem: Loose or finely ground herb dropping from the glass stem into the stainless steel heater chamber, causing foul taste and requiring more frequent cleaning.

Fix: Pack stems more firmly; use the blow-off technique before inserting; install a domed screen in the stem; consider aftermarket stems with better retention (e.g., PVHE stems).

Charring / Black ABV

Problem: Some users reported very dark or black material at the bottom of the stem (nearest the heater), particularly at higher temperature settings. This was a topic of debate within the community.

Discussion: Community opinion was divided on whether black (but not ash-like) ABV constituted combustion. Some members argued that blackened herb indicated combustion had occurred, while others maintained that charring without the production of white ash represented thermal degradation short of true combustion. One community member noted: "On 7 my Solo would turn it black but definitely not combustion." Another countered: "Black = charred = combustion occurred."

Practical advice: If concerned about combustion, avoid Level 7 or use it only briefly at the end of a session. The difference between Solo and Air performance was noted — some users experienced more charring with the Air than the Solo under similar conditions, suggesting slight heater calibration differences between models.

Glass Stem Breakage

Problem: Glass stems could crack or break if dropped or if inserted/removed carelessly.

Fix: Handle with care; purchase spare stems (they were inexpensive); consider sturdier aftermarket options. Some users kept multiple stems pre-loaded for convenience.

Battery Degradation

Problem: Over time (typically 1–2+ years of heavy use), the internal battery's capacity could diminish.

Fix: The Solo could continue to function while plugged into the AC adapter. More technically inclined users could replace the internal battery cells, though this required disassembly and was not officially supported.

Accessories & Modifications

Glass Stems

  • Stock stems: The Solo shipped with a straight stem and a curved (bent) stem. Both were functional but considered basic.
  • PlanetVape High Efficiency (PVHE) stems: Among the most popular aftermarket accessories. These featured a modified bowl design that improved airflow and made it easier to produce dense vapor. Community members consistently reported that the PVHE stems reduced the learning curve for getting large hits. The PVHE "Turbo" variant had a larger bowl for those wanting bigger loads.
  • Ed's TnT stems: Wooden stems crafted by Ed's TnT (Ed's Tobacco and Timber) were praised for improving the draw restriction (which some users found too loose on stock stems) and adding aesthetic appeal.

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

The Solo was exceptionally well-regarded for use with water tools. Several WPA options existed:

  • PlanetVape WPA: A glass adapter that replaced the standard stem and allowed direct connection to 14mm or 18mm water pipes. The Solo was described as "capable of milking glass of all sizes" when used with a WPA.
  • GonG (Glass-on-Glass) adapters: Various third-party glass-on-glass adapters were available. Using the Solo through water was considered one of the best ways to experience the device, particularly for users sensitive to vapor warmth.

Whip Adapter

PlanetVape produced a whip adapter for the Solo that allowed hands-free, whip-style use. One community member specifically praised this accessory, noting "The Solo is great with a whip."

Stealth Adapter

PlanetVape manufactured a "stealth adapter" that allowed the Solo's glass stem to interface with a beverage cup and straw, enabling discreet public use. This concept was popular enough that community members independently replicated it with DIY solutions.

Screens

Stainless steel dome screens were commonly recommended as inexpensive upgrades that significantly improved the stock stem experience by containing herb and improving airflow consistency.

Comparisons

Arizer Solo vs. Arizer Air

The Air was Arizer's slimmer, more portable successor with a user-replaceable 18650 battery. Community consensus was nuanced:

  • The Air was more portable and the swappable battery was a significant advantage for travel.
  • The Solo had longer battery life per charge (dual internal cells vs. the Air's single 18650).
  • Many community members expressed a preference for the Solo's performance, with a frequently repeated observation being: "The Air is a great vape... but I still prefer the Solo."
  • Some users reported the Air was more prone to charring herb at the bottom of the stem compared to the Solo.
  • For primarily home use, the Solo was generally preferred; for true portability, the Air was recommended.

Arizer Solo vs. Magic Flight Launch Box (MFLB)

  • The MFLB was smaller, more discreet, and functioned as an on-demand vaporizer.
  • The Solo produced denser vapor, was easier to use without technique mastery, and was considered more suitable for sharing.
  • Community members often noted the MFLB could feel "limiting" over time, while the Solo offered more room to grow.
  • For users who primarily wanted single quick hits, the MFLB had advantages; for session-style vaping with consistent performance, the Solo was preferred.

Arizer Solo vs. Pax

  • The Pax was significantly more portable and stealthy.
  • The Solo was regarded as producing better flavor due to its glass vapor path.
  • The Solo was considerably less expensive.
  • The Pax required more cleaning and maintenance.
  • For stealth and pocket portability, the Pax won; for flavor, value, and simplicity, the Solo was the community favorite.

Arizer Solo vs. Desktop Vaporizers (Volcano, E-Nano, Underdog)

  • Desktop vaporizers generally offered superior extraction power and vapor density.
  • The E-Nano was described as "definitely superior to the Solo in terms of extraction and vapor production."
  • However, the Solo was frequently described as a portable that could "compete with a desktop," particularly when used through water.
  • The Solo's portability and battery operation gave it a significant versatility advantage.
  • For users who primarily vaped at home but wanted occasional portability, the Solo was often considered a compelling single-device solution.

Arizer Solo vs. Storz & Bickel Mighty

  • The Mighty was more expensive (roughly 2–3x the cost).
  • Community members who owned both generally rated the Mighty as the superior performer.
  • However, the Solo's lower price point, reliability, and glass vapor path kept it competitive, and many users felt the performance gap did not justify the price difference.
  • One community member who owned both the Mighty, Solo, and Air summarized: "For my purposes, The Mighty is 1st, but the Solo and the Air are very very good."

Arizer Solo vs. Flowermate V5.0S

  • The Flowermate was less expensive but was reported to have significant reliability issues.
  • Community members who experienced Flowermate failures were routinely directed toward the Solo as a more dependable upgrade.
  • The Solo's all-glass vapor path was considered a meaningful quality advantage.
