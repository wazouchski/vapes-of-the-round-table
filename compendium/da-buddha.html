# Da Buddha (DBV)

Overview

The Da Buddha (commonly abbreviated DBV) is a desktop whip-style dry herb vaporizer manufactured by 7th Floor, a Colorado-based company also known for producing the Silver Surfer Vaporizer (SSV), the Life Saber Vaporizer (LSV), and the Ditanium. The Da Buddha was positioned as 7th Floor's more affordable entry-level unit, sharing the same core ceramic heating element as its higher-end sibling, the SSV, but in a simpler, more streamlined package.

The DBV uses a convection-dominant heating method, where air is drawn over a ceramic heating element and through ground herb held in a glass wand (sometimes called a "whip wand"). It is an AC-powered desktop unit — not portable — requiring a standard wall outlet. The unit features an analog rotary dial for temperature control and a simple, no-frills design with few moving or breakable parts.

The Da Buddha earned a reputation within the vaporizer community as a reliable, well-built workhorse — an excellent introduction to desktop vaporization at a price point significantly below premium units. Its straightforward operation, robust build quality, and compatibility with water pipes made it a mainstay recommendation for years.

Community Reception

The Da Buddha was widely regarded within the community as one of the best values in desktop vaporization. Its combination of proven 7th Floor heating technology, simple design, and accessible price point made it a perennial recommendation for newcomers and budget-conscious buyers alike.

Strengths

  • Excellent value for money. The DBV delivered performance that the community considered very close to the more expensive SSV, using the same ceramic heating element. As one community member noted, it offered SSV-level performance for significantly less money.
  • Simplicity and reliability. The minimal design with few parts meant there was little to break or malfunction. The community noted that 7th Floor reportedly had a heating element running continuously in their factory for years without failure.
  • Hands-free operation by default. The DBV's 90-degree wand angle allowed the wand to sit in the heater cover without being held, which was appreciated by users who wanted to vape while gaming or doing other activities.
  • Versatile session vape. Users praised the DBV as an excellent session vaporizer, particularly for groups of 1–3 people. It was frequently described as a "fun party/session vape."
  • Good with lower-quality material. The moderate-to-large bowl and high heat capability meant the DBV could effectively vaporize mid-grade or even sub-standard herb, a quality not shared by all vaporizers.
  • Easy water pipe compatibility. The standard whip tubing fit directly into 14.4mm glass-on-glass joints without an adapter, and dedicated water pipe adapters (WPAs) were also available. The "vaporbong" setup was a popular configuration.
  • ABV production. Like all vaporizers, the DBV produced already-been-vaped material (ABV) that could be saved and repurposed for edibles or other uses.

Weaknesses

  • Not efficient for solo micro-dosing. The community widely acknowledged that the DBV's bowl size encouraged using more herb than necessary for a single user. Users transitioning from the DBV to log vaporizers (Purple Days, Underdog, E-Nano) consistently reported significant reductions in herb consumption.
  • Whip-style vapor path concerns. Some users noted that the silicone tubing was not as clean a vapor path as all-glass systems. One community member specifically cautioned that "clean vapor path" was not one of the DBV's strongest selling points compared to all-glass alternatives.
  • Analog temperature control. The lack of a digital readout meant temperature settings were approximate and could vary between units. Users had to learn their specific unit's sweet spots through experimentation.
  • Not portable. Some newcomers mistakenly believed the DBV was portable; the community was quick to clarify that it is strictly a desktop unit requiring wall power.
  • Hot vapor on the throat. Some users, particularly those transitioning from smoking, found the vapor harsh or hot, especially at higher temperatures without water filtration.
  • Transition difficulty for smokers. Several community discussions documented the challenges former combustion users faced in adapting to the DBV. The different ritual, the need for technique refinement, and the absence of combustion byproducts led some new users to initially abandon the device before eventually returning to it.

Tips & Techniques

Loading the Wand

  • Grind herb to a medium consistency — not too fine, as very fine material can fall through the screen or get sucked into the heater cover.
  • Load the wand loosely; do not pack tightly. Airflow is critical for convection vaporization.
  • The bowl can accommodate a wide range of load sizes, but for solo use, a partial load is acceptable.

Drawing Technique

  • Start low and slow. Set the dial to approximately the 12 o'clock position and draw at a normal pace. If vapor production is thin, slow your draw speed before increasing temperature.
  • Breath control is key. One experienced community member explained that with the DBV, you can keep the temperature dial constant and adjust effective temperature through draw speed: slower draws increase the temperature of air reaching the herb; faster draws cool it. This technique was considered an advanced but valuable skill.
  • Moist herb may need conditioning. Fresh or moist herb sometimes requires a couple of initial draws to dry out before visible vapor production begins. This is normal and does not indicate a problem.
  • Avoid blowing through the whip. A common warning in the community was to never exhale or talk while the mouthpiece is near your face. Blowing air back through the whip can push ground herb into the heater cover, which is difficult to clean and can produce unpleasant odors. Experienced users advised taking a deep breath or two before bringing the whip to your mouth, and keeping the mouthpiece well away from your face between draws.

Session Management

  • Stir periodically. The horizontal wand angle means the herb closest to the heat source vaporizes faster. Stirring between draws ensures even extraction. Users seeking minimal stirring noted that the DBV required more intervention than some competing designs.
  • Progressive temperature approach. Many experienced users started at a lower dial setting, enjoyed the flavor-forward early draws, then gradually increased the temperature to extract remaining cannabinoids. This "temp stepping" approach was a widely discussed technique across the community.
  • Don't rush. The DBV excels as a session vaporizer. Users who tried to extract everything quickly at high temperatures reported diminished flavor and harsher vapor.

Water Pipe (Vaporbong) Setup

  • The standard silicone whip tubing fits directly into a 14.4mm ground glass joint without any adapter, though dedicated adapters were available from 7th Floor and third parties.
  • When using the DBV through a bong, pull the adapter or whip to clear the chamber rather than blowing back through the system, which can dislodge herb into the heater cover.
  • Water filtration significantly cooled the vapor and was highly recommended by the community for users who found direct-whip hits too warm.

Temperature Guide

Because the Da Buddha uses an analog dial without a digital temperature readout, the community developed a clock-face reference system. Note that individual units may vary, so these are general guidelines:

Dial PositionApproximate EffectNotes

Beyond 2:00Very high; combustion riskNot generally recommended

Sweet Spot

The community widely agreed that the 12:00 to 12:30 range was the ideal starting point. Users were advised to begin at noon, evaluate vapor production, and adjust upward in small increments (waiting a minute or so after each adjustment for the temperature to stabilize).

Draw Speed as Temperature Control

An important community insight was that draw speed functions as a secondary temperature control:

  • Slower draws = hotter air reaching the herb = more vapor production
  • Faster draws = cooler air = lighter vapor, more flavor

This allowed experienced users to effectively modulate temperature without touching the dial. One member noted that their DBV stayed at the same temperature throughout a session, with all temperature adjustment achieved through breathing technique.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Whip and Wand

  • The glass wand and mouthpiece can be soaked in isopropyl alcohol (ISO) to dissolve resin buildup. Many users performed this weekly or as needed based on visible residue.
  • The silicone tubing should be replaced periodically as it absorbs odors and develops residue that is difficult to fully clean. Some users replaced tubing on a regular schedule.
  • Screens in the wand should be replaced when airflow becomes restricted or when they are visibly clogged and cannot be adequately cleaned.

Heater Cover and Ceramic Element

  • The ceramic heating rod may develop visible marks over time, including dark lines and white flakes. The community discussed this at length: the dark lines are where the heating wire runs beneath the ceramic surface and are normal. White flakes were observed by some users and were a point of concern.
  • A moderator and knowledgeable community member noted that concerns about ceramic dust and silicosis from vaporizer heating elements were extensively researched within the community. The conclusion reached by most informed members was that the levels of potential ceramic dust exposure from normal vaporizer use were far below the occupational exposure thresholds associated with silicosis risk. However, individual risk tolerance varied, and some users chose alternative designs as a precaution.
  • If herb falls into the heater cover (typically from accidentally blowing through the whip), it should be cleaned out promptly to prevent burning and off-flavors.

General Maintenance

  • The aluminum housing can be wiped down with a damp cloth.
  • Ensure the unit is unplugged and fully cooled before performing any maintenance.

Common Issues & Fixes

Heating Element Not Working (Power Light On)

Symptom: The power indicator light on the back of the unit illuminates, but the heating element never gets hot.

Cause: This was reported by multiple users and typically indicates a failed heating element.

Fix:

  • First, check the power strip — one community member reported that a tripped breaker on a power strip mimicked this symptom perfectly.
  • Try different outlets to rule out power delivery issues.
  • If the problem persists, contact 7th Floor directly. The community reported that 7th Floor would either send a replacement heater for user installation (for technically inclined owners) or have the unit shipped in for repair. If purchased through a third-party retailer, warranty claims may need to go through that retailer.

Delayed or Unresponsive Customer Service (Email)

Symptom: Emails to 7th Floor customer service go unanswered for extended periods.

Fix: The community strongly and consistently advised calling 7th Floor by phone rather than emailing. Email response was described as unreliable by multiple users, while phone support was generally well-regarded. Note that 7th Floor operated on Pacific Time.

Herb Falling into Heater Cover

Symptom: Material drops into the heater cover area, producing burning smells.

Cause: Almost always caused by accidentally blowing (exhaling) through the whip, or by not pulling the whip/adapter when clearing a bong.

Fix: Clean out any material from the heater cover when the unit is cool. Prevention is key — see the tips section above regarding drawing technique and bong clearing.

Difficulty Transitioning from Smoking

Symptom: New users find the vaping experience unsatisfying compared to smoking, with vapor feeling "too light" or the ritual feeling unfamiliar.

Fix: The community consistently advised patience and persistence. Key recommendations included:

  • Take a tolerance break before switching to give your body a chance to adjust.
  • Try using the DBV through a water pipe for a more familiar "bong rip" sensation.
  • Give it at least a week of exclusive vaping; many users reported that after this period, attempting to return to smoking was an unpleasant experience, making the transition permanent.
  • Start at higher temperatures initially for a smoking-like experience, then gradually explore lower temperatures for flavor.

Accessories & Modifications

Water Pipe Adapters (WPA)

  • 7th Floor sold dedicated WPAs for connecting the DBV to standard glass-on-glass water pipes.
  • The standard silicone whip tubing also fits directly into 14.4mm GonG joints, making an adapter technically optional for basic setups.
  • The "vaporbong" configuration was one of the most popular ways to use the DBV, significantly cooling vapor and enabling larger hits.

SSV Heater Cover and Wand Compatibility

  • Community members reported using SSV standard heater covers and wands with the DBV, as the heating element is shared between the two units. This allowed for some cross-compatibility with SSV accessories.
  • The "VB mini wand" (a shorter wand design) was also reported to work with the DBV by at least one community member.

7th Floor Vapor Tamers

  • 7th Floor produced a line of cooling accessories called Vapor Tamers in three sizes: Original, Fatty, and Saturn.
  • When used with the DBV, the Original model was noted to restrict airflow somewhat, while the Saturn and Fatty offered little to no noticeable restriction.
  • Vapor Tamers were designed to be frozen before use, providing cooled vapor without water filtration. The community consensus was that they were effective but did not hold their cold temperature for extended sessions, requiring periodic re-freezing (approximately every 20 minutes of continuous use, per some reports).

Glass Upgrades

  • Aftermarket and 7th Floor glass wands in various sizes and styles were available and popular for customizing the experience.
  • Some users experimented with different screen types and positions within the wand to optimize airflow and herb retention.

Community members who owned both generally preferred the SSV for its more versatile wand angle (especially for concentrates and kief) and build quality, but acknowledged the DBV delivered the same fundamental vaping experience at a substantially lower price. As one member put it: "They do both have that same 7th Floor Heater!" The community generally agreed the SSV was "worth the price premium" for those who could afford it, particularly for concentrate users, but the DBV was never considered a poor alternative.

The community consensus was clear: for solo users primarily concerned with conservation, a log vaporizer would pay for itself in saved herb over time. As one member noted, "if you predominantly vape alone, you'll most likely make that $85 back in the long run due to the micro loads and conserved stash associated with log vapes." However, for users who regularly shared with others, wanted long relaxed sessions, or needed higher temperatures for lower-quality material or sedative effects, the DBV was often the better choice.

Da Buddha vs. Ditanium

One community member who owned both the Da Buddha, the Ditanium, and the Vapor Brothers VB1 ranked the Da Buddha first for pure performance, followed by the Ditanium, then the Vapor Brothers. The Ditanium was noted to be significantly more expensive (approximately double the price in the EU market) and offered additional features including a titanium hand piece designed for both flower and concentrate use. For flower-only users, the Da Buddha was considered the better value.

Da Buddha vs. Arizer Extreme Q

While not extensively compared in the available threads, the Extreme Q was occasionally mentioned as an alternative that offered both whip and bag filling modes. The DBV was generally considered to deliver better whip performance, while the Extreme Q offered more versatility. The Extreme Q's cyclone bowl was noted for potentially requiring less stirring than the DBV's wand.

Da Buddha vs. Portable Vaporizers (Pax, MFLB, Arizer Solo)

The community consistently emphasized that the DBV is not portable and should not be compared directly to portables for use-case purposes. For users who vaped almost exclusively at home, the DBV was generally recommended over similarly-priced portables due to superior vapor production, simpler operation, and no battery concerns. The standard advice was to invest in a good desktop unit for home use and, budget permitting, add an inexpensive portable for occasional on-the-go use.
