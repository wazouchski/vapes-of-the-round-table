# Da Buddha (DBV)

Overview

The Da Buddha (commonly abbreviated DBV) is a desktop whip-style dry herb vaporizer manufactured by 7th Floor, a company based in Colorado Springs, Colorado. The DBV uses convection heating via a ceramic heating element, drawing ambient air over the herb as the user inhales through a silicone whip. It is a plug-in, AC-powered desktop unit with no battery — designed for stationary home use only.

The Da Buddha is widely recognized as the more affordable sibling of 7th Floor's Silver Surfer Vaporizer (SSV), sharing the same ceramic heating element but differing in form factor and heater cover orientation. The DBV has been a mainstay in the desktop vaporizer market since its introduction, offering a straightforward, no-frills vaporization experience at a competitive price point. The unit features an analog rotary dial (rheostat) for temperature control rather than digital readout, a cylindrical body with a "hands-free" horizontal heater cover orientation, and a glass-on-glass wand connection.

Community Reception

The Da Buddha was widely regarded within the vaporizer community as an excellent entry-level desktop vaporizer and a strong value proposition. Members consistently praised its simplicity, durability, and vapor quality relative to its price point.

Strengths highlighted by the community:

  • Vapor production: The DBV was praised for delivering thick, satisfying vapor, particularly when paired with water filtration. One experienced community member noted that it delivers a high vapor-to-air ratio that is "close" to the SSV, with the added benefit of requiring "less fiddling" and producing "more predictable hits."
  • Build quality: Multiple members described the unit as well-built and durable. The simple design with few moving parts was considered an advantage for longevity. One reviewer specifically chose the DBV because "it has a basic design with few parts, none of which are really in any danger of breaking."
  • Versatility: The hands-free wand design was frequently praised, allowing users to set the wand in the heater cover and draw without holding anything. The unit was considered particularly well-suited for session vaping, gaming, or social use.
  • Price: At approximately $150–190 USD (prices varied by era and retailer), the DBV was consistently positioned as one of the best values in desktop vaporization. Canadian community members reported finding it for approximately $155 CAD.
  • Effectiveness with varying herb quality: One community member specifically noted that the DBV's moderate-to-large bowl capacity meant it remained "wholly effective" even with "mid-grade or even sub-standard herb."
  • Fun for groups: The community widely agreed that the DBV excelled as a "party/session vape" suitable for 2–3 users.

Weaknesses and criticisms:

  • Herb conservation: This was the most commonly cited drawback. Compared to log vapes (Purple Days, Underdog, E-Nano) and micro-dosing portables, the DBV consumed noticeably more material per session. One member who transitioned from a DBV to a Purple Days noted they were "eating through my stash wayyyy too fast" with the larger-bowl 7th Floor design. Another member cautioned that a buyer concerned with conservation "will use a lot more than with your MFLB."
  • Whip-style vapor path: The silicone whip, while functional, was noted as less "clean" than an all-glass air path. One community member specifically stated that "all-glass would be better than whip-style" for those prioritizing vapor path cleanliness.
  • Learning curve for newcomers from smoking: Some users transitioning directly from combustion found the DBV experience initially underwhelming. One member purchased a DBV and bong setup for over $300 but returned to smoking, finding the transition difficult — missing the "quickness" and "easiness" of combustion methods. The community widely acknowledged that converting from smoking to vaping required an adjustment period regardless of the device.
  • Not portable: At least one prospective buyer initially believed the DBV was portable; the community was quick to clarify that it is strictly a stationary desktop unit.
  • No digital temperature control: The analog dial, while simple, meant users could not set precise temperatures and had to learn their particular unit's sweet spots by feel.

Overall, the community regarded the Da Buddha as a reliable workhorse — not the most efficient or refined vaporizer available, but an excellent performer for its price that many members used happily for years.

Tips & Techniques

The community developed extensive technique advice for getting the best performance from the Da Buddha:

Loading

  • Grind quality matters: A medium-fine grind was generally recommended for optimal extraction. The herb should be evenly distributed in the wand without packing too tightly, which would restrict airflow.
  • Bowl size is adjustable: While the wand can hold a relatively large amount, the community emphasized that loading less herb was perfectly viable. One experienced member noted that "a quarter stem, which is I think around 0.2 grams, is more than enough for me in the DBV." Loading smaller amounts improved efficiency considerably.
  • Moist herb requires warm-up hits: Several community members observed that fresh or moist herb sometimes needed "a couple hits" to dry out before it began producing visible vapor. This was considered normal behavior, not a malfunction.

Drawing Technique

  • Breath speed controls effective temperature: A critical insight shared across the community was that draw speed directly influences the temperature of air hitting the herb. Slower draws allow the air to heat more thoroughly, producing thicker vapor. One member explained their approach: "My DBV stays on the same temperature and I adjust my breathing to increase temps."
  • Start slow: The recommended technique was to begin with a normal draw speed, then slow down if vapor production was thin. The community widely agreed that slow, steady draws yielded the best results.
  • Never blow through the whip: This was emphatically stressed by experienced users. Accidentally exhaling through the whip would blow herb particles back into the heater cover, creating a mess and potentially causing burning smells. One member advised: "Take a deep breath or two before bringing the whip anywhere near your face and never talk with it near your face either."

Temperature Dial Technique

  • Start low, step up gradually: The most widely recommended approach was to start with the dial set to approximately the 12 o'clock position (noon), then incrementally increase by small amounts (roughly half-hour increments on the clock face) if vapor production was insufficient. One experienced member laid out the process: "Set it low, around noon and hit it normal. If hits too thin, try hitting it slow... If you still don't get anything and the bowl doesn't look browned, turn it up to about 12:30. Give it a minute or so to make sure it's up to the new temp and then repeat."
  • Allow settling time: After adjusting the dial, the community recommended waiting at least a minute for the temperature to stabilize before evaluating results.

Session Management

  • Stirring between draws: Light stirring of the bowl contents between draws was recommended to expose fresh surfaces for more even extraction, though the DBV was noted to require less stirring than the SSV due to its more even air distribution.
  • Leave it on: Multiple members referenced 7th Floor's claim that they had a heating element running continuously in their factory for years without failure. While not universally recommended, many users left their DBV on for extended periods or throughout entire evenings of use.

Temperature Guide

Because the Da Buddha uses an analog dial without a digital temperature readout, temperature guidance within the community was expressed in terms of dial position (using clock-face notation) rather than specific degree values.

Dial PositionEffectDescription
~11:00–12:00Flavor / Low-tempMinimal visible vapor; excellent taste. Good for initial draws to dry herb and taste terpenes. Some community members, particularly those running European units at 220V, found that the very lowest settings (just before the "click" of extinction) were sufficient for full sessions.
~12:00–1:00Sweet SpotThe most widely recommended range. Balanced flavor and vapor production. The community generally agreed this was the optimal zone for most sessions. One French-language community member described their entire session at just above minimum, getting visible vapor after 2–4 initial taste-only draws.
~1:00–2:00Full extraction / CloudsThicker, more visible vapor with more complete extraction. Higher-temperature cannabinoids and terpenes released. Recommended for users seeking stronger sedative effects or treating insomnia.
~2:00+Maximum extractionRisk of approaching combustion temperatures. Used sparingly for finishing bowls or extracting from lower-quality herb. Community members cautioned against going too high.

Important notes:

  • Individual units can vary. The community noted that dial calibration was not perfectly consistent between units — one member's "noon" might not be another's.
  • Voltage differences matter: European users (220V mains) reported that their units operated at lower dial positions compared to North American units (120V). One French-speaking community member found that the optimal range was very close to the unit's minimum setting, and another confirmed this discrepancy existed.
  • Draw speed modulates effective temperature: As noted above, slowing your draw increases the effective temperature without touching the dial. Many experienced users kept the dial fixed and controlled their experience entirely through draw technique.
  • Step-up technique for full extraction: Some members started sessions at a lower dial setting and progressively increased it as the bowl was depleted, while others maintained a single temperature throughout. Both approaches were considered valid, with the step-up method extracting more from the material and the fixed-temperature method being simpler.

Maintenance & Cleaning

Whip and Wand Cleaning

  • Glass wand: Soak in isopropyl alcohol (90% or higher recommended) to dissolve resin buildup. Some members used coarse salt as an abrasive when shaking the wand in a bag or container with isopropyl alcohol.
  • Silicone whip/tubing: The tubing should be cleaned or replaced periodically. Warm water and isopropyl alcohol were the most commonly recommended cleaning agents. The tubing will accumulate residue over time, which some members noted could affect flavor.
  • Glass mouthpiece: Clean alongside the wand in isopropyl alcohol.
  • Reclaiming residue: The buildup inside the whip and wand ("reclaim") contains active compounds. Some community members collected this material for later use.

Heater Cover and Ceramic Element

  • The ceramic heating rod was generally considered low-maintenance but should be inspected periodically. One community member reported observing "little black lines" and "white flakes" on the ceramic rod, raising concerns about ceramic dust. A knowledgeable community moderator reviewed the available evidence and concluded that "any increase in risk with ceramic heaters was not enough for me to care about," noting that problematic ceramic dust exposure levels referenced in industrial safety literature were orders of magnitude higher than what a vaporizer user would encounter. However, the moderator acknowledged that "everyone has a different limit for risk."
  • If herb particles fall into the heater cover (usually from accidentally blowing through the whip), they should be carefully removed once the unit has cooled completely.

Screen Maintenance

  • Wand screens should be replaced when clogged or damaged. Keeping screens clean ensures proper airflow, which is critical for convection efficiency.

Common Issues & Fixes

Heating Element Not Working (Power Light On)

Symptom: The power indicator light on the back of the unit illuminates, but the ceramic heating element never gets hot.

Diagnosis: This indicates a likely failure of the heating element itself, as the power supply is functioning (evidenced by the indicator light).

Solution: Contact 7th Floor directly. The community reported that the company would either send a replacement heater element for self-installation (for technically inclined users) or arrange for the unit to be sent in for repair. One community member noted: "Call them up and they'll hook you up. Either they'll send you the heater for you to fix it if you are technically inclined or they'll have you send it in." This assumes the unit was purchased from 7th Floor directly or an authorized dealer; otherwise, the original point of purchase may need to be contacted.

Quick check first: Before assuming a hardware failure, verify that the power strip or surge protector isn't the culprit. One member reported that a tripped breaker on their power strip mimicked a dead unit — the power light on the strip still appeared functional while the vaporizer received no power.

Delayed Shipping / Poor Email Customer Service from 7th Floor

Symptom: Long delays between order confirmation and shipping notification; emails to customer service go unanswered.

Solution: The community widely agreed that calling 7th Floor by phone was far more effective than emailing. One member reported: "My experience with the emails are terrible with the company... if you want to talk to them, calling is your best bet." The company operates on Pacific Time. Despite email communication issues, the community generally confirmed that orders did ultimately arrive: "I don't think I read a problem with someone not getting their vape from them." International orders (particularly to Europe) were noted to take additional time.

Herb Falling into Heater Cover

Symptom: Material gets blown back into the heater cover, potentially creating smoke and unpleasant smells.

Cause: Almost always caused by accidentally blowing (exhaling) through the whip instead of inhaling. This can also happen when using the DBV with a water pipe/bong if the user doesn't pull the adapter to clear the chamber.

Solution: Prevention is key — always ensure you are inhaling, not exhaling, through the whip. Experienced users recommended taking preparatory breaths away from the whip before drawing. Allow the unit to cool completely, then carefully remove any debris from the heater cover.

Difficulty Transitioning from Smoking

Symptom: New users find vapor unsatisfying compared to smoke, feel they aren't getting adequate effects.

Solution: The community advised patience during the transition period. One veteran member shared their own experience of initially struggling with a whip vape and returning to smoking, only to successfully convert later. Suggestions included:

  • Take a short tolerance break before switching to help reset expectations
  • Start at moderate temperatures and work up
  • Use the DBV through a water pipe for a more familiar bong-like experience
  • Commit to vaping exclusively for at least a week to allow adjustment

Accessories & Modifications

Water Pipe Adapter (WPA) / Vape-Bong Setup

Running the DBV through a water pipe ("vape-bong") was one of the most popular modifications discussed in the community. This cools the vapor significantly and provides a more familiar experience for users transitioning from smoking.

  • Direct whip connection: The standard silicone tubing from the DBV/SSV fits directly into a 14.4mm ground glass joint on most bongs and bubblers without any adapter. One community member noted: "You really don't even need the adapter — the standard hose that works on the SSV/DBV will fit just fine in a 14.4mm GonG joint."
  • Dedicated WPA: 7th Floor and third-party manufacturers offered ground glass adapters for more secure connections to 18.8mm water pipes.
  • Clearing technique: When using a bong, the community recommended pulling the adapter/whip to clear the chamber rather than continuing to draw through the vaporizer, to avoid blowing herb back into the heater cover.

7th Floor Vapor Tamers

7th Floor produced a line of Vapor Tamer accessories — glass cooling devices filled with glycerin that could be frozen to cool vapor. Three models were discussed:

  • Original Vapor Tamer: The smallest model. Provided good cooling but restricted airflow somewhat when used with the DBV's standard heater cover and tubing setup. Didn't hold cold temperatures for long sessions.
  • Fatty Vapor Tamer: Larger than the Original with reportedly less airflow restriction.
  • Saturn Vapor Tamer: The largest model. Offered "no real noticeable restriction if any at all" when used with the DBV, according to one community member who tested all three models. Best at maintaining cold temperatures due to larger glycerin volume.

The community consensus on Vapor Tamers was that they provided effective cooling but had limited duration — the glycerin would warm up during use, requiring re-freezing between sessions. Some members preferred water filtration via bong/bubbler as a more consistent cooling solution.

Heater Cover and Wand Modifications

The community discussed various modifications including:

  • SSV standard heater cover: Some DBV owners swapped to SSV-compatible heater covers for different airflow characteristics.
  • VaporBrothers mini wand: At least one community member reported using a VB mini wand with the DBV setup (with SSV standard heater cover and tubing) for a different bowl size and draw experience.
  • Custom modifications: The main Da Buddha thread (spanning over 100 pages in the community forum) reportedly included members who fabricated custom stainless steel heater covers, custom wands, and other modifications.

Grinder Recommendations

While not specific to the DBV, the community generally recommended a quality grinder producing a medium-fine consistency for optimal convection vaporization.

Community consensus was that the SSV's angled heater cover was its primary functional advantage, particularly for concentrates, hash, and kief. The community noted that the SSV "will accommodate" hash and kief use while the DBV's horizontal orientation made this more challenging. Multiple members who owned both units felt the SSV was "totally worth the price premium" and offered "a more enjoyable vaping experience." One knowledgeable member noted that "the angle is more convenient, and the hands free setup is more versatile" on the SSV. However, others considered the differences minor enough that the DBV represented the better value: "They do both have that same 7th Floor Heater!"

The community generally considered the DBV to be the superior whip vape, delivering denser and more satisfying direct-draw hits. However, the Extreme Q's bag-filling capability made it more versatile for group settings. One knowledgeable member summarized: "A dual purpose vape like the EQ... won't deliver hits as heavy as the DBV or SSV." The community tended to recommend the DBV for users who didn't specifically need bags, and the Extreme Q for users who valued versatility and precise digital temperature control.

Da Buddha vs. Ditanium

In a direct comparison by a member who owned both units along with a VaporBrothers, the Da Buddha was ranked first in performance among the three whip-style desktops, with the Ditanium second and VaporBrothers third. The Ditanium was noted to be approximately twice the price in European markets. Both were described as enjoyable and used regularly, but the DBV's performance edge at a lower price point made it the value leader.

Da Buddha vs. VaporBrothers (VB1)

Both are classic whip-style desktops with analog temperature control. Members who owned both considered them "very awesome" and close in performance. The VaporBrothers was noted for being available in both hands-free and manual (non-hands-free) configurations, with some members preferring the manual VB for its simplicity and lower cost. The DBV was generally rated slightly higher in overall performance in direct comparisons.
